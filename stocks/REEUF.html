Analyst Summary

Post-redemption SPACs that have been delisted to the pinks are one of the cleanest asymmetric setups in the listed-securities universe — but only when you understand the mechanics. The investment is not a common stock — it is an option on either (a) a deal closing at trust value plus warrants/sponsor consideration, or (b) the trust being distributed in cash. Cartesian's $37.8M trust against a market cap of $38M and a quoted $10.30 print suggests the market is roughly pricing it at trust, with a small discount for the time value of holding through the August 5, 2026 deadline.

The edge here is operational, not fundamental. OTC delisting forces out funds that have listing-venue constraints, creating a structural seller base; the resulting illiquidity discount can be 1-3% versus pure trust value, which annualizes attractively over a four-month hold to the deadline. The redemption right itself is the key — as a public holder of record, you can submit shares for redemption ahead of any deal vote at the per-share trust value, which has been growing modestly as trust assets accrue interest. As long as the trust accounting remains clean and the sponsor doesn't attempt fee-extraction games (rare but not unheard of in distressed SPACs), the trust-value floor is real.

The risk that justifies any honest investor skepticism: late-stage SPACs occasionally announce "deals" that are effectively backdoor reverse mergers with low-quality private companies, often combined with PIPE structures that dilute non-redeeming holders. The defensive posture is to be a redeemer regardless of any announced transaction — vote your conscience, but always submit for redemption. With that discipline, REEUF is a ~3-5% IRR over four months at low risk, plus optionality on any non-trust upside.

What a Screen Can't See

A screen sees a $10.30 OTC tickers with no operating business and skips past it. What's invisible: (1) the trust value per share is approximately $10.50-10.60 depending on accrued interest, meaning the current price actually trades at a ~2% discount to redeemable cash; (2) OTC delisting creates a forced seller base of mandate-constrained funds; (3) the August 5, 2026 hard deadline is an effective stop-loss — beyond that, the sponsor cannot extend without another shareholder vote; and (4) the redemption right is what makes this a near-cash instrument, not the share price itself.
