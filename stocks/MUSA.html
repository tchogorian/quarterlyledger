Analyst Summary

Murphy USA operates ~1,750 fuel-and-convenience stores under the Murphy USA, Murphy Express, and QuickChek banners, concentrated in the Southeast and Midwest U.S. The model is structurally advantaged: small-format kiosk/convenience boxes co-located with Walmart Supercenters drive captive traffic at cost-of-occupancy that big-box C-store competitors (Sheetz, Wawa, Circle K) cannot replicate. Fuel volumes per store and merchandise contribution have both grown for a decade, while the company is mid-way through a multi-year remodel/raze-and-rebuild program that lifts merchandise sales per store materially. The financial signature is the giveaway: tax-efficient share repurchases that have shrunk the float from ~46M at spin to roughly half that today, with management explicitly framing every capital decision through the per-share lens.

The bear case is that MUSA is a fuel retailer, period — and fuel margins are cyclical. Retail fuel cents-per-gallon margins expanded structurally post-COVID as small operators exited and consumers became less price-sensitive at the pump; if those margins normalize lower, MUSA's earnings power resets. Tobacco, which is roughly a third of inside-store gross profit, is in secular decline (cigarette volumes down mid-single-digits annually), and EV adoption — while slower than projections — is a long-duration headwind for fuel volume. The Walmart co-location model, which built the company, has limited remaining new-build runway in the U.S.

The bull case is compounding: MUSA has bought back ~50% of shares since spin, dividend has grown every year, and the QuickChek acquisition gives the company a higher-margin food and beverage platform to roll out across the legacy footprint. Management's "raze-and-rebuild" program lifts per-store EBITDA meaningfully and the unit economics on new builds remain attractive. At 10–11x earnings on normalized fuel margins versus 18–22x for peer C-store operators (and 25x+ for Costco-like compounders), the gap is the entire thesis. A continued ~5–8% annual share-count reduction translates 0% topline growth into mid-teens EPS growth.

What a Screen Can't See

The 10-K segment disclosure shows that "Merchandise contribution dollars" — gross profit, not revenue — has grown for the better part of a decade even when fuel volumes were flat, because the mix has shifted toward higher-margin food, packaged beverage, and non-tobacco categories accelerated by the QuickChek acquisition. The per-store remodel disclosure (number of stores converted, capex per store, lift in merchandise sales) is the cleanest read on the compounding engine and is buried in the operations MD&A. Equally important: Murphy owns the underlying real estate at the majority of its sites, an asset not on the screen. The buyback cadence — disclosed quarterly in the 10-Q — should be read as the most reliable indicator of management's view on intrinsic value: when MUSA accelerates buybacks, fuel margins are typically being reinvested at a discount.

### CATALYSTS (3 dated) 1. **Q1 2026 earnings — late April / early May 2026** — buyback pace, fuel margin trajectory, raze-and-rebuild count. 2. **Investor Day / Annual Meeting — May 2026** — long-term guidance refresh and merchandise contribution targets. 3. **Q4 2026 holiday tobacco/merchandise data — February 2027** — checkpoint on inside-store mix shift versus tobacco decline.
