Analyst Summary

NewLake Capital Partners is a triple-net REIT that owns 34 single-tenant cannabis properties — 15 cultivation facilities and 19 dispensaries — leased to state-licensed cannabis operators on long-term, sale-leaseback structures. The 10-K reports 91% of rental income from cultivation facilities and properties held in core medical/limited-license states. The company is essentially the smaller, less-levered counterpart to IIPR with a concentrated tenant roster: Curaleaf, Cresco Labs, Trulieve, PharmaCann, Ascend, Verano, and several mid-cap MSOs comprise the rent roll. Screen prints P/B 0.76, P/E 11.4, MCap $300M. The dividend has been the marquee feature: a double-digit yield (~12-13%) supported by an AFFO payout ratio reported in the high 70s to low 80s percent range, on a near-zero-leverage balance sheet.

The bear case is the entire reason NLCP trades below book: federal cannabis prohibition, plus a tenant base concentrated in MSOs whose own equity prices have collapsed. The 10-K explicitly states "tenant concentration risk (and related risk of tenant defaults) may be more pronounced in the cannabis industry due to the fact that some of our tenants have limited operating histories" and that "cannabis remains illegal under federal law." The top three tenants (Curaleaf, Cresco Labs, Trulieve) reportedly represent ~50% of annualized rent — single-name credit events meaningfully impair coverage. Rescheduling to Schedule III has been repeatedly delayed; safe-harbor banking legislation has gone nowhere in Congress. Re-tenanting a vacant 100,000+ sq ft cannabis cultivation facility in a non-adult-use state is non-trivial, and at least three properties (Massachusetts, Pennsylvania, Nevada) appear vacant in the property table.

The bull case: triple-net REIT with negligible debt and ~13% yield, trading at 76% of book, where the worst-case tenant defaults have largely already occurred or been priced in (Revolutionary Clinics and AYR Wellness situations were managed without dividend cuts). The fortress balance sheet — net cash, no significant credit-facility draws, no near-term debt maturities — eliminates the financial-leverage risk that has wrecked many cannabis-adjacent companies. If federal reform happens at any point in the next 24 months (DEA rescheduling, SAFE Banking, or even softer 280E enforcement), MSO tenant credit improves materially, the discount-to-book compresses, and current shareholders capture a 30-50% re-rating on top of the 13% yield. Even with no reform, holding to collect 13% annually on book-discounted real estate with hard collateral is a defensible base case.

What a Screen Can't See

The 10-K's property tables — XBRL tags reveal entries like "Trulieve" in Pennsylvania, multiple "Curaleaf" properties in Florida and Illinois, "Vacant1Member" in Nevada and "Vacant2Member" in Pennsylvania — show a real, identifiable list of distressed re-tenanting situations. The unsecured loan to C3 Industries (originated June 10, 2022 per XBRL) is an off-the-radar credit exposure separate from rent — a working-capital-style loan to one of NLCP's tenants, exactly the kind of related-tenant lending that converts a passive landlord into a credit investor. Also worth noting: NLCP carries a CECL allowance for credit losses (adopted January 1, 2023) on its receivables — a non-trivial accounting choice for a REIT and a leading indicator of how management views tenant deterioration before headline cash rent collection numbers move.
