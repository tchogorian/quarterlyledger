Analyst Summary

Seaport Entertainment is the orphaned entertainment, hospitality and sports stub Howard Hughes carved out so HHH could be a pure master-planned-community real-estate company. The crown jewels: (1) the Seaport historic district in lower Manhattan — Pier 17, the Tin Building food hall (with chef Jean-Georges), and surrounding street-level retail; (2) the Las Vegas Aviators Triple-A baseball franchise plus the Las Vegas Ballpark; (3) air rights and undeveloped acreage at the former Tropicana site on the Las Vegas Strip (where MLB's Athletics are building their new stadium next door, with construction underway); (4) various minority interests including the Jean-Georges restaurant group stake. Q4 2025 revenue was $29.5M (+7% y/y pro-forma) with a $36.9M net loss. The post-spin balance sheet was scary; after the $143M sale of 250 Water Street to Tavros in February 2026, the company netted ~$76M and crucially repaid all variable-rate debt — no meaningful maturities until 2038.

Bear case: Seaport district has been a money-losing project for HHH for a decade — Pier 17 concerts and the Tin Building have never produced sustainable cash flow, and management has been "two years from profitability" since the original Howard Hughes carve-out from General Growth in 2010. The Las Vegas Aviators are a single Triple-A franchise — fun but hardly transformational economics. The Tropicana land sits unmonetized and entitled-but-undeveloped; an air-rights JV requires the right counterparty and capital. Operating losses run $60-100M annually with no clear inflection. NYSE-American listing means the stock will never get index-fund support, capping multiple expansion. Micro-cap means a single bad quarter triggers a stop-loss cascade. Ackman's stake is supportive but he's not actively pushing — Pershing Square has many fish to fry.

Bull case: a real sum-of-parts gets you $40-50/share, easily double the current price. Tropicana land alone (35 acres on the Strip, adjacent to the Athletics stadium under construction) is worth $300-500M on plausible per-acre Strip comparables — that's the entire current market cap. The Aviators + Ballpark is a $80-150M asset by recent MiLB transaction comps with stadium real estate. Seaport district at any reasonable cap rate is another $200-400M. Net debt is now manageable post-250-Water sale. The 2038 debt maturity wall buys management 14 years to monetize. Catalysts are concrete: a Tropicana JV or sale, accelerated Aviators monetization as Las Vegas Strip foot traffic compounds with the A's stadium opening, and the optionality of Ackman pushing for a sale or strategic transaction. NAV-discount closing alone is the trade.

What a Screen Can't See

Form 10 / 10-K specifics: (1) Howard Hughes seeded SEG with $25M cash plus ~$60M from a rights offering Pershing Square backstopped — the rights offering was the FORCED SELLING mechanism reverse-engineered (Pershing got more shares cheap, signaling commitment). (2) The Tropicana land is held with NO debt — the air-rights value is "free" on the balance sheet; cap-rate analysis gives no credit. (3) Transition Services Agreement with HHH is short (12 months) and largely unwound — no parent drag. (4) Board includes Anton Nikodemus (CEO, ex-MGM) and Pershing Square representation, signaling activist-friendly governance. (5) The 250 Water Street sale didn't just bring cash — it eliminated the largest variable-rate debt facility, removing the only near-term bankruptcy-risk pixel from the chart. (6) The Tin Building is a Jean-Georges JV — SEG's economics from it are a profit-share, not gross revenue, masking unit economics on screens.

### CATALYSTS (next 6-12 months) - Tropicana site JV or outright sale (most likely H2 2026 as A's stadium construction progresses) - Quarterly proof of operating-loss narrowing now that variable-rate debt is gone - Strategic transaction — Ackman-led take-private or asset sale to a casino operator / sports-entertainment buyer
