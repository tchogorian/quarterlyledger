Analyst Summary

Tri-County Financial Group is the holding company for First State Bank, an 85-year-old north-central Illinois community bank headquartered in Mendota with branches across Batavia, Bloomington, Champaign, Geneva, LaMoille, McNabb, North Aurora, Ottawa, Peru, Princeton, Rochelle, Shabbona, St. Charles, Streator, Sycamore, Waterman, and West Brooklyn — eighteen branches plus a residential-mortgage subsidiary (FSM) and an insurance subsidiary (First State Insurance). The screen flagged TYFG at P/B 0.80 and P/E 9.3 on a $126M market cap. The 2025 10-K shows ~$1.6B in total assets, return on average assets of 0.88% (vs. 0.68% in 2024), return on average equity of 9.09% (vs. 7.45%), net interest margin of 3.40% (vs. 2.96%), allowance for credit losses of 1.14% of loans, nonperforming loans of just 0.43% of loans, Tier 1 leverage capital ratio of 10.51%, total risk-based capital of 14.91%, and stockholders' equity to total assets of 9.89%. With 2,376,998 shares outstanding and a $43.50 reference price, this is a tiny, illiquid name.

The bear case is structural illiquidity and concentration. The shares trade on OTCQX, not a national exchange — the 10-K explicitly notes "OTCQX… provides limited liquidity. Consequently, you may not be able to sell your common stock at or above the price you paid." A 2.4M-share float means a single seller can move price 5-10% on a normal day. The loan book is concentrated in north-central Illinois agriculture, commercial real estate, and small-business lending — a regional recession or farm-belt commodity slump would hit credit quality fast. Loan-to-deposit at 100.87% leaves no slack for funding stress. As a community bank, TYFG cannot match the deposit pricing of larger competitors if rates spike again, and digital banks continue to skim younger Illinois depositors. The 17.4% dividend payout ratio is conservative, but bank regulators can suspend dividends at any time under capital-distribution rules referenced in the 10-K.

The bull case is straightforward small-bank value. Earning 9% on book at 0.80x book is mathematically a 11.3% earnings yield with capital ratios well above regulatory well-capitalized thresholds. NIM expanded 44bp year-over-year as legacy low-yield assets reprice; with another 12-18 months of asset reset, NIM could push toward 3.6-3.8%, lifting ROA toward 1%. Catalysts: continued rate-cycle benefit, possible NASDAQ uplisting that would unlock institutional ownership, and ultimate consolidation in Illinois community banking — a buyer would likely pay 1.2-1.5x book given ROE trajectory. Capital allocation discipline is evident in the conservative payout, modest balance sheet leverage, and 85-year operating history without a forced recapitalization.

What a Screen Can't See

Three filing-level details deserve weight. First, FSM (mortgage banking) gives TYFG fee income that is not captured in the headline NIM picture but is highly cyclical with mortgage-rate volatility — 2026 rate cuts would mean a refi tailwind. Second, the 10-K notes the bank has unused FHLB-Chicago borrowing capacity collateralized at varying advance rates against the loan book (76% of book value of 1-4 family real estate, 62% of HELOCs, etc.) — meaningful contingent liquidity not visible to a screen. Third, OTCQX listing is a self-imposed constraint, not a regulatory one; management has stayed off NASDAQ to avoid Sarbanes-Oxley-class compliance costs, but a future board could uplist. Tiny float means any institutional discovery is a re-rating catalyst. Insiders likely own meaningful percentage of shares — the typical Illinois community-bank holdco pattern.
