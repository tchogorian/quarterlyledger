Analyst Summary

Nexstar is the consolidator-of-last-resort in U.S. local broadcast television. CEO Perry Sook built the company through serial acquisitions (Tribune in 2019, Media General before that), and the model is brutally simple: own as many Big-4 affiliated stations as possible, harvest dual revenue streams from political/local advertising and retransmission consent fees from cable/satellite/vMVPD distributors, and return ~100% of free cash flow to shareholders via buybacks and dividends. The company generates billions in free cash flow in even-year (election + Olympics) cycles and meaningfully less in odd years. Even-year FCF is highly visible and recurring.

The bear case is the secular cord-cutting story: traditional pay-TV subscribers continue to decline 6-8% annually, which compresses the retransmission-fee base over time. The CW Network acquisition has been a cash drain. Political ad spend is lumpy and reform efforts (small-donor caps, digital substitution) could erode the local-broadcast political moat. Streaming bypass means networks like NBC and CBS can theoretically go direct-to-consumer and disintermediate the affiliate. The recently completed FCC ownership-cap rules at 39% national reach are technically a binding constraint, though Nexstar has used UHF discounts and structural workarounds. Leverage is meaningful (~3x net debt/EBITDA on trailing eight-quarter average), and refinancing risk exists if rates stay elevated.

The bull case is that Nexstar is the cheapest distressed-mid-teens-FCF-yield asset in media-land, with an actively pro-deregulation FCC under the current administration likely to lift or modify the 39% cap — which would unlock immediate accretive M&A (Tegna, Sinclair, Scripps stations all in play). Sook has compounded book value/share at mid-teens for 20 years. The 2024 election cycle generated record political ad revenue, and 2026 midterms set up another. The 31% stake in Food Network (TV Food) is unconsolidated and worth several billion in a separation. Buybacks at current FCF yields are accretive double-digits.

What a Screen Can't See

The Food Network stake (TV Food, LLC) is carried at a tiny GAAP basis on the balance sheet but sources estimate fair value at $1.5-2.5B based on Warner Bros. Discovery's implied multiple — that's $50-80/share of hidden NAV that does not show up in screen-level multiples. Also invisible: Nexstar's retransmission contracts are staggered with multi-year renewal cycles; the company has historically extracted 8-15% step-ups at each renewal because its station portfolio is must-carry for any vMVPD launching nationally (YouTube TV, Hulu Live, Fubo all need local Big-4 affiliates). The political-revenue cyclicality means screens computing TTM FCF in Q1 of an odd year systematically understate normalized cash generation by 25-40%.

### CATALYSTS (3 dated) 1. **2026 midterms (Nov 2026)** — Political ad cycle peak; expected $500M+ incremental revenue. 2. **2026 FCC ownership-cap review** — Potential cap lift unlocks Tegna/Sinclair-style M&A. 3. **Food Network strategic review** — WBD has signaled openness to monetize Food/HGTV; Nexstar's 31% stake is a contingent catalyst.
