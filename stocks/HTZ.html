Analyst Summary

Hertz is the prior-cycle archetype of a post-BK reorg gone wrong, then quietly being reset. The June 2021 emergence handed equity to a creditor consortium (Knighthead Capital, Certares, Apollo, plus Pershing Square as a public-market participant) at what looked like an excellent recovery basis. Subsequent secondary offerings rotated nearly all of that creditor stock into public hands at $25–35, leaving today's float dominated by retail and generalist holders rather than original reorg owners. The capital structure remains heavy: ABS fleet financing, corporate notes, and a thin equity sleeve against a depreciating fleet of ~500K vehicles. Market cap around $2.3B at $7.43.

The bear case is straightforward: rental-car economics are punishing when used-car residuals roll over, and Hertz is still digesting the Tesla/Polestar EV mistake (write-downs, depreciation acceleration, and reputational damage with corporate accounts). Interest expense on ABS facilities reprices higher; competition from Avis and Enterprise is rational on pricing but the industry has structurally lower per-unit profitability than the 2021–22 super-cycle implied. New CEO Gil West (ex-Delta/Cruise) inherits a balance sheet with limited margin for error, and any soft summer travel season would re-test liquidity covenants. The "broken IPO" stigma keeps generalists away.

The bull case rests on three legs: (1) fleet refresh away from EVs back to ICE largely complete by Q1 2026, restoring depreciation predictability; (2) Pershing Square's January 2024 disclosure of a 19.8M share stake (~4.1%) signals deep-value alignment with Knighthead operationally; (3) optionality on a take-private at trough multiples by Knighthead/Certares once cash flows normalize. Used-car residuals stabilizing in late 2025 would flip the depreciation tailwind. At ~$7, the stock prices in continued distress; any quarter of clean per-unit margins re-rates the equity meaningfully.

What a Screen Can't See

The ABS facility covenants and the residual-value adjustments inside fleet depreciation are the actual scorecard, not GAAP EPS. Inside the 10-Q, the "monthly depreciation per unit" disclosure tells you whether management's residual assumptions are tracking Manheim. The Polestar settlement and the Tesla resale-loss tail are also disclosed only in narrative footnotes. Ackman's 13D mechanics matter too: Pershing's stake came via swaps, meaning vote-influence is more limited than a clean common position. Finally, Hertz's net interest expense run-rate is the swing variable for FCF — a screen showing trailing EBITDA misses how much of that EBITDA gets eaten by ABS interest at 6%+.
