Analyst Summary

Highland Opportunities & Income Fund trades at a 43-55% discount to its reported net asset value of $11.38 per share, with the stock at approximately $5-6. This is the widest CEF discount in our entire screen. Saba Capital — the most prolific and successful closed-end fund activist in the United States — has filed a Schedule 13D disclosing a 5.05% stake and explicitly stated they may pursue "discount-to-NAV, governance, capitalization, open- versus closed-end structure, or liquidation timing" changes. The Saba playbook is well-documented and effective: buy CEFs at deep discounts, push for board changes through proxy fights, force tender offers at or near NAV, or push for conversion to open-end structure (which eliminates the discount entirely). Saba has executed this playbook successfully at dozens of funds including BlackRock trusts, Pioneer funds, and others. The settlement record shows tender offers at 98-99.5% of NAV are the typical outcome. The risk is real: the discount exists because the fund holds some illiquid assets, and the NAV may be overstated if those assets are marked above true realizable value. NexPoint Asset Management runs the fund, and management has historically been resistant to shareholder-friendly actions. But with Saba on the register at 5%+ and actively filing proxy materials, the probability of some form of discount-closing action within 12-18 months is meaningful.

What a Screen Can't See

The Saba 13D filing language is explicit about intentions. This is not passive investment — it is an activist campaign with a proven playbook. Saba's track record shows a ~70%+ success rate at CEF campaigns. The monthly distribution of $0.0385/share provides income while waiting. Even if the discount only narrows from 50% to 25% (not full closure), that represents a 50% return on the current price.
