Analyst Summary

WBD operates three segments: Studios (Warner Bros. film/TV production), Networks (HBO/Max streaming, plus declining linear cable: TNT, TBS, CNN, Discovery), and DTC (Max streaming standalone). The proposed Netflix transaction involves Netflix acquiring select WBD streaming/studio assets, leaving a stub of cable networks. At $27.47 with ~2.5B shares the equity is ~$69B; net debt of ~$35B brings EV to ~$100B+. The stock currently reflects the Netflix bid value plus minor optionality. Comps: Disney trades at higher multiples on Studios + parks; Paramount-Skydance pro forma is the closest analog; standalone cable/network multiples have collapsed to 3-4x EBITDA.

**Bear case:** The Netflix deal is what shareholders are going to get — Paramount's balance sheet post-Skydance merger is stretched, and Larry Ellison's appetite for sweetening has limits. The cable network stub, if the deal closes, is a melting ice cube: linear ad revenue declining double-digits annually, sports rights inflation crushing margins, no obvious buyer for cable assets at any price. Zaslav's track record on capital allocation (Discovery-AT&T merger, $50B in net debt acquired) is poor; betting on board accountability when Zaslav controls the proxy machine is a lottery ticket. Ancora's $200M stake is too small to drive board change at a $69B mega-cap; their wins (Norfolk Southern, U.S. Steel) involved larger stakes and operating-vulnerability narratives that don't apply here.

**Bull case:** Merger-arb dynamics favor the patient holder. Shareholder vote in April 2026 is a real binary — if Netflix deal fails (or Paramount escalates), stock pops to $32-35 immediately. If Netflix deal closes, the cable stub trades to $5-8 plus you own pro forma Netflix shares with synergy upside. Ellison/Skydance has demonstrated willingness to overpay for media (Paramount itself); a $35-40 counter-bid is not crazy, and a third party (Comcast, Apple, even Saudi PIF) could enter as a white knight. Path to re-rate: (1) Paramount counter-bid escalates pre-vote, (2) shareholders reject Netflix deal forcing auction, (3) post-deal stub trades cheap enough to be acquired by PE.

What a Screen Can't See

The 10-K discloses ~$2B in annual restructuring/integration charges that have never normalized — suggesting either continuous reorganization or aggressive add-back accounting; either way, true free cash flow is overstated. Second filing-level alpha: the Netflix deal documents (when fully proxied) will reveal management's tax-deferral structuring — historically Zaslav's deals (Discovery-Scripps, AT&T spin) used Reverse Morris Trusts, and any complexity in the Netflix structure suggests room for Paramount to offer a cleaner alternative. Third: HBO's content library is on the balance sheet at amortized cost, materially below market value — the Harry Potter, DC, Friends, Sopranos catalogs alone are worth $20B+ in a content-scarce streaming world. Fourth: Ancora's previous campaigns (Norfolk Southern, U.S. Steel) succeeded because they identified governance misalignment; their public letters here will likely surface director conflicts that force at least board engagement.
