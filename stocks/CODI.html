Analyst Summary

CODI owns a portfolio of branded-consumer and industrial businesses with collective adjusted EBITDA of roughly $430M+. Branded-consumer holdings include BOA Technology (the Boa lacing system used in athletic and outdoor footwear), 5.11 Tactical (tactical apparel and gear), Marucci Sports (premium baseball bats), Velocity Outdoor (just sold), Ergobaby (baby carriers), PrimaLoft (synthetic insulation), and Lugano Diamonds (under restatement). Industrials include Altor Solutions (cold-chain packaging), Sterno, Arnold Magnetics, and Honey Pot Co. The model is to buy private mid-market businesses in the $200M-$2B range, hold them, and pay a quarterly distribution to shareholders.

The bear case is the overhang. Lugano was the highest-growth, highest-margin subsidiary by reported numbers — and those numbers turned out to be inflated. SEC investigation is ongoing and could result in fines, restated financials going further back, or executive accountability actions. The distribution was suspended/cut, removing the income-buyer base. HoldCo-level debt sits around $1.8B, and the conversion from a partnership (K-1) to a C-corp added tax friction. New leadership is unproven. Even excluding Lugano, the remaining EBITDA contribution depends on consumer health for several of the largest pieces (5.11, Marucci, Ergobaby) — all sensitive to discretionary spend.

The bull case is the math. Strip Lugano entirely from the model, assume ~$330M of clean EBITDA from the rest, apply 8x — a defensible multiple for a basket of branded niche leaders — and you get ~$2.6B EV. Net of HoldCo debt, equity value clears the current $781M cap with room to spare. BOA, 5.11, and Marucci alone arguably justify the entire market cap. The Velocity divestiture (announced March 2026) demonstrates active portfolio rationalization, generates cash to delever, and proves the SOTP exercise works in real life. New CEO + refreshed board have signaled intent to crystallize value through additional sales. Insider buying has appeared at recent prices.

What a Screen Can't See

A screen can't see segment-level economics that aren't disclosed. CODI reports adjusted EBITDA by segment in the 10-Q, but valuable detail — like BOA's licensing-vs-product mix, 5.11's government-channel revenue, or Marucci's gross margin progression — sits in MD&A footnotes and earnings call transcripts. The Velocity sale price (when disclosed) becomes a real-money read on what mid-market private buyers will pay for CODI's portfolio companies, calibrating the SOTP. Also: HoldCo debt covenants and any Lugano-related representation/warranty exposure — buried in credit-agreement amendments — could constrain capital allocation if the restatement worsens.
