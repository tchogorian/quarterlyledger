Analyst Summary

Sandisk is the only US-listed pure-play NAND flash memory company post-separation. The business: NAND wafer production via the long-running joint venture with Kioxia (Japan) — a structural cost-share arrangement that lets both partners stay at the leading edge with half the capex burden — packaged into consumer SSDs (the SanDisk and WD brands), enterprise/data-center SSDs, and embedded/automotive solutions. Q3 FY26 revenue guide is $4.4-$4.8B with gross margin guidance of 65-67% — versus 26% in the prior-year quarter and a 7.1% trough in FY23. The cycle has flipped: contract NAND prices were up 85-90% q/q, Goldman models +100-120% NAND pricing in 2026, and the demand-supply gap (22% vs 16-17%) implies persistent shortage through 2028 per multiple sell-side desks. Free cash flow generation in H2 2025 was $1.2B, used to pay down separation-inherited debt. The HBF (High Bandwidth Flash) co-development with SK Hynix targets 8-16x HBM capacity at comparable bandwidth — if it ships H2 2026 it directly competes for AI-accelerator memory sockets.

Bear case: NAND is the most cyclical commodity in semiconductors. The peak-to-trough on gross margins has historically been 50+ points. At 65%+ GM you are at-or-above prior cycle peaks; mean reversion alone is brutal. Capex discipline lasts only as long as everyone is profitable — Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron and Kioxia all add capacity into shortages, eventually flooding the market. The $1.165B installment payment to Kioxia (2026-2029) is a real cash outflow tied to JV restructuring. HBF is a technology bet against entrenched HBM (Samsung/SK Hynix DRAM stacking) — first-mover doesn't always win, and AI accelerators are tightly co-designed with specific memory architectures. The stock has 30x'd in 14 months — every "AI-adjacent" momentum holder is in, and any cycle wobble (China recession, Apple iPhone air pocket, hyperscaler capex pause) triggers a violent reversal. NAND prices have historically over- and under-shot by 50%+ — picking the cycle top here is a coin flip.

Bull case: even at $920, forward EV/EBITDA on FY26-FY27 numbers under sell-side cycle-peak assumptions is below 6x — meaning the market still implicitly assumes mean reversion within 18 months. If the structural AI shortage extends to 2028 (consensus among NAND-dedicated desks), three more years of peak earnings prints, with FCF used to delever and buy back stock. HBF is the genuine wild card — if it captures even 10% of what becomes a $50B+ AI memory market by 2028, that's a $5B/year revenue lever at gross margins north of 60%. S&P 500 inclusion is pending (already meets size and float thresholds) — a mechanical demand catalyst. Sandisk brand has consumer pricing power that pure-merchant NAND players (Micron NAND, SK Hynix Solidigm) lack. The Kioxia JV is the deepest moat — fab capacity that takes 3-5 years and $30B+ to replicate.

What a Screen Can't See

Form 10 / S-1A / TSA specifics: (1) The Kioxia JV is the entire cost-of-goods structure — Sandisk doesn't own fabs outright, it shares them. Screens looking at "fab utilization" miss this. (2) The $1.165B installment payment to Kioxia (2026-2029) shows up as committed contractual cash obligations, not opex — it's an artifact of the separation/JV renewal. (3) Transition Services Agreement with WDC runs up to 18 months covering quality assurance, IT, procurement, logistics, finance, HR, engineering, marketing, operations, sales, manufacturing, R&D — broad scope means standalone-cost emergence is just unwinding now (2026). (4) WDC's debt-for-equity exchange plan with ~28.8M SNDK shares creates discrete overhang events — these are predictable and discountable. (5) Board includes legacy WDC and JV-relationship members; CEO David Goeckeler is a known semis operator. (6) HBF JDA with SK Hynix is structured as a joint development with ip-sharing on the architecture — economics are partner-friendly but not exclusive.
