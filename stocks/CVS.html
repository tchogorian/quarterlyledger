Analyst Summary

CVS is a vertically integrated healthcare conglomerate: roughly 9,000 retail pharmacies, Caremark (the largest U.S. PBM), Aetna (a top-three managed-care plan), and Oak Street Health (value-based primary care). Revenues approach $400B and the company sits at the intersection of retail, insurance, and healthcare services. Screen catches it because Glenview Capital's Larry Robbins took an active stake disclosed Oct 14, 2024 followed by a Nov 2024 cooperation agreement that added 4 directors. Position is up roughly 25% YTD per the activist update; market cap is $99B; Glenview trimmed slightly Q1 2025 but remains active.

Bear case from the 10-K: Aetna's medical loss ratio surged in 2024 as the company materially under-priced 2024 individual exchange and Medicare Advantage benefits, requiring multiple guidance cuts. Star Ratings downgrades hit MA bonus revenue. The PBM model faces ongoing FTC scrutiny and legislative threats, with multiple states moving on reimbursement-floor laws. Retail pharmacy gross margins are structurally compressed by reimbursement pressure and front-store traffic decline. The integrated-model thesis — that owning all four pieces drives synergies — has yet to show in margins, and a sum-of-parts unwind would face enormous transaction tax leakage.

Bull case: the 2024 Aetna miss appears transitory — repricing of 2025 and 2026 plans plus exits from unprofitable counties should normalize MLR back toward target by H2 2026. With Robbins on the board, capital allocation discipline (dividend, buyback, leverage) and operational accountability are formalized. CEO David Joyner has held the role through this cycle, and the activist relationship is constructive rather than hostile. At $77 versus credible $100+ sum-of-parts (Aetna ~$40, Caremark ~$30, Pharmacy ~$25, Oak Street ~$10), there is meaningful upside even without a break-up — operational normalization alone could re-rate the stock to $90+. Free cash flow remains substantial and supports buybacks.

What a Screen Can't See

The Nov 18, 2024 cooperation agreement added Robbins, Larry Norwalk, Doug Shulman, and Guy Sansone — a slate that includes deep healthcare services, IRS / regulatory, and turnaround expertise specifically calibrated to CVS's three biggest 2024 problems (Aetna pricing, PBM regulation, Oak Street integration). The 10-K's MA segment disclosures show 2024 underwriting losses concentrated in specific plan years that are now being repriced — the 2026 bid filings (already submitted to CMS) are the leading indicator that Wall Street has not yet fully modeled. The hidden lever is Caremark's specialty pharmacy growth, which is partially offsetting front-store declines but is buried in segment reporting.
