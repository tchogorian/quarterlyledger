### THE SETUP

The underfollowed micro-cap discount is the asymmetry — institutional analysts haven't picked it up post-IPO.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
OMS Energy Technologies designs and manufactures premium connector systems and surface wellhead equipment for the upstream oil and gas industry. The company operates manufacturing in Singapore, Indonesia, and Saudi Arabia, and is qualified under Saudi Aramco's IKTVA (In-Kingdom Total Value Add) localization program — a structural moat that only a few non-Saudi suppliers have earned. The screen captures it via $119M net cash on a $190M cap (63% cash), a TTM P/E of 6.5, and $29M of TTM net income. H1 FY2026 (six months to September 30, 2025) revenue was $82.8M with $14.6M net profit. The H1 number declined vs. unusually strong prior-year comp driven by exceptional Saudi call-off volumes in H1 FY25.

The bear case starts and ends with customer concentration: Saudi Aramco accounted for 67% of FY25 revenue, and the long-term supply agreement allows termination on 30 days' notice. That is an extreme single-customer dependency in a region where geopolitical risk is non-trivial. Secondary risks: oil & gas capex cyclicality, lumpy call-off orders that complicate revenue forecasting (already visible in the H1 normalization), recent-IPO insider lockup expirations creating supply, and the small-float / illiquid trading that makes both entry and exit harder. As a foreign private issuer (20-F filer), disclosure cadence is also lighter than US issuers — only semi-annual financials.

The bull case: cash is nearly two-thirds of market cap and growing, the company is profitable, the Saudi Aramco relationship is structurally hard to replicate (IKTVA qualification took years), and a March 2026 $11M call-off order signals continued spending under the umbrella agreement. With $128.7M cash plus $61M of trailing operating leverage, the entire enterprise value is roughly $61M for a business throwing off $25–30M of net income. That is a sub-3x EV/earnings on a profitable, IKTVA-qualified specialty oilfield supplier with multi-year contractual visibility. Even with a haircut for customer concentration, this is a structurally cheap micro-cap.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Three filing-level points the screen misses. First, the IKTVA qualification (and Indonesia's TKDN equivalent) is a regulatory moat — Aramco's localization program rewards qualified suppliers with preferred bidding rights, and de-qualification would require a multi-year unwind. Second, the H1 FY26 net cash growth came partly from $28.9M of IPO proceeds — strip those and operating cash generation is still positive but the runway extension is structural, not one-off. Third, OMS reports under IFRS as a foreign private issuer; "net cash" and "EBITDA" are calculated differently than US GAAP screens assume. Backlog disclosure is qualitative ("sizeable") rather than quantified — a notable gap.
