### THE SETUP
A $1.5B Liberty Malone-orphan holding GCI — Alaska's near-monopoly broadband and wireless operator (~90% market share) — spun out of Liberty Broadband immediately ahead of the Charter/Cox merger. LBRDA holders only wanted Charter exposure and dumped the GCI stub on day one; the Series A/B/C share structure suppressed price discovery; and a $4.7B 45-year network investment in 200+ Alaska communities sits at materially lower EBITDA multiples than Frontier, Windstream, or WOW.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
GCI Liberty's only material asset is GCI, LLC — Alaska's dominant facilities-based connectivity company providing data, mobile, voice and managed services to consumer, business, government and carrier customers across more than 200 communities, on the back of $4.7B of cumulative network investment over 45 years. 2025 was a record year: full-year adjusted OIBDA hit ~$403M; Q2 revenue grew 6% to $261M with OIBDA up 26% to $108M; Q3 revenue was $257M (-2%) with OIBDA $92M (-8%) reflecting rural healthcare-program timing. Year-end consolidated cash was $137M, total principal debt ~$1B, GCI credit-agreement leverage 2.3x, and post-rights-offering net leverage just 1.6x — a notably under-levered balance sheet for a Liberty entity.

Bear case: it's still Alaska. The state has structural headwinds — slow population growth, oil-price dependence, federal subsidy exposure (rural healthcare and BEAD funding). Consumer cable modem subs declined 3% YoY to 153,100 in Q3 — wireline is shrinking even in a near-monopoly. Wireless competition from AT&T and Verizon is real even if subscale. The Liberty Malone tracker-stock complexity (A/B/C series) won't fully unwind for years and limits institutional ownership. And a "Liberty spin" reputation cuts both ways: patient holders win, but the corporate structure is built to optimize taxes for the Malone family first, minority second.

Bull/catalyst: GCI is functionally a regulated utility for Alaska connectivity — geography is the moat (no rational competitor will replicate $4.7B of remote network) and the state government is a captive customer. Wireless lines grew 2% to 207,500 in Q3, offsetting cable decline, and total OIBDA hit a record $403M. At ~$1.5B equity + $1B debt = ~$2.5B EV vs $403M OIBDA, GLIBA trades at ~6.2x — Frontier, Lumen, WOW trade 7-9x even after their own selloffs and have inferior moats. Liberty management has a multi-decade track record of rewarding patient holders via tax-free splits, buybacks, and opportunistic deals; a PE take-private or strategic combination at peer multiples is a realistic exit. The Charter/Cox merger close (expected 2026) removes the residual structural complexity overhang.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The S-1 / S-1/A from June and November 2025 reveal that the standalone entity was deliberately recapitalized via a PIPE/rights offering specifically to drop net leverage to 1.6x — well below Liberty's typical 4-5x. That signals the Malone team is positioning GLIBA as a clean buyout vehicle, not a long-hold telecom utility. Filings also disclose that GCI is the dominant carrier for Alaska's rural healthcare connectivity program (a federal Universal Service Fund mechanism) — quasi-guaranteed revenue not visible in any peer screen. The 200+ community footprint includes proprietary subsea fiber (TERRA) that no competitor can replicate; replacement cost easily exceeds the current EV.
