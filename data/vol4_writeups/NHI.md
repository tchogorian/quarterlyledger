The Brody-family relationship to NHC (founder lineage) is exactly the related-party governance flag activists love: Litt argues better capital allocation and an arms-length relationship would close the AFFO multiple gap. Trades roughly 13x AFFO and ~7-8% implied cap rate versus pure-play senior housing names like Welltower and Ventas at 16-18x AFFO and sub-6% caps — a 25-35% discount to NAV on conservative marks. Dividend coverage looks fine post-2024 reset.

Bear case: NHI's tenant concentration and exposure to skilled nursing (lower multiple, reimbursement risk) structurally limit re-rating versus pure senior-housing peers. NHC is ~16% of revenue and the related-party optics will not unwind easily. The dividend was previously cut, which damaged the income franchise. If Litt loses the proxy vote (1.5% stake against family-aligned holders is uphill), the optionality evaporates and you own a slow-growth, mid-cap healthcare REIT in a higher-for-longer rate environment. Senior housing labor inflation could re-accelerate.

Bull case: Three independent levers. (1) Senior housing fundamentals — occupancy is still recovering 200-300 bps off the COVID trough with limited new supply through 2026 and rent growth durable. (2) Rate path — every 25 bps off the long end is meaningful for AFFO multiples and implied cap rates. (3) Activist-driven governance refresh — even a settlement (one board seat, refreshed comp/related-party committee) catalyzes a re-rate. Re-rate to even 15x AFFO equals roughly $100-105 share price plus a covered ~5%+ dividend — total return optionality of 25-35% over 18 months with downside cushioned by the yield.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The NHC related-party arrangement is the buried alpha. NHI was spun out of NHC in 1991 and the same Brody-family lineage chairs both companies — Litt's filings highlight this as the source of capital allocation drift (sub-market lease economics, slow disposition of underperforming SNFs, balance-sheet conservatism that suppresses ROE). A screen sees a healthcare REIT with average metrics; the filing-level alpha is that an independent board majority would almost certainly (a) renegotiate or recut the NHC master lease at market terms, (b) accelerate non-core SNF dispositions, and (c) redeploy into higher-growth senior housing operating partnerships (RIDEA structures). None of that is in consensus AFFO.
