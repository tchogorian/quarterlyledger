### THE SETUP
First-ever publicly traded "land-banking REIT" — Lennar dropped $5.5B of land plus $1B of cash into Millrose, distributed it to shareholders, and now MRP earns contracted option-fee yield as Lennar (and increasingly other builders) takes down homesites on a just-in-time basis. Market is mis-pricing this as a homebuilder when it's structurally a finance company with a secured-credit-style book. ~14 months post-spin, paying $0.76/quarter ($3.04 annualized) — a ~10% yield at the current price. BTIG initiated Buy at $35.

### SPINOFF MECHANICS
- Parent: Lennar Corporation (NYSE: LEN)
- Spin date: February 7, 2025 — ~14 months old
- Distribution ratio: pro-rata to LEN A and LEN B holders; Lennar retained ~20% (33.2M Class A shares, non-voting initially) for subsequent disposition
- Initial trading vs current: opened ~$25-26 with heavy first-week selling pressure; current ~$31.03 — already re-rated, but still cheap on yield
- Forced selling reasons: (1) LEN holders are homebuilder-equity investors with zero appetite for an externally-managed REIT; (2) novel business model (no direct comp) made it un-modelable for LEN sell-side analysts who simply marked it for distribution and moved on; (3) Kennedy Lewis external-management structure carries fee-drag stigma that REIT-dedicated investors penalize; (4) early share distribution was un-indexed, so on Day 1 every passive vehicle holding LEN had to sell SEG mechanically; (5) Lennar's residual 20% creates a known supply overhang (subsequent spin/split-off/sale planned).

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Millrose is the first publicly traded land-banking REIT. The structure: Lennar contributed ~$5.5B of homesite land plus $1B cash and ~142,000 controlled homesites. MRP holds the land; Lennar (and now other homebuilders, per the December 2025 exchange and onboarding announcements) pays MRP a monthly option fee for the right to take down lots on a defined cadence. The economics resemble secured term lending: Lennar is contractually committed to the takedown schedule, and the underlying land collateral is high quality. 2025 results: $600.5M total revenue, $2.44 EPS, $2.58 AFFO/share, $5.5B deployed in new land, $3.4B takedown proceeds, 31,000+ homesite closings. Q4 dividend of $0.75 ($124.5M total) and Q1 2026 dividend of $0.76 ($126.2M) — a clear sign of a 10-12% yield REIT that the market hasn't priced. Externally managed by Kennedy Lewis Land and Residential Advisors at a 1.25% per annum fee on Tangible Assets. 2026 base case: grow invested capital outside the Lennar Master Program Agreement by $2B, reaching ~$10.5B total with >40% non-Lennar.

Bear case: external management is a permanent ~125 bps drag and creates conflict-of-interest perception — every dollar of asset growth pays Kennedy Lewis. The novel structure invites short-seller attention asking "why isn't this just a homebuilder finance subsidiary?" Homebuilder cycle risk is fully embedded — if Lennar (or onboarded peer builders) slows takedowns in a recession, MRP's revenue cliffs even though contracts are "binding" (in practice, builders renegotiate). Lennar's residual 20% is overhang until cleared. Dividend is large but funded by recycling principal as much as operating earnings — any stress on takedown cadence and the dividend gets cut, triggering yield-investor exodus. REIT structure means raising equity to grow — secondary offerings are likely and a price ceiling. No track record as a standalone — every quarter is the first of its kind to model. Direct-comp absence cuts both ways: it can stay cheap because nobody knows what to mark it against.

Bull case: a 10-12% ROE recurring-fee compounder with a contracted, high-visibility revenue stream and an aligned anchor customer (Lennar 20% + multi-year MPA). At ~$31 the stock yields ~9.8% in cash distributions while growing the asset base. Onboarding non-Lennar builders is the surprise upside — every new builder is incremental capital deployment at attractive spreads with no incremental fixed cost. December 2025 Lennar exchange offer cleared part of the overhang; remaining stake is a known, manageable supply. As a 2026 first-full-year reporter, MRP gets discovered by REIT-dedicated investors who couldn't model it last year. BTIG's $35 PT is a starting point; if the market re-rates it as a 7-8% yield REIT, the stock prints $40+. Index inclusion (Russell, eventually MSCI REIT indices) is a 2026-2027 catalyst.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

Form S-11 / 10-K specifics: (1) Kennedy Lewis fee is 1.25% of Tangible Assets — at $10B+ assets, that's $125M+ annually, paid externally. The conflict: Kennedy Lewis is incentivized to grow assets, not necessarily ROE. (2) Lennar retains consent rights on the Manager until its asset contribution drops below 10% of total assets for six consecutive months — meaning Lennar effectively governs the manager relationship for years. (3) Master Program Agreement with Lennar specifies takedown windows, option fees, and pricing mechanics — this is the load-bearing contract; a Material Adverse Change clause exists but is narrow. (4) Lennar's 33.2M Class A residual shares are non-voting initially with a planned subsequent transaction (split-off, secondary, or sale) — a known catalyst on a clock. (5) Most of the dividend is technically a return of capital that recycles as Lennar takes down lots — distinguishing "true" yield from principal-return is critical and screens conflate them. (6) Tax-status as REIT requires 90% distribution of taxable income — the $0.76 dividend is structural, not discretionary.
