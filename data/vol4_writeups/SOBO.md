### THE SETUP
South Bow is the Keystone liquids pipeline system spun out of TC Energy (TRP) — a ~94%-contracted, take-or-pay heavy-crude system that for years was buried inside a Canadian gas-utility conglomerate. TRP holders bought TRP for natural-gas utility yield and reflexively dumped SOBO on receipt; meanwhile SOBO is too Canadian for US MLP indices and not in core Canadian dividend ETFs, creating dual orphan status. Result: ~7% yield on contracted infrastructure trading at a discount to US C-corp pipeline peers.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
South Bow operates the Keystone Pipeline System — the 4,900km crude oil pipeline running from Hardisty, Alberta to Cushing, Oklahoma and the US Gulf Coast — plus the Marketlink system (Cushing to Houston/Port Arthur) and the Houston terminal complex. Importantly, the spin assets exclude Keystone XL (the cancelled segment whose 2021 termination drove a multi-billion impairment at TRP). What's left is a fully built-out, in-service pipeline with long-term Transportation Service Agreements (TSAs) and Minimum Volume Commitments (MVCs) covering the vast majority of throughput. Management has guided to ~94% contracted revenue with weighted-average contract life north of seven years, plus a substantial book of contracts with 30-year tails on certain MVCs. Initial annual EBITDA guidance was approximately $1.0B USD, with maintenance capex modest and growth capex disciplined.

The bear case: Keystone is a single asset with concentrated reputational risk — the December 2022 Mill Creek spill dumped 14,000 barrels in Kansas and remains in litigation/regulatory remediation. Heavy crude differentials and Canadian production growth (TMX expansion now in service) could divert volumes. SOBO inherited substantial debt from TRP — net debt around $4.8B against ~$1B EBITDA, a 4.5-5x leverage ratio that's normal for midstream but limits dividend growth. The 2025 Cushing-area capacity story is mature; throughput growth from here depends on Western Canadian heavy oil supply, which faces Trans Mountain competition.

The bull case: contracted cash flows are utility-like, and the dividend (initially USD $2.00 annualized, ~6-7% yield) is well-covered with a low-60s payout ratio on DCF. Trans Mountain reaching capacity actually reinforces Keystone's value as the only other major egress option to the US Gulf. Each clean operating quarter compresses the discount vs. C-corp midstream peers (PAA, ENB, PPL) trading at 10-12x EV/EBITDA while SOBO is closer to 8-9x. Settlement of historical leak liabilities removes overhang. As US dividend funds/closet MLP investors discover an unlevered-by-MLP-K1-headache 7% yielder, multiple compression toward peers is plausible.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN't SEE
Form 10 details: (1) the inter-co debt allocation — TRP loaded SOBO with roughly USD $4.8B of debt at separation, sized to maintain investment-grade ratings (BBB area) and to send cash back to the parent; this is a classic "parent dumps debt on the cleaner asset" mechanic but the cash flow comfortably services it. (2) Transition Services Agreements with TRP cover IT, treasury, and certain accounting functions for up to 24 months — South Bow is building these in-house but the headline G&A run-rate will normalize higher in 2025-26. (3) Cross-border tax structuring: SOBO is a Canadian corporation paying its dividend in USD with a 15% Canadian withholding tax for US holders (recoverable in taxable accounts via foreign tax credit, lost in IRAs unless held below the IRA-treaty threshold). This nuance alone keeps many US retail income investors away. (4) The Keystone XL cancellation suit settlement and remaining environmental remediation costs are quantified in the risk factors at modest levels.
