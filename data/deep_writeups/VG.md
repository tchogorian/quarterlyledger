# {NUMBER}. VG — Venture Global Inc
## POST-LOCKUP | Lockup expired Jul 22, 2025 / -42% from IPO | Score: 58/100

### THE SETUP
Venture Global is a U.S. LNG export developer that owns and operates the Calcasieu Pass facility in Louisiana and is constructing the much larger Plaquemines and CP2 facilities. The company IPO'd January 24, 2025 at $25.00 per share — already marked down from an initial range in the $40s — making it one of the most high-profile but initially disappointing IPOs of 2025. The 180-day lockup expired July 22, 2025; insider selling and broader skepticism around the company's commercial model drove the stock to approximately $14.50 by April 2026, down roughly 42% from IPO and implying a market cap near $35 billion (still very large given the multi-class share structure).

### ANALYST SUMMARY
The bull thesis rests on long-dated LNG offtake economics and U.S. structural advantage. Venture Global has signed 20-year sale-and-purchase agreements (SPAs) with credit-worthy counterparties (Shell, BP, Repsol, EDF, and others) on take-or-pay terms indexed to Henry Hub plus a liquefaction fee. If the announced capacity at Plaquemines and CP2 is delivered on schedule and the SPAs remain enforceable, the discounted cash flow math supports an equity value materially above $14.50. Management has consistently argued the spot-vs-contract "gap" — the company's practice of selling commissioning cargoes at elevated spot prices before declaring commercial operations under SPAs — is a legitimate feature of the project-financing model.

The bear thesis explains the stock price. Several key SPA counterparties — including Shell, BP, and Repsol — have filed arbitration claims alleging Venture Global breached contractual obligations by diverting cargoes to the spot market during the 2022-2023 TTF spike. The aggregate damages sought reportedly approach or exceed $5 billion. Beyond the litigation, the longer-dated concern is that counterparties' loss of trust may complicate CP2's remaining offtake marketing and refinancing. The post-lockup drawdown was partly a structural flow phenomenon, but the arbitration overhang is fundamental and not yet resolved.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The dual-class share structure and controlling shareholder dynamic matter. Founders Michael Sabel and Robert Pelletier retain supermajority voting control post-IPO, which has governance implications for any strategic response to the arbitration claims. A second screen-invisible factor is the project-finance architecture: Venture Global's debt is largely non-recourse at the project level, which means the parent-company equity holds meaningful optionality if individual project outcomes diverge. Third, the broader LNG build-out context — Trump administration lifting of the LNG export pause in January 2025, FERC and DOE approvals accelerating — is a genuine tailwind for U.S. LNG equities that is not specific to VG but supports the sector backdrop.

### CATALYSTS
- Arbitration rulings or settlements with Shell, BP, and Repsol — binary and material
- Plaquemines LNG commercial operations declaration
- CP2 LNG final investment decision and offtake marketing
- Q1 2026 earnings — cash-cost guidance and realized pricing
- Potential dividend or capital return given high operating cash flow at Calcasieu

### MUNGER CHECKLIST
- Simple business: Partially — the physical operation is simple (liquefy, load, ship); the commercial and contractual layer is not
- Pricing power: Yes on spot cargoes during tight markets; no on SPA-contracted volumes (by design)
- Honest, capable management: Contested — the arbitration filings allege material breaches; founders retain voting control
- Reasonable price: At $14.50 vs $25 IPO, the valuation has compressed; the arbitration contingency makes point-estimate IRR unreliable
- Within circle of competence: Requires LNG commercial contract expertise and project-finance literacy
- Margin of safety: Complicated — the equity has real value if SPAs hold, potentially impaired if arbitration results in large damages or contract termination

The trade is a busted-IPO with a genuine legal overhang rather than a simple post-lockup technical recovery. A position here requires comfort with binary arbitration outcomes. For investors who can underwrite the legal risk, the 42% discount to IPO on a levered LNG build-out with U.S. policy tailwinds is interesting. For everyone else, wait for arbitration clarity — the first meaningful ruling is likely to move the stock more than another year of operational progress.
