## SPINOFF | ~14% dividend yield, $114M micro-cap | Score: 62/100

### THE SETUP

Sunrise Realty Trust was spun off from AFC Gamma (AFCG) on July 9, 2024. The parent, AFC Gamma, is a specialty cannabis-credit lender; Sunrise inherited AFCG's small portfolio of non-cannabis Sun-Belt commercial real estate loans and operates as an externally managed mortgage REIT (the manager, Sunrise Manager LLC, is affiliated with the same sponsor — Leonard Tannenbaum's complex). Market cap is ~$114M, current share price ~$8.53, with a trailing dividend yield in the low-to-mid teens.

The spinoff dynamics here are textbook: AFCG holders are cannabis-credit specialists who suddenly received a thinly traded Sun-Belt CRE-mezz REIT they had zero appetite for. Forced selling pressure was immediate and persistent through late 2024 and into 2025. The micro-cap size disqualifies it from virtually every institutional benchmark — there's no natural buyer base. Layer on the externally managed structure (which classic REIT investors deride for fee drag), and you have a chronic orphan asset.

The bull case is that the loan book is brand new — 2024-to-2026 vintages underwritten *after* the regional banking dislocation, when distressed banks were actively shedding CRE exposure. Non-bank originators with capital and underwriting discipline can pick over the carcass at attractive spreads. SUNS's loans are largely floating-rate, secured by Sun-Belt assets (multifamily, mixed-use, hospitality), with sponsors who have skin in the game.

### ANALYST SUMMARY (3p)

This is a yield-and-NAV story dressed up as a spinoff. The 14% dividend looks scary but is currently covered by net interest income on the loan book — the question is whether it stays covered as loans season and any credit issues emerge. Vintage matters: a brand-new loan book underwritten in 2024–2026 should not have material credit problems for at least 2–3 years (CRE loans typically have 3–5 year terms with delayed credit recognition). The portfolio's Sun-Belt focus benefits from population/job migration trends.

The structural negatives are real. External management = ~1.5% management fee on equity plus an incentive fee; this drags ROE materially. The micro-cap status creates persistent illiquidity. The shared-sponsor relationship with AFC Gamma creates governance optics issues. And there's a complete absence of operating history as a standalone entity — no one knows yet whether the manager can build a book of meaningful scale.

The optionality lever is "strategic alternatives" — at $114M market cap, SUNS is an obvious roll-up candidate for a larger non-bank CRE lender, or could be liquidated at NAV (which is meaningfully above current price). Tannenbaum's complex has shown willingness to monetize prior platforms, and the externally managed structure makes a manager buy-in or sale comparatively straightforward.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

A screen sees: micro-cap mortgage REIT, unproven, externally managed, double-digit yield (often a yield trap signal). It misses: (a) the spinoff orphan dynamic that explains the discount mechanically, (b) the brand-new loan vintage as a credit-quality positive, (c) the structural takeout/rollup optionality given the parent's track record, and (d) that NAV per share materially exceeds market price — a recurring CEF-like discount that activist or strategic intervention could close. It also misses the Sun-Belt CRE thesis: while national CRE remains under pressure, Sun-Belt fundamentals (multifamily, industrial) have held up better than gateway markets.

### CATALYSTS

- Quarterly NII and originations updates demonstrating loan book scaling
- Strategic alternatives review outcome (if pursued)
- Dividend coverage maintained or grown over coming quarters
- Loan-portfolio mark-to-market updates as Sun-Belt CRE values stabilize
- Potential rollup/sale to a larger non-bank CRE lender
- Any institutional ownership disclosure

### MUNGER CHECKLIST

- **Business quality**: Below average. Externally managed mortgage REITs are structurally inferior to internally managed peers due to fee drag and conflicts. CRE credit is cyclical. Brand-new platform with no operating track record.
- **Management**: Tannenbaum complex has built and monetized prior credit platforms; experienced underwriters but the external structure aligns interests imperfectly with public shareholders.
- **Balance sheet**: Clean — minimal corporate leverage at the entity level, loan book is the asset. As a young REIT, balance sheet has not been tested.
- **Capital allocation**: Pays out the bulk of taxable income as required for REIT status; share count is small but has grown via secondaries. Watch for dilutive issuance.
- **Margin of safety**: Trades meaningfully below NAV; downside cushioned by loan book asset value if credit holds. Upside requires either (a) the discount to NAV to close as the platform scales, or (b) a strategic transaction.
- **Risks**: Credit deterioration in any single large loan, external manager conflicts, dividend cut if NII compresses, micro-cap illiquidity making exits painful, sponsor self-dealing.

This is a position-sizing story more than a conviction story — the asymmetry is real but the platform is unproven. Suitable as a small allocation in a basket of orphan-spinoff yield names where the goal is to clip the dividend while waiting for either NAV convergence or a strategic event. The 62 score reflects a genuine value opportunity weighed against the structural negatives of micro-cap external management.
