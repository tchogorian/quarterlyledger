### ANALYST SUMMARY
Mister Car Wash is the largest US conveyorized car wash chain and a classic LGP holding from the pre-IPO era. After taking the company public in 2021 at $15, LGP held its controlling stake through the post-IPO compression and announced the $7.00/share take-private on February 17, 2026, valuing the company at roughly $3.1B equity. Financing risk is low — LGP is the buyer at 67% control, with $900M in committed term loan financing — and shareholder vote approval is near-certain on the controller's vote alone, subject to majority-of-minority structures depending on the merger agreement.

The bear case is precisely the controller-conflict structure: Leonard Green is buying out minority shareholders at a price the controller chose, and the tape trading through $7.00 indicates that arb desks see real risk of either an appraisal action or a fairness-opinion-driven bump. If no bump materializes and the deal closes at $7.00, anyone buying at $7.01 takes a small loss plus opportunity cost from the time-to-close. If the deal breaks (unlikely given controller economics), the unaffected price was meaningfully lower and the downside is real.

The bull case has two legs. First, the appraisal/litigation path: in conflicted controller deals Delaware courts have repeatedly priced fair value above announced consideration, and the bare-minimum investor strategy is to vote no, hold, and pursue appraisal. Second, the bump path: independent committees in similar LGP-style sponsor take-privates have negotiated 5–10% bumps when minority pressure or fairness-opinion challenges materialize. Either path has positive expected value at $7.01 against $7.00 deal terms, with very limited downside given controller's financial commitment.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 13E-3 filed April 3, 2026 should disclose the special committee process, the financial advisor's fairness opinion range, and projections used — these define the appraisal floor. The proxy will show whether the deal includes a majority-of-the-minority condition; if it does, the litigation risk on the controller is dramatically lower (per MFW), and the bump probability falls. Also worth checking: store-level unit economics and 2025 same-store-sales trends in the proxy projections — those are the cleanest carwash industry comps available anywhere and are the actual fundamental anchor for any appraisal claim.
