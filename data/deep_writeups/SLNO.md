### THE SETUP
On April 6, 2026, Neurocrine Biosciences announced a definitive agreement to acquire Soleno Therapeutics for $53.00 per share in cash via a tender offer plus second-step merger structure, valuing the deal at approximately $2.9B. Soleno is a small-cap rare-disease biotech whose lead asset is VYKAT XR, a once-daily extended-release diazoxide choline therapy for Prader-Willi syndrome — a rare genetic disorder causing chronic hyperphagia. The transaction was announced at a 34% premium to Soleno's prior unaffected price. SLNO traded almost immediately into a tight gross spread: at the current price of $52.78 versus the $53.00 deal price, the gross spread is approximately 0.42%, implying an annualized return of roughly 2.5% assuming a Q2 2026 close. The SC TO-C was filed in early April 2026, with the formal tender expected to commence by mid-April and HSR review pending. This is a clean, low-complexity strategic-buyer rare-disease deal — exactly the profile that historically prints with high probability of close.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
The merger-arbitrage case here is "high-probability close at modest annualized return," not a wide-spread special situation. The pieces line up cleanly. (1) Strategic buyer: Neurocrine is an established, cash-rich endocrinology and rare-disease specialist — VYKAT XR fits squarely into its existing therapeutic franchise (INGREZZA, CRENESSITY) and there is essentially no product overlap that would create antitrust concern. (2) Financing risk: Effectively zero. Neurocrine is funding the deal from balance-sheet cash and has no contingent financing condition. (3) Antitrust: Rare-disease deals between non-overlapping franchises typically clear HSR without a second request. The 30-day HSR clock should run cleanly. (4) Shareholder approval: Tender offer requires only >50% of shares tendered — far easier to clear than a vote, and large institutional holders typically tender into a 34% premium without resistance. (5) Interloper risk: Possible but low — the 34% premium is reasonable relative to comparable rare-disease takeouts, and Soleno would almost certainly have run a process before signing. The principal risk to the spread is timing slippage rather than deal break: if HSR drags from "Q2" into "early Q3," the annualized return compresses but the absolute return is preserved. At current levels, the trade is a low-IRR, high-probability bond-like position appropriate for arbitrage capital with a defined holding window.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Three things. First, the rare-disease structure means the true diligence question isn't "will the deal close" but "will VYKAT XR's launch trajectory hold up between sign and close" — which matters for any potential MAC clause but in practice rarely triggers given the orphan-drug economics. Second, Neurocrine's strategic logic — building scale in endocrinology after CRENESSITY's launch — makes this acquisition more important to Neurocrine than the headline price suggests, reducing walk-away risk. Third, tender-offer mechanics mean the deal can close meaningfully faster than a proxy-vote merger: once minimum tender condition is met and HSR clears, settlement happens within days, not weeks — a structural feature that screens treating all M&A spreads identically miss.
