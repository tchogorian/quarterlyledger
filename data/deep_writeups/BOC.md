### THE SETUP
Boston Omaha is Adam Peterson's (Buffett's grand-nephew via Magnolia Capital) micro-cap holdco trading at $12.53 vs. ~$18 indicative tangible book — a ~30% discount to a portfolio of cash-generative billboard assets, rural fiber broadband, surety insurance, an asset-management platform and a stake in Sky Harbour. New buyback authorized; closely held; sentiment bombed-out after co-CEO departure. The kind of small, ugly, ignored compounder Munger said you find by sitting alone in a room.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Boston Omaha (BOC) is a $388M-mcap holdco operating four segments: (1) Link Media Outdoor — the fourth-largest US billboard operator behind Lamar/Outfront/Clear Channel with ~3,500 billboards; (2) AireBeam + FibAir rural fiber broadband (>$30M revenue, growing); (3) General Indemnity Group — surety insurance growing 20%+ annually; (4) Boston Omaha Asset Management (BOAM), managing third-party LP capital across real estate, sports and a minority interest in Sky Harbour (private aviation hangars). Cash and securities ~$200M+. ~30.7M shares outstanding. Indicative NAV/share ~$18 (per the bull thesis circulating in Q1 2026 Nasdaq/Motley Fool coverage), price $12.53 → ~30% discount. Operating segments combined produce ~$50M EBITDA, valued at 10x = $500M equity vs. $388M mcap before adjusting for cash and Sky Harbour stake.

Bear case: governance scar tissue. Co-CEO Alex Rozek departed April 2024, leaving Adam Peterson as sole CEO — the market has not forgiven the resulting ambiguity around capital allocation and segment focus. March 2026 Motley Fool piece labeled it a "value trap" citing weak management execution and slow capital deployment. Sky Harbour stake creates equity-mark volatility (it's a public stock; quarterly mark-to-market hits BOC's earnings). Billboards are a slow-growth, ad-cyclical business; rural fiber is capex-heavy and faces BEAD subsidy delays. Surety insurance is sub-scale vs. The Hartford/Travelers. ~$1M daily volume = small-cap illiquidity that punishes any forced exit. The Berkshire-junior comparisons set unrealistic expectations.

Bull case: tangible book of ~$18/share is mostly hard assets (billboard structures with permanent permits, fiber buried in the ground, regulated insurance capital, public-mark Sky Harbour shares, T-bills). At $12.53, you're paying 70 cents on the dollar for a portfolio that compounds at mid-teens. New buyback authorization is meaningful — at $12.53 with ~30.7M shares and $200M+ cash, management can shrink the share count materially if Peterson chooses to. Median analyst PT $18 = 44% upside to fair value before any operating beat. Catalysts: broadband EBITDA inflection (negative → positive as subscriber base scales), Sky Harbour value crystallization, BOAM AUM growth providing high-margin third-party fees, and accretive M&A using Peterson's underwriting discipline.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 10-K segment disclosures show Link Media's billboard portfolio is about 70% digital-convertible — meaning the 3,500 boards have an embedded "digital upgrade" optionality that triples revenue per face when converted. Roughly 20% has already been converted; the remaining runway is 5-7 years of organic capex-funded growth at 15-20% IRRs. Sky Harbour stake (BOC owns ~10-15% via SHG) is marked at quoted price, but the underlying private-aviation hangar business has 50%+ EBITDA margins and a 20-year lease book — likely undervalued at quoted price. Adam Peterson's Magnolia Capital owns ~17% of BOC personally — every dollar of buyback shrinks his denominator while he keeps absolute holdings. Boston Omaha Asset Management (BOAM) is a hidden compounder: it earns 1-2% management fees on outside LP capital with near-zero incremental cost, and AUM disclosure has been climbing.
