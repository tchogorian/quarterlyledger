# {NUMBER}. SBC — SBC Medical Group Holdings Inc.
## SPINOFF | Japan aesthetic-medicine MSO at ~$4.50 | Score: 58/100

### THE SETUP
SBC Medical Group is the largest aesthetic-medicine management services organization (MSO) in Japan, providing franchise services — branding, IT, supply chain, marketing — to a network of cosmetic surgery, dermatology, and fertility clinics. The company began trading on Nasdaq on September 25, 2024 via a Go-IPO/SPAC-style listing process arranged by HeartCore Enterprises and the Pono Capital structure. At ~$4.50 the market cap is ~$460M against a Japan-domiciled, founder-controlled (Aikawa-led) operator with mid-teens revenue growth, high-30s EBITDA margins, and an announced $50M shelf registration plus buyback authorization. Every box of the "orphan equity post-SPAC" pattern is checked: confusing legal genealogy, foreign-issuer reporting (20-F), no US sell-side coverage, and a controlling shareholder.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
The underlying business is genuinely attractive. Aesthetic medicine in Japan has structural tailwinds (aging affluent population, normalization of cosmetic procedures across millennial cohorts, low historical penetration relative to Korea). SBC operates an asset-light franchise model where individual clinics are owned by physicians and SBC collects per-clinic management fees, supply mark-ups, and brand royalties — analogous to a healthcare-services consolidator with negative working capital characteristics. FY2025 results disclosed mid-teens revenue growth and high-30s EBITDA margins, which would put the stock at roughly mid-single-digit EV/EBITDA depending on cash position — extraordinarily cheap for a profitable services compounder if the numbers prove durable.

The mispricing is structural, not fundamental. SBC arrived on US markets via the Pono Capital SPAC vehicle and the HeartCore Go-IPO consulting structure — a path that is, fairly or unfairly, associated with low-quality Asian carve-outs. The float is small, the controlling shareholder owns the supermajority, and the SEC reporting status is foreign private issuer. US institutional buyers cannot index it (no S&P/Russell inclusion), cannot run it through standard quant screens (limited history, foreign GAAP reconciliation), and cannot easily diligence the Japan clinic operations. The ~$50M shelf + buyback announcement (March 2026) suggests management views the discount as opportunity.

The honest risks: (1) related-party transactions with the parent network are extensive and disclosure quality is improving but still below US standards; (2) aesthetic medicine is genuinely discretionary — a Japan recession or a regulatory tightening (e.g., PMDA stance on injectables) hurts immediately; (3) US franchise expansion ambitions are at the "press release" stage with limited evidence of unit-economics traction; (4) a low float plus controlling shareholder creates squeeze and dump dynamics. Position sizing should reflect microcap foreign-issuer risk.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
US screens flag the SPAC heritage and skip. They cannot price a Japanese Go-IPO carve-out trading on Nasdaq at single-digit EBITDA multiples while the comparable US MSO peer set (USPH, OPCH-style) trades at 12-18x. They also cannot see that the buyback was authorized rather than executed — actual share count reduction will be the real signal of management conviction.

### CATALYSTS
- **Buyback execution disclosures (each quarterly 6-K through 2026):** evidence of actual repurchases under the $50M authorization.
- **First US clinic franchise opening (announced for 2026):** validates the international expansion thesis with real unit economics.
- **Initiation of US sell-side coverage (likely H2 2026):** the most likely re-rating trigger as the foreign-issuer discount compresses.
