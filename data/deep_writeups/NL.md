# {NUMBER}. NL — NL Industries, Inc.
## HOLDCO — NAV DISCOUNT | ~45% Look-Through Discount | Score: 64/100

### THE SETUP
NL Industries is the middle layer of the Harold Simmons / Contran three-tier holding structure (Contran -> Valhi VHI -> NL -> CompX/Kronos). NL owns 87% of CompX International (CIX) and roughly 30% of Kronos Worldwide (KRO). The look-through public-market value of those stakes alone is north of $470M against NL's ~$290M market cap — before counting net cash, the dividend, or any operating earnings. You are buying the underlying public stocks at roughly 60 cents on the dollar.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
NL Industries is a remnant of the legacy National Lead conglomerate, today a holding company whose value sits almost entirely in two publicly traded stakes plus net cash and a small residual operations footprint. The 87% stake in CompX (CIX), a security-products and marine-components maker, throws off a meaningful slug of dividend income and is the highest-quality asset in the structure. The ~30% stake in Kronos Worldwide (KRO) is a leveraged play on the global TiO2 (titanium dioxide) cycle, currently near multi-year price lows after a brutal post-COVID destocking. NL pays a $0.32/share dividend and has historically issued large special dividends when the holding-company cash pile gets uncomfortably big — this is the principal mechanism by which the discount is monetized over time.

The bear case for NL is that the discount is structural and won't close. The Contran/Valhi/NL/Kronos structure has existed for decades, the Simmons heirs control roughly 87% of NL through Contran/Valhi, and there is no realistic activist path. Liquidity is poor (average daily volume often under 100k shares), which excludes most institutional capital. Lead-paint litigation, while largely settled at this point, has periodically flared up over the past two decades and creates tail-risk that is impossible to fully underwrite. KRO is a commodity chemicals producer with limited pricing power, capital-intensive, and currently underearning — so the look-through "value" of the KRO stake assumes a cyclical recovery that may take years. The CIX stake is high quality but small in absolute dollars.

The bull case is straightforward look-through math: NL market cap of ~$290M versus stakes worth $470M+ at current public prices, plus net cash at the holdco level, plus a 5%+ dividend yield collected while you wait. Trades at 0.86x book value and roughly 5x trailing P/E. KRO is at a cyclical trough — TiO2 prices and operating rates typically mean-revert sharply, and a Kronos earnings recovery could double the look-through NAV. CompX continues to grind out high single-digit organic growth with strong margins. The Simmons family has a multi-decade history of paying out cash via specials when the holdco is liquid — patient holders have been rewarded with periodic large distributions.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
NL's 10-K balance sheet shows the stake holdings at carrying value, not market value — to compute look-through NAV you need to multiply NL's CIX share count (~10.7M shares × CIX price) and KRO share count (~30% of ~115M = ~34M shares × KRO price) and add NL's holdco cash net of liabilities. The footnotes also disclose remaining lead-paint reserves and the status of California public-nuisance litigation, which has been the single biggest swing factor over the past 15 years. Insider/Contran ownership at ~87% is disclosed in the proxy. Most importantly: the dividend history, including specials, is not in the typical screening database — NL has paid multiple large special dividends over the past decade, and these specials, not capital appreciation, are the principal historical return mechanism.
