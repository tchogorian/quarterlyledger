### THE SETUP
A 95-year-old equity CEF persistently trading at an 11.5% discount to NAV with a 0.49% expense ratio (cheaper than most ETFs), an annual managed distribution policy targeting 6%+, and Saba Capital sitting as the 8th-largest position in their portfolio at $109M / 5.4% of fund. Discount-narrowing catalyst is policy-mandated: tender at 98% of NAV if discount averages >19% for 30 consecutive days.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Adams Diversified Equity Fund (NYSE: ADX) is a closed-end equity fund founded in 1929, managed in-house by Adams Funds (Baltimore), with a 0.49% expense ratio in 2025 (down from 0.56% in 2024 and 0.61% in 2023 per the DEF 14A filed Feb 18, 2026). Annual portfolio turnover was 55.6% in 2025. The fund's 6%+ managed distribution policy, paid in quarterly installments with a year-end true-up, is the standard "yield-shop" CEF structure. Net asset value per share at the latest disclosed point is $27.10 vs market price $23.98 (11.5% discount), within the chronic 9–13% historical band. The annual meeting (Apr 16, 2026) had two directors up for election — routine governance items, no Saba activist proposal yet on the ballot. PwC reappointed as auditor.

The bear case is structural: ADX has traded at a persistent discount for decades because it owns a S&P 500-like portfolio with no leverage, no special distributions, no derivatives kicker. An investor seeking equity exposure can buy SPY at NAV with 0.09% expense ratio versus paying 0.49% AND a discount-narrowing wait at ADX. The managed distribution policy (~6% annual rate) is funded partly from realized gains, partly from ROC if NAV doesn't appreciate enough — meaning in a bear market the distribution becomes self-liquidating. Saba's most recent reported activity was actually TRIMMING the position 15.5% in Q4 2025 to 5,428,866 shares, suggesting either profit-taking or that Boaz Weinstein doesn't see imminent catalyst. The board has shown no willingness to convert to ETF or open-end the fund.

The bull case is an asymmetric trade with low downside. Total return = (1) underlying S&P-like equity beta, (2) discount narrowing from ~11.5% toward 5–7% if Saba escalates or the board adopts a Stronger DMP (any narrowing of 3–5 percentage points = 3–5% windfall on top of beta), and (3) the policy-mandated tender at 98% of NAV if discount averages >19% for 30 days creates a hard cap on widening risk. The 6%+ distribution rate at a 11.5% discount = effective yield-on-cost of 6.8% on equity holdings. If Saba files a 13D/A escalating demands (or proposes board candidates for 2027), the discount could rapidly compress. ADX's $3B-ish AUM makes it large enough for institutional involvement but small enough for activist meaningful influence.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
ADX is one of the few CEFs with an INTERNALIZED management team (Adams Funds employees ARE the manager — there's no third-party advisor extracting fees). The proxy discloses employees participate in a tax-qualified Thrift Plan with the Fund matching 100% of contributions up to 6% of base salary plus discretionary 6% on top — alignment is decent. The 0.49% expense ratio is actually lower than many active ETFs because the fund has no external advisor markup. Saba's stake reduction in Q4 2025 to 5.4M shares (still 8th largest in Saba's book at $109M) suggests Weinstein is reweighting toward higher-discount opportunities, but the position remains material. The Adams Funds family also runs ADX-sister fund PEO (Adams Natural Resources) which Saba targeted previously — same sponsor, similar discount dynamics — providing a template for board concessions if pressure escalates.

### CATALYSTS (next 6-12 months)

- **Potential 2027 proxy slate** — Saba precedent at other CEFs (ASA Gold, BlackRock muni funds) is to nominate dissident directors
