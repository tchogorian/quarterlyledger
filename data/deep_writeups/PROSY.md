# {NUMBER}. PROSY — Prosus N.V.
## HOLDCO — NAV DISCOUNT | ~38% DISCOUNT | Score: 80/100

### THE SETUP

Prosus N.V. is the Amsterdam-listed technology investment vehicle controlled by South Africa's Naspers, holding ~24% of Tencent (HK:0700) plus a portfolio of emerging-market consumer-internet assets. At a current price near $8.95 with indicative NAV of ~$14.50, Prosus trades at a roughly 38% discount to NAV — a structural inefficiency that management has been actively attacking via what is arguably the most sophisticated buyback engine in global large-cap holdcos. Market cap stands near $200B, making this one of the largest "stub-trade" opportunities in public markets.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

The mechanics of the Prosus open-ended buyback are elegant: Prosus periodically sells small slices of its Tencent stake into the market, and uses the cash to repurchase Prosus (and parent Naspers) shares trading at a 38% discount. Every $1 of Tencent sold and $1 of Prosus retired creates ~$0.60 of accretion to NAV-per-share for remaining holders — a self-funding compounding loop. From the program's 2022 launch through early 2024, the holdco discount narrowed by 21 points, unlocking roughly $30B of value and growing NAV-per-share by ~8%. With $200B of market cap and a still-wide 38% discount, the program has years of runway.

The asset stack: ~24% of Tencent (worth roughly $130B at today's ~$540B Tencent market cap), plus owned/controlled e-commerce franchises — iFood (Latin American food delivery, dominant in Brazil), Stack Overflow, eMag (Eastern European e-commerce), Swiggy stake (recently IPO'd in India), iyzico (payments), OLX Group (classifieds), and a substantial net cash position (~$15B). The e-commerce portfolio is genuinely growing — iFood is profitable, Swiggy has IPO'd creating a public mark, and several assets approach IPO-readiness.

The discount persists because: (1) European-listed holdcos with single-asset concentration carry endemic discounts; (2) the perception of China/Tencent regulatory risk weighs on valuations even as Tencent's earnings have stabilized; (3) the e-commerce assets are complex and harder to mark; (4) Tencent ETF substitutes (KraneShares, etc.) offer purer Tencent exposure for traders. But none of those structural factors prevent NAV-per-share compounding via buybacks — they just keep the headline discount sticky while underlying intrinsic value grows.

In 2024 Tencent began making in-kind dividend distributions (paying out shares of Meituan, JD.com), and Prosus has been distributing or monetizing those stakes — adding another vector of intrinsic value crystallization. Naspers (the South African parent) runs an analogous buyback against its discount to Prosus, creating a double-compounding chain.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

A screen sees a foreign ADR with confusing accounting and a single big-tech bet. It does not see the buyback flywheel, the in-kind dividend monetization, the e-commerce optionality (iFood IPO could mark $5–10B), or the parent/sub structural arbitrage between Naspers and Prosus. Most US screens also fail to capture the in-kind Tencent distribution mechanics, which are structurally accretive.
