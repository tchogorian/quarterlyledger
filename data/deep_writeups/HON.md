# {NUMBER}. HON — Honeywell International Inc.
## ACTIVIST | Three-Way Breakup H2 2026 | Score: 76/100

### THE SETUP
Elliott Investment Management disclosed a $5B+ stake in Honeywell on November 12, 2024 — its largest-ever single-stock position — and explicitly demanded a three-way break-up. On February 6, 2025 Honeywell capitulated and announced separation into three independent listed companies (Aerospace, Automation, Advanced Materials) targeted for H2 2026. At $233.55 the stub still trades below most sum-of-parts analyses that re-rate Aerospace toward LMT/RTX multiples post-separation.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Honeywell is the textbook industrial conglomerate — Aerospace (engines, avionics, APUs, defense), Industrial Automation (process controls, productivity solutions, sensing), Building Automation, and Advanced Materials (specialty chemicals, fluorine products, electronic materials). The historical conglomerate discount has been worth roughly 20-30% versus pure-play comparables for years; Aerospace alone, if traded as a standalone, would arguably command 18-22x earnings versus the blended 18x the parent receives. Elliott's pitch, made publicly in November 2024 and codified in a deepened cooperation agreement in May 2025, is the classic "split into pieces, let each get its rightful multiple" thesis — and Honeywell agreed within 90 days of Elliott going public, an unusually fast capitulation.

The bear case: separations are operationally taxing. Honeywell will spend 18+ months running Form 10 work, dis-synergies on shared services, and management distraction from operating the core businesses. Aerospace, the crown jewel, is exposed to defense budget cyclicality and commercial OEM build-rate volatility (Boeing 737 MAX, Airbus rate ramps). Automation is a competitive battle against ETN, ROK, EMR. Advanced Materials carries chemical-cycle and PFAS-litigation tail risk that may not get a clean public-market reception as a standalone — investors may discount it harshly, partially offsetting the Aerospace re-rating. Execution risk is real: Honeywell hasn't run a multi-way separation before, and management bandwidth is finite.

The bull case: post-separation Aerospace alone, at LMT's 22x or RTX's 20x earnings on its share of HON profit, justifies the bulk of today's $233 stub. Automation, valued conservatively at 15-18x, adds real upside. Advanced Materials at even a discount-house 10-12x is incremental. Sum-of-parts analyses from Street and Elliott implications point to mid-$280s to $300+ post-separation value within 12-18 months. Index-rebalancing forced flows on separation day create a known catalyst window; long-only money is structurally underweight all three pieces today.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 2024 10-K Aerospace segment disclosure shows commercial original-equipment and aftermarket revenue continuing to recover post-COVID, with margins expanding as mix shifts back toward higher-margin aftermarket parts and services. The 10-K describes the company's portfolio actions including the Quantinuum carve-out, the announced spin of Advanced Materials, and the planned separation of Aerospace and Automation. Quantinuum (quantum computing JV) is carried at modest book but commands billion-dollar private-market valuations and may itself be monetized post-split. Pension over-funding and surplus cash on the balance sheet support pre-separation special distributions or buybacks that aren't in screen-friendly EPS estimates.

- Q1/Q2 2026 earnings updates on cost-to-separate
