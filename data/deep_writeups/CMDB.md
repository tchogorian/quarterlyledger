# {NUMBER}. CMDB — Costamare Bulkers Holdings Limited
## SPINOFF — FORCED SELLING | Sub-$500M Float, NAV Discount | Score: 74/100

### THE SETUP
Costamare spun its dry-bulk fleet to shareholders on May 7, 2025, distributing one CMDB share for every five CMRE shares. Container-shipping investors were handed a sub-$500M Greek dry-bulk orphan and dumped it. CMDB now trades at $17.26 — well below the implied vessel-NAV — while dry-bulk rates have rerated through 2025-2026 on China stimulus and grain re-routing.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Costamare Bulkers is a pure-play dry-bulk shipping vehicle owning a fleet of Capesize, Kamsarmax, and Ultramax bulk carriers, plus an asset-light trading platform that charters in third-party tonnage to capture spread. The Konstantakopoulos family — long-term Greek shipping operators with a strong capital-allocation reputation at parent CMRE — retains material insider ownership and is aligned alongside minorities. The combined model (owned fleet + trading desk) is unusual for a pure-play dry-bulk peer set dominated by single-segment fleet owners (SBLK, GOGL, EGLE).

The bear case is that dry-bulk is brutally cyclical: rates can collapse 70% in 90 days when iron ore, coal, or grain trade slows. Single-vessel deals (S&P) and TCE rates have already rolled over from 2024 peaks for some segments. CMDB's small float (~$425M cap), foreign-private-issuer status, K-1 mechanics for U.S. investors, and Marshall Islands incorporation make it an institutional non-starter. Trading-platform earnings can be lumpy and depend on positioning. Greek-shipping holdcos historically trade at persistent discounts to NAV; CMRE itself has averaged ~15-25% discount over a decade. Dilution risk if new vessels are funded with equity at depressed prices.

The bull case is structural mispricing from a clean spinoff dynamic. CMRE shareholders had no reason to want dry bulk and forced sellers cleared at any price. Vessel values for Capesize and Kamsarmax have firmed in late 2025 / early 2026, suggesting CMDB's contributed book is conservative — NAV likely sits well above the current $17.26. The trading platform requires minimal capital and earns spreads measured in thousands per day per vessel chartered in. Management has signaled willingness to repurchase shares if the discount persists, and a strategic combination with another bulker or a take-private by the Konstantakopoulos family is the eventual catalyst.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The trading platform is the hidden asset. Within the segment disclosure, the chartered-in / charter-out spread business generates EBITDA on essentially zero capital employed — but is buried inside the consolidated income statement and is not separated by sell-side models. Most screens lump CMDB with steel-on-water bulker peers and apply a fleet multiple, missing the platform's capital-light contribution. Also: vessel values are reported at historical cost less depreciation, not market — the gap between book and broker-quoted values is the silent margin of safety here. Finally, the K-1 partnership structure deters U.S. mutual fund ownership, structurally suppressing the bid.

### CATALYSTS (next 6-12 months)
- Quarterly TCE updates and trading-platform segment disclosure (next: Q1 2026 results May 2026)
- Vessel-value mark-to-market in 2025 annual report
- Insider buying or buyback announcement; potential family take-private offer
