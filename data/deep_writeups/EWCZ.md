### THE SETUP
On February 9, 2026, European Wax Center announced a $5.80/share all-cash take-private by General Atlantic (already a ~42% holder), valuing the company at roughly $330M. Shareholder vote is scheduled May 7, 2026 at 10:30am CDT. The stock trades through the deal price ($5.83 vs. $5.80) on Kaskela investigation news, indicating market expectation of a price bump or litigation-driven term improvement.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
European Wax Center is the largest US franchise-based out-of-home waxing services chain, with 1,000+ locations operated by independent franchisees. The asset-light franchisor model generates royalty and product-distribution revenue at high margins. Post-IPO (August 2021 at $17), the stock has declined steadily as same-store sales decelerated, consumer discretionary spending weakened, and the franchise build-out slowed. General Atlantic, as the controlling pre-IPO sponsor, retained a substantial stake and is now taking the company private at a price that values it at a fraction of the IPO valuation.

The bear case is straightforward: the conflicted-controller structure means GA dictated the terms and minority shareholders have limited leverage. Special committee processes in conflicted controller buyouts typically yield modest 5-10% bumps at best, often zero. The Kaskela law firm investigation is likely a fishing expedition — most such investigations settle for token disclosure additions rather than price improvement. Vote passage looks very likely given GA's 42% stake and standard sponsor support agreements; a deal break would send the stock back toward standalone fair value, which is plausibly below the $5.80 deal price given recent operating trends. With financing already committed ($74M debt + $110M equity) and limited regulatory hurdles, the deal will close on terms.

The bull case is exactly the bump speculation the price action implies. Conflicted controller deals are subject to Delaware MFW scrutiny — the special committee must function properly, and minority approval thresholds (majority of the minority) give non-GA shareholders real voting leverage. Multiple investor letters and legal investigations create pressure for the special committee to negotiate harder. If GA bumps to $6.25-6.75 to ensure clean passage and avoid post-close appraisal litigation, the trade returns ~7-15% in three months. Appraisal rights in Delaware also provide a backstop for institutional holders willing to pursue them.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The proxy statement will include the special committee process narrative — financial advisors retained, range of bids considered, fairness opinion analysis, and any "go-shop" or market-check language. Read this carefully: weak process language signals bump vulnerability; strong process language (multiple advisors, broad market check, multiple board negotiations) signals deal price is final. Kaskela and similar firms file boilerplate investigations on virtually every controller buyout — these rarely produce material outcomes alone but serve as rallying points for institutional dissent. The May 7 vote is the binding event; if GA needs to bump to clear the majority-of-the-minority threshold (typically not required but sometimes structured in MFW deals), the bump comes 1-2 weeks before vote. Franchise unit count growth, same-center sales trends, and franchisee profitability data in the proxy will reveal standalone fair value — and likely show why GA is buying now.

- Potential bump announcement 1-2 weeks before May 7 vote
