### THE SETUP
Repay Holdings is a B2B and consumer payments processor sitting at the intersection of three live storylines as of mid-April 2026. (1) On March 30, 2026, RPAY announced an agreement to acquire KUBRA — a Canadian utility/government bill-presentment provider — for approximately $372M, financed with cash on hand and a new $500M term loan. (2) On April 9, 2026, Veradace Partners — an 8.4% holder per its SC 13D — issued an open letter to the independent board calling for the KUBRA deal to be terminated, citing "size, valuation and leverage" of the transaction and asserting that surveyed major holders unanimously oppose it; Veradace also demanded two shareholder representatives on the board. (3) RPAY had previously disclosed an unsolicited $4.80/share take-private proposal (Forager Capital), which the board has not accepted. RPAY's April 13, 2026 statement reiterated commitment to KUBRA, calling it governed by a definitive purchase agreement. With the stock at $4.11 versus the $4.80 standing bid, the gross spread is ~17% and the situation has multiple paths to resolution within 2026.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Three nested decisions sit on top of one B2B payments microcap. First, the operating business: RPAY's core consumer-payments verticals (auto loan, receivables management, mortgage servicing) and B2B AP automation generate recurring transaction revenue with reasonable but not spectacular growth. The stock has de-rated meaningfully over the past two years on competitive pressure and missed guides, which is precisely what attracted both an activist and a financial buyer. Second, the KUBRA deal: at ~$372M against a ~$359M market cap, this is a larger-than-the-buyer acquisition that levers the balance sheet to expand into utility/government bill pay — strategically defensible, but at a price and leverage profile that current holders find unattractive given RPAY's own depressed multiple. Third, the take-out: a $4.80 unsolicited proposal exists but has not been embraced; it implies ~$420M equity and may not survive the activist standoff intact.

The bull case is path-independent: even if KUBRA closes and the activist loses, the combined business will still trade at a depressed multiple that should attract another bid in 2026-2027; if KUBRA is terminated, the freed-up balance sheet capacity supports either a re-engaged sale process or aggressive buybacks at a value-accretive multiple; if the $4.80 bid (or higher) prevails, holders harvest 17%+ from current levels in months. The bear case is also real: the board can plough through, the proxy battle drags on without a deal, KUBRA integration distracts, payments competition erodes the legacy book, and the stock drifts back into the $3-handle.

The investable angle is the activist's leverage. Veradace's 8.4% stake, paired with what it represents as broad institutional opposition, is enough to (a) put a nomination threat on the table, (b) raise the cost of executing KUBRA without a re-cut, and (c) keep alive the prospect of a re-emergent or higher take-private bid. The asymmetry is not extreme — downside to the high-$3s if everything goes wrong is real — but the multi-path optionality and the existing standing offer make the risk-reward defensible at $4.11. Position-sizing should reflect the genuine possibility that the board simply outlasts the activist.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
A screen sees a sub-$5 payments microcap with declining profitability and elevated leverage from the pending acquisition. What it cannot see: (1) the activist proxy mechanics — Veradace has filed an SC 13D and put board demands in writing, which materially constrains directors; (2) the standing $4.80 take-private floor that has not been formally rejected; (3) the strategic logic (and the criticism) of KUBRA — a defensible expansion into utility/government billing at what holders argue is a premium price; (4) the dual-track optionality where either deal cancellation or a higher buyout improves shareholder outcomes; (5) the high reflexivity between price and bid behavior — every drop below $4 makes a take-out at $4.80 look stronger and vice versa.
