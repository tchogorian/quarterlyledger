# {NUMBER}. SNAP — Snap Inc.
## ACTIVIST | Irenic Capital "Snap Back to Reality" Mar 31 2026 | Score: 58/100

### THE SETUP
On March 31, 2026, activist Irenic Capital Management published an open letter titled "Snap Back to Reality" outlining a six-step turnaround plan for Snap Inc. and disclosing an approximately 2.5% Class A stake. The campaign argues that Snap, currently trading near $6 with a market cap of ~$10.2B, is structurally undervalued because of three decisions: (1) sustained spending on Spectacles AR hardware that has consumed roughly $3.5B of cumulative investment with ongoing burn of approximately $500M annually and no clear monetization path; (2) bloated headcount that exceeds what the business needs at its current scale; and (3) inadequate use of AI-driven automation in operations and content moderation. Irenic's plan calls for shutting down or spinning off Spectacles, a 21% workforce reduction, aggressive AI-driven cost reset, granting Class A shareholders one vote per share (eliminating the supervoting structure that gives founder/CEO Evan Spiegel near-complete voting control), and aggressive capital return. The activist's price target is $26 per share — roughly 7x the current price. SNAP shares jumped approximately 14% on the day of the letter to ~$6, and management has not yet formally responded.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
The Irenic case rests on a real fact pattern but runs into a structural wall. The fact pattern: cumulative Spectacles investment has likely exceeded the entire current market cap, and continuing $500M annual burn against a non-monetized product line is genuinely difficult to defend. The remaining business — Snapchat's social platform with 800M+ MAUs, Lens Studio AR developer ecosystem, Snapchat+ premium subscription tier, and an advertising business — would generate dramatically better margins if Spectacles were wound down and headcount were rationalized. Irenic's $26 target implicitly assumes that even a partial Spectacles wind-down combined with cost reset could double EBITDA, which is mathematically defensible. The structural wall: Snap's dual-class structure gives Evan Spiegel and Bobby Murphy effectively complete voting control. Without their cooperation, no activist proposal — Spectacles wind-down, headcount cuts, capital return, or one-share-one-vote — can be forced. Spectacles has been Spiegel's signature long-term bet, making him the least likely insider to voluntarily kill it. The realistic outcomes are therefore: (a) Spiegel partially capitulates to public pressure and quietly trims Spectacles spend without fully exiting (possible — improves the bear/base case modestly); (b) management announces a cost reset and partial buyback to defuse the campaign (more likely — gets stock to $8–10 zone); or (c) campaign fizzles and stock retraces toward $5 (also possible). The asymmetric upside Irenic articulates is real on paper but requires governance change that the dual-class structure makes very difficult.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Three things. First, dual-class structure: standard governance and activist screens that flag "activist target" frequently miss the binary nature of supervoting control — at Snap, Spiegel can simply say no, full stop. Second, the cumulative Spectacles investment exceeds reported R&D in any single year because much of it sits across multiple expense lines (R&D, S&M, G&A, capitalized software) — Irenic's $3.5B figure is sourced from cumulative segment-level disclosure that is not visible on standard fundamental screens. Third, advertising-business unit economics: Snap's ad business has improved meaningfully on direct-response ROAS in 2025–2026 but is masked at the consolidated level by Spectacles burn — a partial wind-down would re-rate the visible operating margin substantially even without core revenue growth.

- Any Spectacles wind-down, restructuring, or spin announcement
- Workforce reduction announcement
- Capital return / buyback authorization expansion
