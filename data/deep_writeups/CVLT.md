### ANALYST SUMMARY
Commvault is a mature data-protection and backup software vendor whose business has pivoted from on-prem perpetual licenses to a recurring subscription and SaaS (Metallic) model. Customers use it to back up enterprise workloads, manage compliance retention, and increasingly to recover from ransomware. The screen catches it because of the active confirmed sale process — InsideArbitrage flagged multiple PE bidders circling at speculative pricing around $110 per share or $4.9B equity. Spot at $94.23 implies the market is discounting deal-failure risk meaningfully — at full $110, gross spread is 16.7%; if no deal materializes, the unaffected price is likely lower.

Bear case: there is no definitive agreement, only press reports and banker chatter. Sale processes get pulled all the time when bidders fail to meet the board's price expectations or financing markets tighten. PE LBO math on enterprise software requires high recurring revenue mix and predictable retention — Commvault's transition to subscription is real but gross retention has historically lagged best-in-class peers like Veeam (private) and Rubrik (public). A failed process leaves the stock exposed to the post-pop reversion plus a lost competitive cycle. Cyber-resilience competition from Rubrik, Cohesity, Veeam, and the hyperscalers' native tools has intensified.

Bull case: the data-protection category is a genuine PE darling — recurring revenue, mission-critical workloads, ransomware tailwind, and a clear consolidation thesis. Commvault has $1B+ ARR and is FCF-positive, exactly the profile sponsors want for a leveraged buyout. With multiple bidders in process, competitive tension supports a clearing price at or above $110. If the deal lands at $110, the 16.7% gross spread is one of the better risk-adjusted returns in the current arb book, and even a $105 print delivers double-digit IRR. If the deal fails, the strategic review itself often surfaces operational changes that re-rate the stock standalone.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The reported sale process is mid-flight without a definitive 8-K, so the entire spread is qualitative — investors are pricing the joint probability of deal close, bidder identity (sponsor vs strategic), and final clearing price. The 10-K reveals Commvault's subscription mix has crossed 50% of total revenue, which is the LBO underwriting threshold most sponsors require. The Metallic SaaS line is the hidden lever — its growth trajectory is what allows a sponsor to underwrite a 6-7x ARR multiple. Any leak from the Wall Street Journal or Reuters naming a specific bidder would compress the spread by several hundred basis points instantly.
