# {NUMBER}. TVTX — Travere Therapeutics
## FDA BINARY | APPROVAL WON | Score: 78/100

### THE SETUP

Travere Therapeutics is a rare-renal-disease biotech whose lead asset, sparsentan (FILSPARI), is a dual endothelin and angiotensin receptor antagonist already commercialized in IgA nephropathy. The headline event happened on April 13, 2026: the FDA granted full approval of FILSPARI for focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), making it the first and only approved therapy for that indication. The PDUFA had originally been January 13, 2026 and was extended three months to April 13. The stock ripped 37% on the approval print and now trades around $42.13 with a market cap of ~$3.4B. The setup from here is no longer a binary — the binary was won — but rather a launch-execution and label-expansion story where the market has started to underwrite a second rare-disease indication on top of the existing IgAN ramp. FSGS affects roughly 40,000 patients in the US per published epidemiology, and as the only approved therapy, FILSPARI walks into a category where nephrologists have historically used off-label immunosuppression with poor evidence.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Travere's edge after April 13 is the conversion of "FSGS-eligible" patients into actual prescriptions. The existing IgAN commercial infrastructure — REMS-trained nephrologists, payor coverage, specialty pharmacy distribution — drops directly onto FSGS without requiring a second sales force build, which is the central operating-leverage argument. Analyst models that previously hard-coded FSGS as a $0 revenue line are now building in low-hundreds-of-millions of run-rate revenue by 2028, with bull cases substantially higher given the absence of competing approved therapy. The stock's 37% pop already reflects much of the obvious arithmetic, but launch-trajectory beats over the next two to three quarters can drive multiple expansion as the second indication de-risks and Travere starts to look less like a single-product, single-indication biotech and more like a renal-disease franchise. Risks are concrete: payor pushback on a high-priced rare-disease label that overlaps with cheap generic immunosuppression; the REMS program limits prescriber breadth; longer-term durability data in FSGS will dictate whether the market sustains pricing; and Travere still needs to demonstrate operating leverage at the bottom line — gross margins are high but SG&A and ongoing R&D in pegtibatinase and other pipeline assets keep the company near breakeven. A clean Q2 launch metric and a credible payor coverage update are the next two checkpoints.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

Standard biotech screens flag TVTX as expensive on EV/sales because the FSGS revenue contribution hasn't yet shown up in trailing financials — by definition, a label expansion granted in April 2026 has zero trailing revenue. The screens also miss that the FSGS approval converts a deeply underwritten "scenario" line in sell-side models into a "base case" line, which is a discount-rate change disguised as an earnings change. Three other things screens miss: (1) the REMS program is a moat — competitors can't easily replicate the patient-monitoring infrastructure and prescriber training; (2) the IgAN-FSGS commercial overlap means the incremental sales-force cost is near zero, so contribution margins on FSGS revenue are exceptionally high; (3) Travere's pipeline includes pegtibatinase for classical homocystinuria, an ultra-rare-disease asset with its own potential approval pathway, which gives the equity a free option that isn't in any screen.
