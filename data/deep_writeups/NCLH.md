### THE SETUP
Elliott Investment Management disclosed a >10% stake in NCLH on February 15, 2026 and reached a cooperation agreement on March 27, 2026 placing five new directors on a nine-seat board — including Steve Pagliuca (Bain Capital), Alex Cruz (ex-British Airways CEO), Kevin Lansberry (ex-Disney CFO), Brian MacDonald (CDK Global CEO), and Jonathan Cohen (Hepco CEO). CEO Harry Sommer's predecessor Frank Del Rio is gone, current CEO Joel Chidsey takes the chair, and four long-tenured directors stepped down by March 31. The cruise industry's #3 player is now in the middle of a Bain-touched operational reset while trading at a 30%+ EV/EBITDA discount to RCL.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
NCLH operates three cruise brands — Norwegian Cruise Line (contemporary), Oceania (premium), and Regent Seven Seas (luxury) — with ~32 ships and a forward-order book of new builds that will lift capacity meaningfully through 2028. Post-COVID, the cruise industry has run at extraordinary load factors (>105% double occupancy) with strong onboard spending and price/yield growth. NCLH has consistently underearned versus Royal Caribbean (RCL) and Carnival (CCL) on a per-berth basis — a multi-year margin gap that Elliott is now explicitly targeting. Equity at ~$21 against a market cap of ~$9.6B and a heavily leveraged balance sheet (still working off COVID-era debt) — but EBITDA is rising and free cash flow is turning positive at scale.

The bear case starts with the balance sheet: NCLH carries materially more debt per berth than RCL and the maturity wall through 2026–2028 is real. Cruise demand is cyclical and tied to consumer discretionary income; a U.S. consumer rollover (auto delinquencies, credit card balances) would compress yields and onboard spend. New-ship deliveries pressure free cash flow until they start earning. A lingering Middle East/Red Sea routing risk has forced repositioning of itineraries and added fuel cost. Most importantly, even if Elliott is right that operational gaps can be closed, RCL's brand power and Royal/Celebrity scale advantages mean some discount may persist permanently — the multiple may compress, not just expand.

The bull case is the cleanest activist setup of 2026: Elliott does not file 13Ds with five board seats unless they have a credible operational and capital-allocation roadmap. Pagliuca's involvement is the tell — Bain Capital is one of the few sponsors that could write the equity check for a take-private, and his presence on the board signals that path is on the menu. Even absent a deal, simply closing half of the ~30% EV/EBITDA gap to RCL is a 40–60% equity move at constant EBITDA. CEO compensation is being reset to performance metrics, capex discipline is on the table, and Q1/Q2 2026 earnings will reset depressed expectations.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The March 27, 2026 cooperation agreement (filed as exhibit to 8-K) is the document to read carefully — it specifies director-replacement mechanics, standstill terms, voting commitments, and any committee assignments. The newbuild order book in the 10-K capital commitments footnote shows multi-year capex of several billion through 2028; Elliott's most likely first move is to renegotiate or stretch some of these deliveries to free up FCF. Margin-gap math: comparing NCLH's per-berth EBITDA against RCL berth-by-berth (disclosed in supplemental schedules) shows the specific dollar opportunity — typically described as "$300–$500M of run-rate EBITDA" by sell-side analysts, which at a 9–10x multiple is the entire upside thesis. Steve Pagliuca's separate Bain affiliation also means any take-private process would route through a special committee — watch for the formation of one in 8-K disclosures.
