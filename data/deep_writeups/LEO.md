### ANALYST SUMMARY
LEO is a leveraged closed-end municipal bond fund managed by BNY Mellon (Insight Investment subsidiary). The fund holds a portfolio of investment-grade and high-yield US tax-exempt municipal bonds with ~30% structural leverage, distributing tax-exempt monthly income at a current ~6% yield on price. The fund has traded at a persistent discount to NAV for years — a classic muni CEF setup that Saba has industrialized into a templated activist campaign across the BlackRock, Nuveen, BNY, and Eaton Vance fund complexes. With Paul Kazarian (Saba's lead CEF activist) nominated, LEO is now a binary 2026 annual meeting catalyst.

The bear case is the standard CEF activism trap: even when activists win, sponsor families frequently respond with managed distribution policies, modest tender offers (10-15% of shares at 98% NAV), or merger into a larger sister fund — none of which fully closes the discount. Voter turnout in retail-heavy CEFs leans incumbent — BNY's WHITE proxy campaign mobilizes large legacy retail holders who reflexively support the manager. Muni CEFs face additional headwinds: rising rates compress NAV directly (long duration), leverage costs tied to SIFMA/short rates, and a wave of supply from competing CEFs adopting their own discount measures. Even a successful Saba outcome may only deliver a 4-5% NAV-tied tender, not a full liquidation.

The bull case is that Saba has won repeatedly. The DSM/DMB sister-fund campaigns add to the pressure, and BNY Mellon historically responds rationally to activist heat (versus more aggressive defense at BlackRock). A merger of LEO with sister muni funds, an open-ending vote, or a significant tender at 99% NAV would deliver mid-single-digit NAV uplift on top of the underlying coupon return. Holding period is short (annual meeting expected mid-2026), the carry is tax-exempt, and the downside in a "Saba loses" scenario is just owning a yielding muni CEF at a 7-8% discount — capital is preserved.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
LEO's bylaws set the annual meeting record date and proxy mechanics — the PREC14A sets up the proxy contest formally. The fund's leverage is delivered via Variable Rate Demand Preferred (VRDP) shares, which are held by institutional money market buyers. Any tender or merger structure must navigate VRDP holders' rights — a wrinkle that makes hostile open-ending difficult and explains why BNY may settle with a merger into a larger family fund rather than a tender. Saba's 5%+ stake (filed Dec 30, 2025) plus the sister-fund pressure at DSM creates effectively coordinated capital — voting concentration that makes a Kazarian win plausible if retail turnout is weak.
