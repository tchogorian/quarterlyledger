# {NUMBER}. PEO — Adams Natural Resources Fund, Inc.
## CEF DISCOUNT ACTIVISM | -18.1% Discount to NAV | Score: 58/100

### THE SETUP

Adams Natural Resources Fund (PEO) is a $868 million closed-end fund focused on the energy sector — large-cap oil & gas, pipelines, and oilfield services. The fund has traded at a chronic 15-20% discount to net asset value despite a 0.62% expense ratio that is genuinely competitive for a specialized sector CEF. As of the most recent NAV publication, shares trade at $25.61 against an NAV of $31.28 — an 18.1% discount.

On February 17, 2026, Saba Capital (Boaz Weinstein's discount-arbitrage and CEF activist platform) filed a Schedule 13D/A disclosing an 8.31% stake — 2.285 million shares of the 27.5 million outstanding. This is Saba's signature playbook: take a 5-10% position in a chronically-discounted CEF, push for tender offers, share buybacks, conversion to open-end structure, or board changes that force the discount to narrow.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Saba's track record matters here. Over the past five years, Saba has run successful discount-management campaigns at a long list of targets — Templeton funds, BlackRock CEFs, Nuveen muni funds, and most notably, several of the BlackRock equity CEFs that ultimately announced tender offers at 95-99% of NAV. The pattern is consistent: file 13D, escalate at the annual meeting, and either win a tender or convert the structure. Even partial wins (say a 15% tender at 98% of NAV) generate immediate IRR for activists and hangers-on.

PEO has some quirks that complicate the playbook. Adams Funds (which also runs PEO's sister fund ADX) is one of the oldest closed-end fund sponsors in the U.S. — founded in 1929 — and the board has historically resisted activist pressure. The fund has a managed distribution policy targeting 6%+ annual distribution from NAV, and it has done modest opportunistic buybacks. These are tools to fight discount widening, but clearly they have not been sufficient: the discount has persisted at 15%+ for years.

The Q1 2026 NAV total return was reportedly 32.3% on the back of the 2025-26 oil rally. Yet the discount widened rather than narrowed during this period — a classic CEF dynamic where retail flows lag fundamental performance. This is exactly the setup Saba targets: strong sector performance, persistent discount, demonstrably mispriced.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

CEF discount activism has an asymmetric payoff structure that screens miss. The downside is essentially "earn the underlying NAV return" because the fund's holdings are public-market energy stocks. The upside is the discount-narrowing event — a tender at 95% of NAV would generate ~13% one-time gain on top of NAV moves. Combined with a 6%+ distribution yield, the all-in compounded return should beat the underlying energy index even without a Saba win.

Important caveat: Adams Funds has a long-tenured board with deep institutional relationships, and they have fought off discount activism before. This is not a soft target like a small Templeton fund. Saba may need to run a multi-year campaign or accept partial concessions.

- Saba 13D escalation amendments
