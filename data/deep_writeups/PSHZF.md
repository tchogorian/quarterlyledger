# {NUMBER}. PSHZF — Pershing Square Holdings, Ltd.
## HOLDCO — NAV DISCOUNT | 24% DISCOUNT | Score: 78/100

### THE SETUP

Pershing Square Holdings is Bill Ackman's London/Amsterdam-listed permanent-capital vehicle holding a concentrated portfolio of 8–10 high-conviction US large-caps. PSH currently trades around $59.50 versus an indicative NAV of ~$78, a 24% discount that has been the subject of explicit shareholder attack by Ackman himself. The fund delivered 20.9% NAV growth and 33.9% total shareholder return in 2025; net assets exceed $15.5B. Repurchases totaled ~$370M in 2025 ($1.8B cumulative since the program began), narrowing the discount from a previous ~31% to today's 24%.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Pershing Square's portfolio reads like a concentrated bet on franchise quality at scale: Universal Music Group (the dominant catalog-rich music major), Restaurant Brands International (Tim Hortons / Burger King / Popeyes), Chipotle, Hilton, Howard Hughes Holdings, Brookfield, Alphabet (added recently), and a handful of others. This is not a hedge fund book — it's a long-only concentrated equity portfolio with ~5–10 names making up substantially all exposure. Returns over the 2018–2025 period have meaningfully outpaced the S&P 500 net of fees.

The discount exists because of structural friction: (1) the closed-end fund wrapper carries a perpetual stigma; (2) management fees are ~1.5% plus a 16% performance fee above hurdle, which is genuine drag; (3) US investors face ETF/ADR ineligibility — most must buy the OTC PSHZF ticker, which is illiquid; (4) Ackman key-man risk (the entire investment process is his); and (5) UMG concentration concerns (UMG alone is a top-3 position).

Ackman's response to the discount has been three-pronged. First, aggressive at-NAV buybacks — every share repurchased at 76 cents on the dollar mechanically grows NAV per share for remaining holders. Second, a planned US IPO of "Pershing Square USA" (PSUS), creating a US-listed liquid version that should cannibalize discount tourism into PSH. Third, ongoing public commentary and shareholder communications that explicitly target discount compression as a measurable goal.

The math is compelling: if you believe the underlying portfolio compounds at 10–12% annually (in line with Ackman's track record), and the discount narrows from 24% to 15% over 3 years, you get the underlying portfolio return plus a one-time ~10% re-rating gain. If the discount closes entirely (unlikely), the upside is closer to ~30% pure re-rating. The downside scenario is that the discount stays sticky — but at 24% you're essentially buying high-quality franchises at a 24% bid.

The pushback: closed-end fund discounts are notoriously persistent. Greenlight Capital Re, Third Point Reinsurance, and other Ackman-adjacent vehicles have shown that concentrated-manager wrappers tend to stay discounted regardless of performance. The PSUS IPO is now multiple years delayed.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

A screen sees a foreign ADR with high fees and concentrated equity. It does not see: the portfolio composition (which is publicly disclosed via SEC filings and reads like a quality screen), the buyback cadence, Ackman's explicit discount-compression mandate, or the math of buying $1 of UMG/RBI/Chipotle/Hilton at 76 cents. Most importantly, a screen does not see PSH's permanent-capital structure — which is what allows Ackman to run a long-duration, concentrated book without LP redemption pressure.
