# {NUMBER}. KW — Kennedy Wilson Holdings, Inc.
## TAKE-PRIVATE | 0.1% gross spread | Score: 64/100

### THE SETUP
Kennedy Wilson, the global real estate operator and investment manager, agreed on November 24, 2025 to be taken private by founder/CEO Bill McMorrow's management group with Fairfax Financial financing at $10.90/share all-cash ($1.65B). With shares effectively pegged at the deal price (gross spread ~0.1%), this is one of the tightest pending arbs on the tape — but it's a true MBO, with all the conflict-of-interest and litigation surface that implies.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Kennedy Wilson is a global real estate platform with two major segments: a $30B+ AUM investment management business (multifamily, industrial, debt platform with Fairfax) and a balance-sheet portfolio of multifamily and commercial properties primarily in the western US, UK, and Ireland. The post-pandemic period has been brutal — book value compressed by interest rate hikes on its long-duration property holdings, the dividend was cut, and the stock spent two years drifting in the $7-11 range. The November 2025 buyout values KW at about 0.85x reported book and ~$10.90 cash per share, which insiders argue reflects the realizable mark — though minority holders argue it's a lowball given the franchise value of the credit JV with Fairfax.

The bear case is straightforward MBO conflict. Bill McMorrow founded KW in 1988 and runs the take-private alongside Fairfax, a long-standing capital partner. The same insiders who priced the deal also negotiated the deal. Multiple law firms have announced investigations. Standard PE-style downside risks apply: financing contingencies, debt market shifts, Fairfax board approval flow, and a slow shareholder vote calendar pushing close into late Q2 or Q3 2026. With essentially zero spread there is no compensation for any of this risk — adverse outcomes (litigation drag, vote delay, MFW remediation) only hurt.

The bull case for buying here is opportunistic — if the spread widens to 1.5-2% on litigation noise without changing the underlying close probability, it becomes a pure low-rate yield-pickup arb backed by Fairfax's balance sheet. Fairfax (Prem Watsa) has reputational incentive not to walk; the company is operationally in better shape after 2024 dispositions; and the path to close looks routine for a real estate MBO. Today, however, the spread compensates nobody.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
This deal has a "go-shop" period and a special committee process — minority shareholder lawsuits typically force either a small bump or expanded disclosure (proxy supplements). Watch the preliminary proxy for: (1) the special committee's projections vs. management's projections (gaps signal undervaluation arguments), (2) bankers' DCF range vs. $10.90 deal price, (3) any second-bidder names in the background section. Fairfax already owns ~9% of KW common stock and has a deep credit-platform JV — meaning the buyer arguably already controls economic exposure regardless of vote outcome. The CEO and senior executives are rolling significant equity, which both confirms thesis and creates the conflict.
