The company listed on Nasdaq in 2023 and has since become a serial offender of every distress signal a public-company tracker can flag: large dilutive equity raises, repeated reverse splits, going-concern uncertainty, and persistent sub-$1 trading. On February 25, 2026, VCI announced a 1-for-60 reverse stock split effective February 27, 2026, executed solely to maintain Nasdaq's $1.00 minimum bid price requirement. Post-split market cap is approximately $30 million on a $1.50 share price.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
A 1-for-60 ratio is not an ordinary cosmetic adjustment; it is among the most extreme split ratios filed in any 12-month period and signals that the pre-split price was in the low single-digit cents. Companies that require a 60-to-1 collapse to clear the $1 bar are, with rare exception, in terminal decline. The historical base rate for sub-$50M micro-caps that execute high-ratio reverse splits is brutal: the vast majority continue to dilute, conduct further splits within 12-18 months, and ultimately delist or trade to sub-penny levels. Academic studies of the post-split window (Han 1995, Kim 2008, more recently Babenko et al.) find statistically significant negative drift in the 6-12 months after high-ratio splits, with the effect concentrated in low-quality issuers.

The optimistic case is mechanical: post-split capitulation typically forces shareholder turnover, which can produce a sharp short-term bounce as forced sellers exit and the float resets. If VCI Global's underlying advisory business actually has cash-generative substance — which is unverified — the post-split shakeout could mark a deep-value entry. The realistic case is that VCI follows the modal path: another capital raise within six months at a fresh discount, persistent dilution, and a return to sub-$1 trading that triggers another reverse split.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The Malaysian holding company structure and related-party transactions are the meaningful unmodeled risk. Foreign issuers on Nasdaq with multi-subsidiary structures have a persistently bad track record — the regulatory recourse for U.S. shareholders is limited, audit quality is variable, and the SEC has flagged enforcement priorities around small-cap foreign issuers with capital-raise patterns matching VCI's profile. The PCAOB inspection regime over Asia-based auditors remains an open issue. Additionally, the disclosed business mix — advisory, AI, fintech, renewables — is a near-perfect catalog of every "hot sector" used by micro-cap promoters to justify capital raises; it does not describe a coherent operating company.
