# {NUMBER}. RGR — Sturm, Ruger & Company, Inc.
## ACTIVIST / TENDER OFFER | $44.80 Beretta Tender / Pill in Place | Score: 78/100
### THE SETUP
Sturm, Ruger & Company is the second-largest US firearms manufacturer (behind Smith & Wesson Brands), with a roughly $670M market cap and a fortress balance sheet (no debt, meaningful net cash). On February 24, 2026, Beretta Holding S.A. — the privately-held Italian firearms group with a 9.95% stake in Ruger — filed a Schedule 13D nominating four directors for the 2026 annual meeting and signaling intent to push for capital allocation and operational change. On March 25, 2026, Beretta escalated by sending the Ruger board a letter proposing an all-cash partial tender offer at $44.80/share for up to 20.05% of outstanding shares, which would lift Beretta's economic stake to approximately 30%. The offer represents a ~20% premium to the 60-day VWAP. Ruger's poison pill currently blocks the tender; the board has acknowledged receipt but rejected pill exemption. Active proxy contest is now in motion into the 2026 AGM.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
This is one of the cleaner activist setups in the US small-cap universe heading into mid-2026. Beretta is a credible strategic acquirer — privately held, well-capitalized, with deep firearms operating expertise — not a financial activist looking for a quick flip. The $44.80 partial tender at a 20% VWAP premium is a hard floor under the equity for as long as the offer remains live, and the underlying business has real cyclical upside: firearms demand has been bottoming through 2024-2025 as the post-COVID surge unwound, gross margins have compressed to multi-year lows, and the firearm cycle is historically strongly correlated with election years. With 2026 being a presidential election cycle, normalized demand recovery is a near-term reasonable expectation regardless of the activist outcome.

The board's rejection of pill exemption is what makes the setup interesting rather than a foregone conclusion. Ruger's board is not aggressively shareholder-friendly — historically conservative governance, founder-family legacy in management ranks, and a pill that has not been put to a shareholder vote. The pill itself is the activist's primary lever: a public proxy fight to remove or amend the pill, supported by ISS/Glass Lewis, is the playbook. ISS has historically been hostile to pills that block all-cash premium offers from credible strategics, and Beretta's nominee slate is professional rather than confrontational, increasing the probability of governance support.

The realistic outcome distribution: (1) ~35% probability — negotiated settlement with Beretta gaining 1-2 board seats and a partial tender clearing at or above $44.80; (2) ~25% — full proxy victory for Beretta and tender proceeds; (3) ~25% — board prevails but is forced into a defensive capital return (special dividend or large buyback) to placate dissidents; (4) ~15% — status quo. In three of four scenarios the equity re-rates meaningfully above current $42.21, with the bear case being a slow grind back toward $38-40 as the activist premium drains. Capital return is essentially guaranteed in some form — the only question is whether you're paid with a tender, a dividend, or accumulated buyback.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
A screen sees a flat-earnings firearms company with a decent dividend yield and goes no further. What's invisible: (1) the activist tender at $44.80 is a hard floor with explicit cash backing from a strategic, not a hedge fund; (2) the firearms cycle is historically election-correlated and 2026 is a presidential year — normalized demand recovery is a baseline 2H 2026 catalyst irrespective of activism; (3) Ruger's no-debt balance sheet means even a defensive special dividend of $5-7/share is easily fundable from cash; (4) ISS/Glass Lewis precedent strongly favors removing pills that block all-cash premium offers from strategic buyers, which materially raises the probability of activist victory at the AGM vote.
