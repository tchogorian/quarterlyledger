# {NUMBER}. KORE — KORE Group Holdings, Inc.
## TAKE-PRIVATE PENDING | Searchlight + Abry LBO at $9.25; close Q2/Q3 2026 | Score: 55/100

### THE SETUP
KORE Group trades at $9.14 against a $9.25 cash take-private offer from Searchlight Capital Partners and Abry Partners, a ~1.2% gross spread on a deal announced February 26, 2026. The unusual feature: the buyer group already controls the company — Abry owns ~28% of common and Searchlight holds Series A-1 preferred — making this a controller-led LBO with an inherent conflict-of-interest dynamic. The $9.25 price represents a ~132% premium to the unaffected stock, but the structure creates real near-term gates: special-committee fairness, CFIUS review for an IoT connectivity provider, and the Kaskela shareholder challenge.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
KORE Group Holdings is a global IoT connectivity-and-services provider — managed cellular connectivity (eSIM/SIM provisioning across 550+ carrier networks), connectivity-as-a-service software, and IoT solutions across health, fleet, asset tracking, communication services, and industrial IoT. Post its 2021 SPAC de-listing, the company struggled with debt, integration issues from acquisitions (Twilio's IoT business, BMP), and a deeply compressed equity value — the stock traded below $4 at the unaffected date. The Searchlight + Abry take-private at $9.25 ends a multi-year public-market saga and reflects the buyer group's belief that KORE's operating turnaround is best executed in private.

The bear case: this is a controller-conflict LBO, which historically attracts shareholder litigation and can result in price bumps or, in rare cases, deal collapse if a special committee fails to satisfy entire-fairness review. The Kaskela law firm has already announced an investigation, and a 13E-3 filed April 15, 2026 (the going-private form required for affiliate transactions) means heightened SEC scrutiny. CFIUS review for an IoT connectivity provider with international exposure is a genuine risk — IoT cellular metadata is the kind of cross-border data flow that has triggered CFIUS conditions or blocks in past deals. Operationally, KORE's adjusted EBITDA has been inconsistent and customer concentration in connectivity-as-a-service is real.

The bull case: at a 1.2% gross spread, the implied annualized return depends on close timing — Q2 close yields high-single-digit annualized, Q3 close mid-single-digit. The buyer group already controls the company, eliminating financing risk and shareholder-vote risk in any meaningful sense (with proxy advisor support and the affiliate ownership stack, vote approval is highly likely). CFIUS reviews for businesses with no Chinese ownership and US-based connectivity have generally cleared, though with possible NSAs. The Kaskela challenge typically results in supplemental disclosures rather than price bumps. Risk/reward is acceptable for a small position; the principal uncertainty is timing, not deal certainty.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 13E-3 filing details the special committee process, including financial advisor (typically a major bank) and the precedent transactions used in the fairness opinion — these documents typically reveal that the unaffected price had been pressured by below-the-line costs that a private owner can rationalize. The proxy will disclose go-shop provisions (if any) and matching rights — for affiliate-led deals, go-shops are common but rarely produce topping bids since affiliates have right-of-first-refusal mechanics. CFIUS-related risk factors are explicitly called out in the merger agreement, with carve-outs for required mitigation measures (data localization, US-citizen board representation). The Searchlight Series A-1 preferred conversion mechanics (disclosed in prior 10-K Note on Capital Structure) create an additional consideration layer that sophisticated arbs price into the spread.
