# {NUMBER}. WBS — Webster Financial Corporation
## M&A ARB | Deal Value $75.59/sh ($12.3B) | Score: 62/100

### THE SETUP
On February 3, 2026, Banco Santander, S.A. (SAN) announced a definitive agreement to acquire Webster Financial Corporation for approximately $12.3 billion, valuing WBS at $75.59 per share. Consideration is mixed: $48.75 cash plus 2.0548 Santander ADS per WBS share, putting the deal at roughly 65% cash / 35% stock. The combination expands Santander's US commercial-banking footprint materially, layering Webster's Northeast middle-market book and HSA Bank deposit franchise onto Santander's existing US platform. With WBS trading at $73.37, the gross spread is ~3.0%; on an expected Q4 2026 close, that annualizes to roughly 5.4% — a workable risk-arb spread, though not the easy money the headline implies.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
This is fundamentally a regulatory-risk arb, not a financing or shareholder-vote arb. Bank M&A approval pipelines have shortened under the current regulatory regime, but a foreign-bank acquisition of a top-25 US commercial bank brings additional friction: the Federal Reserve and OCC must bless the transaction, CFIUS will review the foreign-acquirer angle, and Santander itself must clear EU regulatory and shareholder hurdles. None of these are blocking in our base case — Santander already operates a sizable US bank (Santander Bank N.A.) and has a clean US regulatory record — but each adds time, and time is what compresses the IRR. The stock-portion exposure is also non-trivial: 2.0548 SAN ADS per WBS share means a 10% drop in Santander stock between announcement and close would reduce WBS's deal value by roughly $1.20-1.30 per share, narrowing the spread or pushing it negative depending on entry. Sophisticated arbs typically hedge this by shorting SAN against the long WBS, which is straightforward but adds borrow cost.

The break risk is asymmetric: an outright block is unlikely given the strategic rationale and Santander's existing US operations, but a regulatory-driven "extract concessions" delay (additional capital requirements, divestiture demands, community-benefits commitments) is plausible and would push close into early-to-mid 2027. The fundamental downside if the deal breaks is the stand-alone WBS franchise value — a quality regional bank with a strong HSA deposit book — which is reasonably valued in the high $50s to low $60s, implying ~15-20% downside in a break scenario versus ~3% upside if it closes on schedule. Risk/reward is acceptable but not exceptional.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Bank-merger arb is not a quantitative game — the spread is set efficiently within hours of announcement. The edge here is qualitative reads on regulatory disposition (Santander's existing relationship with US regulators is strong, which is underappreciated), interest-rate path (a steep cut cycle would re-rate the stock portion), and Santander's strategic commitment (the company has explicitly identified US commercial banking as a core growth pillar, making walk-away unlikely). HSA Bank deposit value as a strategic asset to Santander is also under-modeled by most arb desks.

- CFIUS clearance
- EU regulatory clearance for Santander
- Final close (Q4 2026 target)
