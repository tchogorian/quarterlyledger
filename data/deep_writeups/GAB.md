### ANALYST SUMMARY
The Gabelli Equity Trust (NYSE: GAB) is a closed-end equity fund advised by Gabelli Funds, GAMCO's flagship equity vehicle. Like most Gabelli CEFs it has historically traded at a modest discount to NAV, which Mario Gabelli routinely monetizes for shareholders by issuing transferable rights — a recurring "dilute the absentee, reward the participant" mechanism that has now become formulaic. The current offering, originally priced at $5.50, was repriced to $5.00 on March 31, 2026 and the expiration extended to April 21, 2026. With the stock at $5.20, the rights have intrinsic value but the math is sensitive to the final NAV strike and the oversubscription allocation.

The bear case is structural: every GAB rights offering is dilutive to NAV per share when the subscription price is below NAV, full stop. Non-subscribers see their per-share NAV decline. Distribution coverage already relies on capital, and the 10% managed-distribution policy is a return-of-capital construct, not an earnings yield. Furthermore the discount-to-NAV in CEF land is a permanent feature — not a closing arbitrage — so the rights' theoretical value can leak away if the discount widens after issuance. The repricing itself signals weak demand at $5.50 and suggests the rights cleared cheaply.

The bull case is mechanical, not speculative. Holders who oversubscribe acquire shares at $5.00 plus the cost of additional rights (often nominal). If oversubscription allocations are filled, participants effectively buy NAV-positive incremental shares because oversubscribed shares are awarded pro rata at the subscription price. The catalyst is the April 21 expiration and the post-expiration rebalancing flow. Gabelli's own large stake means the offering is unlikely to be cancelled, and Gabelli historically backstops with insider participation.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The N-CSR filing makes clear this is a managed-distribution-policy fund with a 10% annualized payout, much of which has historically been return of capital. A screen showing "10% yield" on GAB is misleading because part of that yield is your own principal coming back. The rights offering also resets the share count meaningfully — historically GAB's RO's expand the share base 5-10%, which moves NAV per share lower and dilutes the dividend pool unless distributions are held flat in dollar terms. Read the prospectus carefully: oversubscription pro-ration formulas vary, and the fee load (dealer manager fees, soliciting fees) can absorb 3-4% of the offering proceeds.
