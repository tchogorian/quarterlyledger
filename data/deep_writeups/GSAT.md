### ANALYST SUMMARY
Globalstar operates a low-earth-orbit satellite constellation providing mobile satellite services, IoT data, and SOS connectivity — best known publicly as the infrastructure behind Apple's Emergency SOS via satellite on iPhone (under a multi-year capacity reservation contract). The company also holds Band 53/n53 terrestrial spectrum in the U.S. that has long been the speculative valuation kicker. Amazon's offer at $90/share validates both the satellite-IoT/MSS business and the spectrum, and aligns with Amazon's Project Kuiper buildout — vertically integrating ground capacity, spectrum optionality, and AWS cloud destinations for satellite IoT data.

The bear case is regulatory. The FCC must approve the transfer of MSS spectrum licenses; SpaceX (a direct competitor in satellite connectivity) and other incumbents have a clear incentive to file oppositions and drag the proceeding. CFIUS will review satellite infrastructure transfers as a national-security matter; antitrust authorities (FTC and EU Commission) may scrutinize Amazon's vertical integration into the satellite-cloud stack. Deal terms reportedly include satellite-deployment milestones; a delay could trigger MAC discussions. The Apple capacity contract was a key value driver — Apple's response to Amazon owning the underlying capacity provider is an unmodeled wildcard.

The bull case is that Amazon has both the patience and the political capital to push the deal through. The 58% pre-approval block effectively eliminates shareholder-vote risk. The mixed cash/stock structure (capped collar) gives flexibility for tax-sensitive holders and reduces financing burden. At a $90 print, GSAT longs from the pre-Apple-rumor $1-2 range are looking at a generational outcome; from $80 today the trade is a clean ~12-15% annualized arb with optionality on a topping bid (unlikely given Amazon's strategic premium, but non-zero).

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The merger-agreement docs (S-4 and 14D, when filed) will disclose the precise satellite-deployment milestone covenants, the Apple-contract change-of-control mechanics, and the spectrum-license-transfer carve-outs — none of which a generic M&A spread screen captures. The Band n53 spectrum value is carried at historical cost on the balance sheet but has been independently valued at multi-billion levels by sell-side analysts. Globalstar's existing prepaid capacity reservation from Apple represents booked but undelivered revenue that flows through deferred-revenue accounts, distorting reported revenue trajectory. CFIUS mitigation agreements — common in satellite M&A — could include U.S.-government data-routing carve-outs that affect deal value but not deal closure.
