# {NUMBER}. HHH — Howard Hughes Holdings Inc.
## HOLDCO | 31% NAV Discount | Score: 81/100

### THE SETUP
Howard Hughes trades at $65.94 against a self-published indicative NAV of ~$95 — a 31% discount — and Boyar Value Group pegs intrinsic NAV at $128. Bill Ackman's Pershing Square just put $900M of fresh equity into HHH (May 2025) at higher prices and installed himself as Executive Chairman to convert this 120,000-acre master-planned-community land bank into a "Berkshire of real estate plus insurance," with the $2.1B Vantage Insurance acquisition closing Q2 2026.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Howard Hughes Holdings owns five elite master-planned communities — Summerlin (Las Vegas), The Woodlands and Bridgeland (Houston), Teravalis (Phoenix), and Columbia (Maryland) — totaling ~120,000 residual acres being slowly monetized over decades. Layered on top is a Class A operating portfolio (office, retail, multifamily, hospitality), the Seaport district in NYC (now substantially separated/restructured to remove the historical drag), and ~$1.2B of cash. The company's real estate segments throw off recurring NOI; the MPCs throw off periodic land sales whose timing depends on housing-cycle absorption rates and homebuilder demand. This is a 50-year asset book trapped inside a single C-corp wrapper that the public market has historically valued at a 25-40% discount to appraised value.

The bear case is the discount itself. NAV discounts persist because the public market doesn't trust holdco accounting, doesn't trust land-value-now versus land-cash-flow-30-years-out, and doesn't trust complexity. The Seaport overhang, while now mostly fixed, cost the company hundreds of millions before its restructuring. Land-sales pace depends entirely on mortgage-rate trajectory; persistent 6.5%+ mortgages strangle homebuilder absorption and stretch your monetization timeline. Add the May 2025 "takeover lite" — Ackman did not actually buy out minority holders despite an aborted January 2025 proposal — and minority holders rightly distrust the alignment story. Insurance deals (Vantage at 1.5x BV, $2.1B) can destroy capital if loss-reserve assumptions prove wrong; insurance is not a place to learn on the job.

The bull case is the Ackman conversion thesis. Pershing put in $900M at materially higher than current prices, plus up to $1B preferred to fund Vantage. The vehicle now has insurance float layered onto a self-funded land bank — a structurally rare combination. Ackman is Executive Chairman with full operational authority. If Vantage generates even modest underwriting profitability, the float becomes permanent low-cost capital to fund both internal MPC development and external acquisitions. Boyar's $128 NAV implies ~95% upside; even closing half the discount yields a 40%+ return.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 10-K's MPC segment carries land at historical cost — Summerlin's basis dates to a 1996 Rouse-era acquisition. Comparable raw acreage in the Las Vegas valley sells today at multiples of book value the screen can't surface. The 2024 10-K disclosure on remaining sellable acres shows residual saleable land in Summerlin and Bridgeland alone supports decades of cash flow. The Vantage transaction is being funded with up to $1B of perpetual preferred from PSH at a fixed coupon — meaning HHH gets insurance float without diluting common equity at today's depressed multiple. That preferred-funded structure is the alpha screens miss: Ackman is using PSH balance sheet capacity, not HHH common, to grow.
