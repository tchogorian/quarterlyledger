### ANALYST SUMMARY

The AES Corporation (NYSE: AES) is a global utility/IPP with 2025 total revenue of $12.23B, four reportable segments: Renewables ($2.9B revenue, $503M operating margin), Utilities ($4.1B / $635M), Energy Infrastructure ($5.4B / $901M), and New Energy Technologies ($1M / -$11M). US footprint anchored by AES Indiana (regulated utility, $5.5B rate base, 9.75% allowed ROE per partial settlement), AES Ohio (T&D-only utility), plus 4 utilities in El Salvador. The 10-K (filed March 2, 2026) discloses the merger agreement entered March 1, 2026 with Horizon Parent, L.P. — a Delaware LP controlled by GIP and EQT Infrastructure VI funds. Total operating margin was $2,211M in 2025, down from $2,314M in 2024, with the Energy Infrastructure SBU declining 27% (-$332M) due to portfolio rationalization (Chile Renovables sale to GIP partner, AES Renewable Holdings tax-equity sales).

The bear case is regulatory complexity. The deal requires: FERC approval (US wholesale energy markets); state PUC approvals in Indiana (IURC) and Ohio (PUCO); HSR antitrust review; and crucially, CFIUS review given Qatar Investment Authority (sovereign wealth) and presumably a national-security review of foreign control over US critical infrastructure. International approvals span El Salvador, Chile, Brazil, Colombia, Dominican Republic, Mexico, Argentina, Panama, Puerto Rico — each with separate energy ministries and competition authorities. Any single jurisdiction can demand divestitures or impose conditions. Beyond regulatory: energy-policy uncertainty under the Trump 2.0 administration (renewables tax credit repeal/clawback risk via OBBBA) impacts AES's Renewables SBU economics post-close, but the deal price is fixed at $15. No financing risk because the consortium pre-funded 100% equity.

The bull case is the deal closes near-as-promised at $15.00 cash. Consortium economics: GIP paid $12.5B for 49% of Texas-power-IPP Vistra in 2024; QIA invested $10B+ in US energy infrastructure historically; CalPERS as LP de-risks the deal politically (pension fund alignment with US infrastructure). 100% equity financing eliminates debt-market disruption risk that killed several 2022–2023 take-privates. The 30-day VWAP premium pre-rumor was 40.3%, signaling strong board endorsement and likely shareholder approval. At 4.4% annualized return through Q1 2027 close, this is a respectable spread for a deal with low completion risk and well-capitalized buyers — equivalent yield to investment-grade corporate bonds with potential for upside if close accelerates.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 10-K Subsequent Events note details that "Parent is controlled by investment vehicles affiliated with one or more funds, accounts or other entities managed or advised by Global Infrastructure Management, LLC and the EQT Infrastructure VI fund" — meaning the formal control entity excludes CalPERS/QIA, who are LPs in the GIP/EQT vehicles rather than direct co-investors at the legal-control level. This may help on CFIUS framing (sovereign-LP investment via fund-of-funds is more digestible than direct sovereign acquisition). AES's existing partnership with GIP at AES Pacifico/Chile Renovables (49% sold to GIP August 2025 for $77M plus prior tranches) created the strategic relationship that led to this deal. AES Indiana's June 3, 2025 rate case settlement (return on common equity 9.75%, $5.5B rate base, no further rate increases until next case) provides locked-in cash flow visibility for the post-close LBO economics. The 6.50% convertible senior notes were settled in 2025 for $1.0B cash, simplifying the capital stack ahead of the deal.
