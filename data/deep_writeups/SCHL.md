# {NUMBER}. SCHL — Scholastic Corporation
## SELF-TENDER | $36-$40 Modified Dutch Auction expires 4/20/26 | Score: 64/100

### THE SETUP
On March 23, 2026, Scholastic launched a Modified Dutch Auction issuer tender offer to repurchase up to $200M of common stock at a price between $36.00 and $40.00 per share. The offer expires April 20, 2026 at 5:00 PM New York time. At the $40 ceiling the buyback retires roughly 5.0M shares, or approximately 25% of public float (insiders, including the Robinson family trusts and the Robinson Foundation, are not tendering). With the stock pinned at ~$39.77, the spread to the $40 cap is essentially zero — the market is telling you the auction will clear at the top of the range.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Scholastic is the children's-publishing legacy franchise: the Book Fairs and Book Clubs distribution into US schools, the Education Solutions textbook business, and a trade-publishing list anchored on Harry Potter, Captain Underpants, Hunger Games, and Dog Man. Free cash flow has been compressed by post-COVID school spending normalization, classroom-time disruptions, and a soft trade-publishing cycle, but the core Book Fairs business — which is structurally a school-fundraising franchise with no real competitor — remains intact. The strategic move into Wizarding World content via the 9 Story Media acquisition added IP optionality on the cost of cyclically depressed earnings.

The mechanics of the trade are straightforward. A Modified Dutch is the cleanest form of issuer return: shareholders elect to tender at any price within the band, and the company pays the single lowest price needed to clear $200M, paying that price to all accepted shares. The current setup has three implications. First, with the stock at $39.77 versus a $40 ceiling, the implied probability of clearing at $40 is overwhelming — proration risk is real but not terminal. Second, insiders explicitly stated they are not tendering, which signals confidence in fundamental value above $40 and shrinks the available supply. Third, even if you do not tender, the float reduction of ~25% post-deal is a material EPS lever (low-20s percent boost on share count alone, before any margin recovery).

The honest risks: oversubscription means proration. If tenders exceed $200M / $40 = 5.0M shares (likely), each shareholder who tenders gets only their pro-rata fill at $40 and the rest stays at market — which on the day after expiration could trade lower as the technical bid disappears. The "buyback floor at $36" thesis only holds if you actually tender at $36-$37 (low end), which is unattractive given the current quote. And Scholastic's underlying earnings power remains the open question — Book Fairs is a fine business but trade publishing has been mediocre and Education Solutions has not turned the corner.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
A spread screen will flag this as 0.6% gross / not annualized. It misses two things. First, the real trade is post-tender: the float reduction creates a smaller, illiquid stock with the controlling family owning a higher share — possibly a setup for a future take-private at a better premium. Second, insider non-tendering is a tell. The Robinson family trusts have been net buyers of family agency, not sellers, since John Robinson's death in 2021 — they are accumulating influence, not exiting.

### CATALYSTS
- **Tender results announced April 21, 2026:** clearing price and proration factor.
- **Q4 FY2026 earnings (mid-July 2026):** first post-tender print with reduced share count and updated guidance on Book Fairs and trade publishing.
- **Possible follow-on capital return or family-led take-private (12-24 month tail):** post-tender float reduction lowers the cost of any subsequent buyout.
