# {NUMBER}. SIRI — Sirius XM Holdings Inc.
## SPINOFF — FORCED SELLING | <8x FCF, ~5% yield, Berkshire ~35% | Score: 75/100

### THE SETUP
On September 9, 2024, Liberty Media completed a complex two-step transaction: Liberty's SiriusXM tracking stock (LSXMA/K) was split off from Liberty Media and immediately combined with the public Sirius XM minority stake into a single new public company — "New Sirius" — trading under the existing SIRI ticker with a 1-for-10 reverse stock split. The mechanics were tax-efficient for Liberty holders but messy from a market-microstructure perspective: tracking-stock holders received the new SIRI on a tax-free basis (creating natural sellers who never wanted to own the standalone), while the legacy public SIRI minority received a small fractional position post-conversion. The result was extended forced-selling pressure on the new ticker that bore no relationship to the underlying business. Today SIRI sits at roughly $25 with a market cap of ~$8.5B — and Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway has been steadily building, now owning approximately 35% of the company. That is not a typo: a single owner with full information access and no need to sell holds more than a third of the float.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Strip out the spinoff mechanics and what's left is a simple FCF compounder. Sirius XM generates ~$1.2B of annual free cash flow on an ~$8.5B market cap — roughly 14% FCF yield, or under 8x FCF — with a ~5% dividend and ongoing buyback authorization. The bear narrative is "satellite radio is being eaten by streaming," but the data has been more nuanced than the narrative: the in-vehicle attach business (where SIRI is bundled with new car deliveries) has stabilized as US auto SAAR has recovered, the self-pay subscriber base has held up better than expected, and the addition of an ad-supported tier and podcast monetization has incrementally expanded the addressable market beyond the legacy in-car satellite product. Berkshire's accumulation pattern — increasing the stake from ~32% in October 2024 to ~35% by February 2025 — is the most informative single data point: this is value-investor capital with a permanent time horizon adding to the position post-spin. Take-private optionality is real: at a 35% stake, Berkshire could in principle support a take-private at a modest premium to current levels with very little incremental capital. Even absent any strategic transaction, base-case FCF coverage of buyback + dividend at the current price implies a high single-digit per-share intrinsic return regardless of multiple expansion.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Three things. First, the post-spin float is structurally concentrated — Berkshire 35% plus other Liberty-related holders means actual public free float is much smaller than headline market cap suggests, which depresses index-fund participation and keeps the multiple compressed. Second, the secular-decline narrative obscures the fact that SIRI's core economics are subscription-recurring with high margins on each incremental subscriber dollar — the relevant question is subscriber stability, not subscriber growth, and stability is what the recent prints show. Third, the take-private math: at a 35% Berkshire stake, a take-private would only require ~65% of shares to be acquired, and the FCF generation would self-fund a meaningful portion of any LBO structure — this optionality isn't reflected in any consensus model.

### CATALYSTS
- Quarterly FCF prints and continued capital return execution
- Continued Berkshire purchases (each disclosure tightens the float)
- Subscriber stabilization in self-pay tier
- Potential strategic transaction or take-private
- Dividend raise as FCF compounds
- Ad-supported tier and podcast monetization growth

### MUNGER CHECKLIST

- Durable moat: Moderate-to-strong — exclusive in-vehicle satellite footprint, exclusive content (Howard Stern, sports), entrenched OEM relationships
- Honest, capable management: Reasonable — disciplined capital return, FCF-focused
- Bought at fair price: Yes — under 8x FCF for a stable subscription cash flow with ~5% yield is cheap by any consumer-subscription standard
- Margin of safety: Strong — Berkshire at 35% provides both validation and natural floor
- Key risks: Long-term EV/streaming substitution, content cost inflation (Stern renewal, sports rights), subscriber churn acceleration
