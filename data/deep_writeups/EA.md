### ANALYST SUMMARY
On September 29, 2025 EA agreed to be acquired by a consortium of Saudi Arabia's PIF (rolling its existing ~9.9% stake), Silver Lake, and Jared Kushner's Affinity Partners for $210 per share in cash, valuing equity at $55B. JPMorgan committed $20B of debt financing — the largest single-bank LBO debt package on record — with PIF supplying ~$36B of equity. The shareholder vote passed; the UK CMA and Chinese SAMR cleared in early March 2026. CFIUS is the residual consideration but PIF's pre-existing 9.9% stake suggests a familiar pattern. At $203.83 the gross spread is 3.03% with expected close in June 2026 (EA's fiscal Q1 FY27), implying ~18% annualized — a chunky M&A arb spread for a deal this far along the regulatory path.

The bear case for the spread is binary-but-low-probability: (1) political backlash to Saudi sovereign-wealth ownership of a marquee US gaming asset triggers fresh CFIUS scrutiny or congressional pressure — possible but management would have flagged any agency objections by now; (2) the LBO debt package — the largest ever underwritten by a single bank — wobbles if syndication coincides with credit market dislocation, though JPM has commitment letters and bridge facilities in place; (3) closing-mechanics delay pushes settlement past June 2026 and elongates the annualized math but does not impair the $210 outcome. None of these realistically threaten the take-out price.

The bull case is plain merger arb: the deal is fully financed, blessed by the largest regulator (UK), cleared by the most-feared Asian regulator (China), and the shareholder gating event has passed. PIF's 9.9% rollover meaningfully de-risks CFIUS optics. Three percent gross over ~3 months at this stage of a fully financed deal is among the highest absolute spreads in an arb book today. Position sizing should reflect the binary downside if the deal somehow re-traded (likely 30%+ drawdown to standalone ~$140 EA fair value), but the probability-weighted return is highly attractive.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
A standard arb screen captures the 3% spread but can't price the asymmetry of the residual risks. The PIF rollover is the key feature: by agreeing to roll its existing 9.9% stake, the Saudi sovereign wealth fund has materially de-risked CFIUS's foreign-control review — what would otherwise be a brand-new Saudi acquisition of a US gaming asset is, structurally, a partial roll-up of an already-disclosed foreign holder. CFIUS rarely blocks transactions that simply move an existing minority owner to majority. Equally invisible: the JPM bridge has been "in market" since announcement with no syndication snags, signaling debt markets are absorbing the package smoothly.
