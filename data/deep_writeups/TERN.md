### THE SETUP

On March 25, 2026, Merck & Co. announced a definitive agreement to acquire Terns Pharmaceuticals for $53.00 per share in cash, valuing the deal at approximately $6.7 billion. Merck filed the SC TO-T tender offer documents on April 7, 2026; the tender expires May 4, 2026 with a Q2 2026 expected close. As of mid-April 2026, TERN trades at $52.65 — a gross spread of $0.35, or ~0.66%, against a deal certain to close within ~3 weeks. Annualized that's ~4% — a low-risk merger arbitrage with strategic acquirer financing certainty.

The strategic rationale is squarely about TERN-701, Terns' lead asset — a novel allosteric BCR-ABL inhibitor in development for chronic myeloid leukemia (CML). TERN-701 has differentiated potential against the standard-of-care TKIs (imatinib, dasatinib, nilotinib, asciminib), including activity against resistance mutations. Merck has been aggressively rebuilding its hematology pipeline as Keytruda's loss-of-exclusivity countdown continues, and TERN-701 fits that strategic gap. The 31% premium to the unaffected price reflects competitive bidding dynamics (multiple acquirers reportedly engaged before Merck won).

### ANALYST SUMMARY (3p)

This is about as clean a merger-arb setup as biotech offers. Merck is investment-grade, has explicitly committed cash from balance sheet (no financing condition), and the deal is a tender-offer-plus-back-end-merger structure that closes mechanically once the tender condition (>50% of shares) is met. Pre-revenue oncology biotechs almost never face HSR/antitrust complications because there's no overlapping commercial product or competitive market share at issue. Merck's lack of a directly competing CML asset further reduces antitrust risk to near zero.

The shareholder base — biotech specialists who own TERN for the TERN-701 binary, plus event-driven funds who arrived after announcement — is highly likely to tender. Tender conditions of 50%+ of outstanding shares are routinely met on day one of acceptance for deals at premium prices to strategic acquirers. There is no go-shop period concern; the price is locked.

The remaining spread — $0.35 — pays for the residual tail risk that something genuinely surprising emerges in HSR review (extremely unlikely for a pre-revenue oncology asset), a material adverse change occurs at TERN before close (no obvious vector — the TERN-701 dataset is the asset, and trial data won't move materially in three weeks), or Merck somehow walks (essentially zero probability given strategic rationale, signed deal, and reputational cost). The 4% annualized return is below the broader merger-arb market average for risk reasons but reflects the unusually clean setup.
