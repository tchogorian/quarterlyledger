# {NUMBER}. KNF — Knife River Corporation
## SPINOFF — QUALITY-AT-DISCOUNT | MDU spin June 2023; aggregates pure-play | Score: 76/100

### THE SETUP
Knife River trades at $80.01 (~$4.5B market cap) as a focused aggregates and construction-materials business spun out of MDU Resources in June 2023 — a classic spin pattern where utility/dividend holders dumped a cyclical industrial they didn't want to own. Three years post-spin the IIJA tailwind is finally hitting state DOT award flow, the bolt-on M&A program has proven accretive, and KNF's western US pit positions translate to near-monopoly local pricing power. Strategic-acquirer interest from CRH, Vulcan, or Martin Marietta is plausible at any premium.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Knife River is an aggregates-led construction-materials company with operations across 14 western and central US states — sand, gravel, crushed stone, ready-mix concrete, asphalt, and contract paving and construction services. The business model: own irreplaceable pits and quarries close to growing population centers, take pricing as freight economics make competing material from distant sources uneconomic, and pull through ready-mix and asphalt margin on top. The 2023 MDU spin separated the volatile materials business from the regulated utility, allowing dedicated capital allocation including the "Competitive EDGE" margin program and an active M&A roll-up of regional aggregates players.

The bear case: aggregates is a cyclical business tied to non-residential construction, residential housing starts, and public infrastructure spending. A US recession would compress volumes; rising rates have already pressured private non-residential. Several bolt-on acquisitions paid full prices in a competitive M&A market — integration risk and goodwill exposure are real. Margins lag pure-play peers Vulcan and Martin Marietta, partly reflecting Knife River's higher mix of paving services (lower-margin contract work) versus pure aggregates. If the IIJA pipeline disappoints in actual award flow versus authorized funding, the bull thesis loses its key plank. Multiple is no longer obviously cheap versus comps after a ~3x post-spin re-rating.

The bull case: IIJA is a five-to-seven-year tailwind only now hitting peak award flow, with Knife River's western footprint disproportionately exposed (highway and bridge spend is concentrated in growth Sun Belt and Mountain West states). The Competitive EDGE program has delivered visible margin expansion quarter after quarter as legacy pit pricing gets aligned with replacement economics. M&A roll-up is genuinely accretive — Knife River is paying high-single-digit EBITDA multiples for pits that fit existing logistics networks, immediately accretive once integrated. Strategic-acquirer interest is real: aggregates consolidation at the CRH/Vulcan/Martin Marietta level would value KNF's assets at a meaningful premium, and the company's footprint complements all three majors' geographic gaps.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The Form 10 information statement filed for the spin laid out the geographic concentration of permitted reserves — the value isn't in trailing EBITDA, it's in decades of permitted pit life that cannot be easily replicated by competitors due to permitting friction. The 2024 10-K reserves disclosure shows weighted-average reserve life well into the 2050s for the largest pits. The Competitive EDGE program is referenced in MD&A as targeting cost-of-quality improvements and pricing optimization — the implicit margin runway is several hundred basis points over multiple years, not visible in trailing financials. M&A pace (multiple deals annually) compounds quietly: each bolt-on tucks into existing distribution and adds reserve life, but the per-share earnings effect lags reported quarters by 12-18 months.
