# {NUMBER}. TSI — TCW Strategic Income Fund
## RIGHTS OFFERING | CEF DISCOUNT ACTIVISM | Score: 62/100

### THE SETUP

TSI is a small, multi-strategy closed-end fund managed by TCW that invests across investment-grade and high-yield credit, structured product, convertibles, and equity income. Market cap is ~$280M; price ~$4.65. The fund has long traded at a persistent discount to NAV, which over the last several years has put it on the radar of the closed-end-fund activist community — Saba Capital, Bulldog Investors, and similar names that buy CEFs at discounts and pressure boards into tenders, conversions, or open-endings. TSI just completed a transferable rights offering at a $4.52 subscription price; the rights expired at 5:00 p.m. ET on March 18, 2026. The setup here is straightforward: rights offerings in CEFs nearly always create a temporary technical overhang (existing holders sell to fund subscriptions, rights traders flip), and the discount typically widens during the subscription period and then re-rates as the overhang clears, the new share count is published, and the sponsor refreshes the distribution policy on a now-larger asset base.

### ANALYST SUMMARY

The trade is a calendar-driven mean-reversion play with optional activist tailwind. Rights offerings in closed-end funds are mechanically dilutive in the short run — NAV typically prints lower the day after the offering closes because shares are issued at a discount to NAV — but they enlarge the fund's earning asset base, which can support a higher absolute distribution and, more importantly, remove the "rights overhang" that suppresses the secondary market price during the subscription window. Historical CEF rights offerings (Tortoise, Aberdeen, Nuveen, prior TCW) have followed a recognizable pattern: discount widens 100-300 bps into the deadline, then narrows over the following 60-90 days as forced sellers exit and the sponsor markets the post-offering distribution. TSI's edge is amplified by activist presence in the CEF complex — boards of small CEFs with persistent discounts increasingly face shareholder proposals for tenders, terminations, or open-endings, and a fund that just diluted holders at a discount is exactly the kind of situation that attracts a follow-on activist campaign demanding a discount-cure mechanism. Risks: TCW could deploy the new capital into lower-yielding paper, distribution coverage could deteriorate, and the broader credit market could re-price (TSI's NAV is sensitive to high-yield spreads). The fund is also small enough that liquidity constrains position sizing for institutional buyers, which is itself part of why the discount persists.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

Discount-to-NAV screens will show TSI at face value but miss two things: first, the NAV they print is stale because it reflects pre-offering share count for a few days post-close until the fund updates official NAV with the new denominator. Second, screens don't capture the activist 13D/13G ownership profile of CEFs — the names on the share register matter as much as the discount itself, because a passive holder base produces a permanent discount while an activist-heavy register produces a discount with a catalyst. Distribution coverage ratios (the fund's actual investment income versus the declared distribution) also tend to mislead in the immediate aftermath of rights offerings — newly raised capital takes weeks to be deployed, so apparent under-coverage in the first reporting period after a rights offering is mechanical, not structural. Finally, the rights themselves traded as a separate security on NYSE with their own discount/premium dynamic; rights pricing relative to the in-the-money intrinsic value carried information about the market's view of post-offering NAV.
