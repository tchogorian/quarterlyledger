### THE SETUP
On February 23, 2026, Veris Residential agreed to be acquired by an Affinius Capital-led consortium (with Vista Hill Partners) for $19.00 per share in cash, valuing the deal at roughly $3.4B including debt. VRE is a Class-A multifamily REIT focused on the Northeast, particularly New Jersey along the Hudson waterfront — Jersey City, Hoboken, Weehawken — with adjacent assets in Massachusetts. It is what's left of the old Mack-Cali after years of strategic repositioning out of office and into apartments.

The stock currently trades at $18.90 — a tight 53 bps gross spread to the $19.00 deal price, ~3.2% annualized assuming a Q2 2026 close. That spread tells you the market believes the deal closes, but is not certain enough to compress the spread below typical "merger arb yield" levels for a deal with shareholder-vote and financing risks.

### ANALYST SUMMARY (3p)

**Paragraph 1 — The deal mechanics.** Affinius is a real estate-focused alternative manager with deep multifamily experience; Vista Hill Partners is a private investor group. The financing stack is $2.08B of committed senior secured bridge debt plus equity from the consortium. The 23% premium to the unaffected price was sufficient to get the board on board and likely a majority of the holder base. The path to close involves (a) shareholder vote, (b) standard regulatory clearance — minimal antitrust friction here since this is a multifamily REIT roll-up, not a horizontal combination, and (c) the bridge-to-permanent financing execution.

**Paragraph 2 — The risk you're being paid for.** Two real risks anchor the spread. First, financing: a $2.08B bridge needs to be either drawn at close or replaced with permanent financing. If CMBS or agency multifamily debt markets seize (rate volatility, credit-spread blowout), the consortium has options but the cost goes up — and a financing renegotiation would extend the timeline. Second, shareholder vote: VRE has had a complex holder base including activist participation in prior years, and any holder concentration that views $19 as inadequate could create friction. Compared to a typical strategic-buyer deal, a PE take-private of a REIT carries marginally more financing risk and marginally less regulatory risk.

**Paragraph 3 — Why the spread is reasonable.** A 3.2% annualized spread on a deal with a committed bridge, board approval, and minimal antitrust concerns is roughly fair compensation for the residual risk. The downside if the deal breaks is a return to the unaffected ~$15.40 trading range — about 19% downside — against a 53 bps gross / 3.2% annualized upside on closing. That 36:1 risk/reward asymmetry is the standard merger-arb shape; you size accordingly. This is not a "found money" trade; it's a portfolio component for a diversified arb sleeve.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
A screen sees: REIT trading near its deal price, modest yield, no dramatic mispricing. It misses: (1) the specific financing structure ($2.08B bridge — committed but not yet termed-out), (2) the historical activist overhang on VRE that informs how the holder base will vote, (3) the seasonality of multifamily debt markets and how that interacts with a Q2 close, and (4) the comparative deal-break math vs. unaffected price (which is what you actually need to size the position properly).
