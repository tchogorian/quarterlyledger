# {NUMBER}. SFD — Smithfield Foods, Inc.
## SPINOFF — FORCED SELLING | ~10x P/E, 88% controlled by WH Group | Score: 68/100

### THE SETUP
Smithfield Foods is the largest US pork producer — vertically integrated from hog farms in North Carolina, Virginia, Utah and the Midwest through to packaged meats brands like Smithfield, Eckrich, Nathan's Famous and Farmland. The company was taken private in 2013 by Hong Kong-listed WH Group for $4.7B (then the largest Chinese acquisition of a US company). On January 28, 2025, WH Group brought Smithfield back public via a Nasdaq IPO, selling roughly 12% of the float at $20/share — below the indicated range of $23–27. The IPO repriced lower because of (a) Chinese-parent overhang, (b) softness in pork-cycle sentiment, and (c) a generally cool 2025 IPO market. WH Group still owns roughly 88% of the equity, meaning the public float is small relative to the headline $11B market cap. SFD effectively trades like an orphaned mid-cap controlled by a foreign parent — exactly the kind of post-IPO situation where forced-seller dynamics meet a clean cap table and an under-followed name.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Smithfield is one of the cleanest "carve-out + foreign parent" setups of 2025. Revenue of roughly $15.5B grew ~9.8% YoY into 2025 with record free cash flow generation, helped by stable Packaged Meats margins (the higher-quality branded segment) absorbing volatility from the commodity Fresh Pork and Hog Production segments. Trading near $28.88 implies ~10x earnings — a deep discount to consumer-staples meat peers like Tyson and Hormel, despite better organic growth and a more focused portfolio after the divestiture of European and Mexican operations. Capital allocation has been disciplined: a $1.3B Sioux Falls capacity expansion targets the highest-margin Packaged Meats output, and the recent acquisition of Nathan's Famous at roughly 12.4x EBITDA is immediately accretive and brings a high-margin licensed brand into the portfolio. Free cash flow comfortably supports both the dividend and recently-announced buyback authorization. The reflexive bear case — "it's controlled by a Chinese parent" — is real but quantifiable: every secondary sale by WH Group reduces the overhang and increases float, which in turn increases the probability of S&P MidCap inclusion (a meaningful incremental-buyer catalyst). Hog cycle inflection in 2026 — assuming USDA hog inventories continue to normalize — would compound the multiple expansion case.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Three things. First, the post-IPO float math: with only ~12% of shares public, every standard "average daily volume" or "float-adjusted market cap" screen materially understates how thin SFD trades. Index funds and many ETFs are mechanically prevented from owning it at current float, which keeps a structural buyer base on the sidelines until WH Group sells more. Second, Packaged Meats is roughly 60%+ of segment profit despite being a smaller share of revenue — most generalist screens treat SFD as commodity meat (low-multiple) when the earnings stream is much closer to a branded food company (mid-teens multiple). Third, the Nathan's Famous deal isn't just an accretive bolt-on — it signals that management can deploy capital sensibly in branded acquisitions rather than burning cash on commodity capacity, which has historically been the pork industry's failure mode.

### CATALYSTS
- Continued WH Group secondary share sales (each one increases float and reduces overhang)
- S&P MidCap 400 inclusion as float crosses the eligibility threshold
- Nathan's Famous deal close and integration synergies
- Hog cycle inflection — USDA pork inventories already normalizing
- Continued buyback execution under recently-authorized program
- 2026 dividend raise as FCF compounds
