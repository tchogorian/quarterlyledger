# {NUMBER}. WSR — Whitestone REIT
## ACTIVIST | $19.00 ARES TAKE-PRIVATE PENDING | Score: 72/100

### THE SETUP
Whitestone REIT is a Houston-headquartered Sun Belt strip-center REIT that has spent the better part of a decade trading at a persistent NAV discount. Founder and former CEO/Chairman James C. Mastandrea — ousted by the board in 2022 — re-emerged on January 5, 2026 with a PREC14A nomination to replace the entire board with six independent candidates and force a strategic review. His central complaint: the portfolio shrank from 53 to 50 properties since 2021, G&A stayed bloated, and the stock chronically traded below private-market value of the grocery-anchored Sun Belt convenience retail.

The campaign worked — but not in the way Mastandrea expected. On April 9, 2026, Whitestone announced an agreement to be acquired by Ares Management for $19.00 per share in cash, valuing the company at roughly $1.7 billion. The stock now sits at $18.95, a 0.26% gross spread (~0.79% annualized assuming a Q3 close). The activist campaign collapsed into a take-private — exactly the kind of forced-sale outcome the proxy was designed to extract.

### ANALYST SUMMARY (3p)
**(1) The activist forced the sale.** Mastandrea's full-board replacement campaign was credible because he was the founder, knew every property, and named a real slate of six nominees in March 2026. ISS would almost certainly have leaned his way given the multi-year underperformance. Faced with a likely loss at the May annual meeting, the board hired a banker, ran a process, and accepted Ares' bid. This is the textbook outcome activist arb investors are paid to anticipate.

**(2) The arb spread is now microscopic.** At $18.95 vs. $19.00, you're looking at 0.26% gross / 0.79% annualized. That's not really an arb trade anymore — it's a deposit substitute with deal-break risk. Financial-sponsor REIT take-privates have very high closure rates (vote risk only; minimal antitrust). The spread reflects market consensus that this closes Q3 2026.

**(3) The remaining question is bump probability.** Mastandrea has not endorsed the Ares deal publicly as of mid-April. If he calculates that $19 undersells the Sun Belt grocery-anchored portfolio (which arguably trades at sub-6 cap rates in private markets), he can vote NO and agitate for a topping bid. Logical strategics: Curbline Properties (the Site Centers spin focused on convenience retail), Brookfield, Slate Grocery, Phillips Edison. A 5-10% bump to ~$20-$20.50 is plausible but not base case.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Screens see the 0.26% spread and discard the name. What they miss: Mastandrea's 2.3% stake plus moral authority gives him outsized influence on the vote. If he files a DFAN14A opposing the deal, the merger arb community will treat the vote as live and the spread will widen — creating either a buying opportunity (if you trust the strategic backstop) or a clean exit (if you don't). The asymmetry here isn't in the spread; it's in the option value embedded in the founder's vote.

The other invisible layer: Sun Belt grocery-anchored strip is the most-bid asset class in commercial real estate right now. Curbline IPO'd in late 2024 specifically to roll up these assets. A topping bid is genuinely possible, not just a theoretical hope.

- **Moat:** Sun Belt grocery-anchored strip has structural tailwinds (population migration, e-commerce-resistant tenants). The portfolio itself has a moat; Whitestone the corporate entity didn't. NEUTRAL.

**Bottom line:** The activist thesis already played out. WSR is now a low-spread, high-certainty REIT take-private with embedded option value if Mastandrea opposes the deal or a topping bidder emerges. Reasonable holding for arb books; not a fundamental long anymore.
