# {NUMBER}. ING — ING Groep N.V.

### THE SETUP
ING Groep is a Dutch banking giant trading at a persistent discount to US peers despite cleaner capital ratios, an above-15% CET1 buffer, and an aggressive multi-year buyback-plus-dividend program. Not a sub-cash play (deposits are liabilities, not free cash) — but an under-owned European compounder distributing roughly its earnings yield back to holders annually.

### ANALYST SUMMARY
ING Groep N.V. is the Netherlands' largest bank, with a sprawling European retail and wholesale franchise spanning the Benelux core, Germany (consumer/digital), Poland, and selective wholesale banking globally. The post-financial-crisis reorganization shed insurance (NN Group spinoff, 2014) and US banking (ING Direct sold to Capital One) to leave a focused European universal bank. Capital ratios sit comfortably above ECB minima, with CET1 typically running 14-15% — well above the binding requirement — funding an explicit "distribute excess capital" framework via dividends plus serial buybacks. EUR retail franchise generates stable net interest income, with some duration sensitivity to ECB rate path.

The bear case is European bank structural: NIM compression as ECB cuts proceed through 2026, persistent regulatory drag (DGS contributions, single resolution fund levies, the Basel IV phase-in), and chronically below-cost-of-equity returns on the wholesale book. Dutch and German residential mortgage exposure carries housing-market sensitivity. Geopolitical risk is real — ING has notable wholesale exposure to commodity trade finance and emerging markets where credit cycles can turn quickly. The stock's persistent discount to US peers reflects genuine return-on-tangible-equity gap (ING typically 12-13% vs. JPM/BAC mid-teens), not just sentiment. Anti-money-laundering history (2018 Dutch settlement) is a tail risk that occasionally resurfaces.

The bull case is total-shareholder-yield arithmetic. ING's framework targets distribution of excess capital above a CET1 buffer — every year that earnings exceed buffer demands, the difference flows back via dividends and buybacks. At trough European bank multiples (~7-8x earnings, ~0.9-1.1x tangible book), the implied capital-return yield exceeds 10% annually for a credible multi-year horizon. ECB rate cuts hurt asset-side yield but help fee income and reduce funding costs; net NIM impact is modest and offset by volume. The German consumer franchise (ING-DiBa) is a secularly growing digital bank with sticky deposits. M&A optionality exists for any consolidation in European retail banking — ING is plausibly both predator and prey.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The capital framework specifics matter: ING's "CET1 ratio above ~12.5% target" creates a mechanical excess-capital release, and management has been disciplined about returning rather than reinvesting. Read the quarterly capital ratio walk to see the math. The TCFD/climate disclosure includes loan-book carbon intensity that increasingly drives both regulatory capital and reputational risk. Key footnote: ING's structural rate hedges (replicating portfolio) smooth NIM through cycles — actual NIM sensitivity to ECB rates is much lower than naive duration-of-equity calculations suggest. Also note: Dutch withholding tax on dividends affects net yield for US holders (15% treaty rate) — relevant for after-tax math.
