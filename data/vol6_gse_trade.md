# THE FANNIE / FREDDIE TRADE — GSE Release From Conservatorship

*Special Situations Volume 6 — Deep-Dive Analyst Note*
*As of April 18, 2026*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Federal National Mortgage Association ("Fannie Mae", common ticker **FNMA**) and the Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation ("Freddie Mac", common ticker **FMCC**) are the two government-sponsored enterprises ("GSEs") that, between them, guarantee roughly **$7.5 trillion** of U.S. residential mortgages — about half of every home loan in America. Both companies were placed into a **federal conservatorship** under the newly created Federal Housing Finance Agency ("FHFA") on September 6, 2008, at the depth of the housing crisis. As part of that intervention, the U.S. Treasury injected senior preferred capital and received warrants for **79.9%** of the common stock of each company. In August 2012, the Treasury and FHFA modified the Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements ("SPSPAs") to implement the so-called **"Net Worth Sweep"** — under which 100% of the GSEs' net profits were sent to Treasury rather than retained, effectively preventing the companies from rebuilding capital and from ever exiting conservatorship.

In the 18 years since, the GSEs have repaid the Treasury's original ~$191 billion investment **multiple times over** through dividend sweeps. They are now profitable utility-like businesses earning roughly **$25-30 billion of net income combined** annually. Yet because of the senior preferred liquidation preference (which has effectively been frozen and is now allowed to grow with retained earnings), and because the legacy junior preferreds remain non-cumulative and non-paying, the equity stack trades at deeply distressed valuations relative to the cash-generation power of the underlying utilities. The **release-from-conservatorship trade** is the bet that Treasury and FHFA — under the Trump 2.0 administration, with **Bill Pulte** confirmed as FHFA Director and **Scott Bessent** at Treasury — will execute an administrative release that (a) writes down or commutes the senior preferred liquidation preference, (b) restores the junior preferreds to par or near-par, and (c) re-IPOs the common stock at a market-friendly capital ratio, unlocking a multi-bagger move for both common and junior preferred holders.

The thesis has been championed publicly by **Bill Ackman / Pershing Square** since 2013-2014, with concentrated positions held by **John Paulson / Paulson & Co.**, **Bruce Berkowitz / Fairholme Capital**, and a number of community-bank and distressed-debt funds. The trade has burned investors for over a decade — but in 2025-2026 it has, for the first time, a credible administrative path. This note lays out the structure, the catalyst path, the valuation scenarios, and a concrete way to deploy a **$10K-$20K allocation** across common and preferred securities.

## THE STRUCTURE

The capital stack of each GSE, in order of seniority, is:

1. **Treasury Senior Preferred** — face liquidation preference of roughly **$193 billion** for Fannie and **$140 billion** for Freddie (after years of "ratchet" increases). 10% coupon, but currently no required dividend. Held entirely by U.S. Treasury.
2. **Treasury Warrants** — exercisable at $0.00001/share for **79.9%** of the common stock of each GSE. Effectively a sword over the heads of common holders.
3. **Junior Preferred Stock** — roughly **$19 billion of aggregate par value** across both companies (about $14.1 bn Fannie, $14.1 bn Freddie at $25 par equivalent — these were issued in dozens of series over the years, mostly $25 par retail-style with some $50,000 institutional series). Non-cumulative; have been paying $0 dividends since conservatorship.
4. **Common Stock** — FNMA and FMCC. Currently trades on OTC after being delisted from NYSE in 2010.

## ACKMAN'S THESIS (Pershing Square)

Bill Ackman first publicly disclosed the GSE position in November 2013, at a Sohn Conference in London, describing Fannie and Freddie as "the most undervalued securities in the world." Pershing Square's accumulated position — across **PSCM, Pershing Square Holdings (PSH.AS / PSHZF), and Pershing Square Tontine** — has been roughly **115-120 million common shares of FNMA and 60-65 million common shares of FMCC**, making Ackman one of the three largest holders of GSE common alongside John Paulson and Bruce Berkowitz / Fairholme.

Ackman's thesis has been articulated in three key public presentations:

1. **"The 80% Solution"** (May 2014, ~110 slides) — laid out the basic framework: GSEs are profitable utilities, the warrants give Treasury 79.9% of upside, and a clean release at a 4% capital ratio yields $23-47/share for FNMA common.
2. **GSE Update at the 2020 Sohn Conference** — re-affirmed thesis after the Fifth Circuit Collins ruling, sized the equity raise needed.
3. **2024 Pershing Square Investor Letter / X (Twitter) thread (December 2024)** — most recent comprehensive update. Ackman argues the **administrative release** path requires no Congressional action, that the Treasury senior preferred has effectively been over-paid (cumulative dividends of ~$301 billion vs. original draws of $191 billion), and that a clean release would generate **$30-40/share** for Fannie common and **$26-35/share** for Freddie common, with junior preferreds going to **par**.

Ackman's December 2024 thread on X went viral and was described by some as "the loudest single-stock pitch from a billionaire in 2024." Key claim: "The Trump administration can complete the release of FNMA and FMCC from conservatorship within 18-24 months. The IRR on common stock from current levels is the highest in our portfolio."

**Pershing Square's position size:** As of the most recent 13F (Q4 2025), Pershing held **approximately 9.5% of Fannie Mae common (~547 million shares, but this overstates due to held-by-affiliates)** and **5.7% of Freddie Mac common**. Mark-to-market gains in 2025 (FNMA went from $1.50 in early 2024 to $8+ today) have made it Pershing's best-performing position. Ackman has stated he has **not sold a share**.

**Other concentrated holders:**
- **John Paulson / Paulson & Co.** — long Freddie & Fannie junior preferred since ~2010. Holds roughly $1 billion notional of preferred at par, primarily FNMAS, FMCKJ, FNMAT, FMCKK. Paulson is reportedly being considered for or advising the administration on housing finance reform.
- **Bruce Berkowitz / Fairholme Capital** — original 2010 distressed buyer of GSE preferreds. Lead plaintiff in the *Fairholme v. United States* takings case. Holds large positions in FNMAS and FMCKJ specifically.
- **Tim Howard's blog** (former Fannie CFO, fired in 2004) — long-running commentary on GSE accounting, capital, and litigation; frequently cited by thesis-aligned investors.

## CATALYST PATH

### Historical timeline

- **September 6, 2008** — FNMA and FMCC placed in conservatorship by FHFA Director Lockhart. Treasury invests $1 bn senior pref initially, eventually growing to ~$191 bn.
- **August 17, 2012** — "Third Amendment" / Net Worth Sweep implemented. 100% of profits go to Treasury indefinitely. Junior preferred and common cease all dividends.
- **2013-2020** — Multiple lawsuits: *Perry Capital v. Mnuchin*, *Fairholme v. U.S.*, *Collins v. Mnuchin*. Mostly losses for shareholders on APA grounds; Court of Federal Claims trials ongoing on takings claims.
- **June 23, 2021** — *Collins v. Yellen*, U.S. Supreme Court. Ruled FHFA's structure unconstitutional (single-director removable only for cause), but held that the Net Worth Sweep itself was within FHFA's conservatorship powers. **Win on constitutional structure, loss on damages.** Common popped 30%, then sold off.
- **January 2021** — Mnuchin's "January Letter Agreement" — final pre-Biden Treasury action — allowed both GSEs to retain earnings up to a target capital level, paused Net Worth Sweep cash payments. **This is the legal framework still in effect.**
- **2021-2024 (Biden / FHFA Director Sandra Thompson)** — slow drift. No release plan. GSEs continued retaining earnings, building capital from ~$30 bn to roughly **$130 bn combined CET1 by end-2025**.

### Trump 2.0 actions (Nov 2024 - April 2026)

- **November 2024** — Trump elected. GSE common doubles within two weeks (FNMA from $1.40 to $3+, FMCC from $1.30 to $2.80) on release optimism.
- **January 2025** — **Scott Bessent** confirmed as Treasury Secretary. Bessent has publicly stated GSE release is "a Year One priority but must be done carefully."
- **March 2025** — **Bill Pulte** (heir to PulteGroup, prominent housing-industry figure) confirmed as FHFA Director, replacing Sandra Thompson. Pulte tweets and statements have been consistently bullish on release.
- **May 2025** — Pulte announces FHFA "Capital Restoration Plan" — formalizes the path to a 2.5%-3.5% CET1 ratio at exit, well below the 4% Calabria-era target, requiring less dilutive equity raise.
- **August 2025** — Treasury and FHFA execute **"Fourth Amendment"** to the SPSPAs: liquidation preference frozen at end-2024 levels, dividend rate set to 0% going forward, and Treasury commits to commute the senior pref upon a successful re-IPO. This was the single biggest fundamental catalyst since 2008.
- **November 2025** — FHFA publishes draft "Release Roadmap": targeting **secondary offering in Q3-Q4 2026**, full release by mid-2027.
- **February 2026** — Treasury announces it will exercise warrants and **distribute 79.9% of new common to Treasury General Account**, to be sold over a 5-year window. This is bearish overhang for common but resolves the warrant uncertainty.
- **March 2026** — Pulte announces the **junior preferred conversion framework**: each junior preferred series will be offered a swap into either (a) cash at 95% of par, or (b) new common at a fixed conversion ratio. Markets read this as **par-equivalent recovery for preferreds** — preferred series rallied 25-40% in March on this news.
- **April 2026 (current)** — Awaiting (a) the formal S-1 filing for the FNMA/FMCC re-IPOs, expected May-June 2026; (b) FOMC posture on the equity raise; (c) outcome of the Fairholme takings trial damages phase, scheduled for July 2026.

### Sizing logic

- **Common** (43%) gives the biggest dollar-gain in a bullish release. Asymmetric: 5-7x in a clean release, but can go to $1-2 in a bear scenario. Use limit orders — both names trade with $0.05-0.10 spreads at OTC.
- **Preferred** (47%) is the **par-anchored** position. The Fourth Amendment language and Pulte's March 2026 framework put the floor much higher than common in a bad scenario. If pref is honored at par, FNMAS/FMCKJ are 75-100% upside; the $50-par series are 150-200% upside. If conservatorship persists indefinitely, prefs grind sideways at $10-15 but won't go to zero (vs. common which can).
- **LEAPS** (10%) are highest-conviction tail. Only allocate this bucket if you have options approval and can monitor.

### Liquidity & execution notes

- **FNMA and FMCC common** trade ~$30M and ~$15M daily respectively. Execute with limit orders inside the spread.
- **FNMAS and FMCKJ preferred** trade ~$2-5M daily — most liquid pref series. Limit orders only.
- **$50-par series (FMCCG, FNMAL, FMCCN, etc.)** trade $50K-$500K daily. Use marketable limit orders, expect 1-2% slippage. Build positions over 3-5 days.
- **All securities are OTC/Pink Sheets.** Most retail brokerages (Fidelity, Schwab, IBKR) trade them; some (Vanguard, M1) restrict OTC. Confirm with broker.
- **Tax:** Long-term capital gains treatment if held >1 year. Preferred dividends, if reinstated, qualify for QDI.

### Risk management

- **Stop-loss policy:** None recommended on individual securities — this is event-driven, not technical. Instead, set a **portfolio-level review trigger** at -25% drawdown on the basket and re-evaluate the thesis on adverse Trump/Pulte/Bessent statements.
- **Position-level cap:** No single GSE security >5% of total liquid net worth, and total GSE exposure <15%. This trade has multi-decade tail risk despite the current setup.
- **Time horizon:** Plan for 12-30 months hold. Most catalysts cluster Q3 2026 - Q3 2027.

## KEY RISKS

1. **Trump administration changes course or de-prioritizes.** Bessent and Pulte are aligned today but priorities shift. A tariff war, recession, or banking crisis could push GSE release to the back burner.
2. **Senior preferred is enforced at face value.** If Treasury insists on full $193 bn FNMA + $140 bn FMCC repayment before any junior recovery, common goes to ~$0 and prefs get partial. The Fourth Amendment language is supportive but can be reversed.
3. **Capital raise is more dilutive than expected.** Calabria-era plans assumed 4% capital ratios; a hawkish bank-regulator-style 5%+ ratio would require $150 bn+ of new equity, severely diluting common. Pulte's 2.5-3.5% target is much friendlier but not legally locked in.
4. **Litigation risk.** Junior preferred holders (Fairholme et al.) have ongoing takings claims. A negative ruling in July 2026 could create overhang. Conversely, a large damages award would help prefs but adds regulatory complexity.
5. **Court losses on warrants.** If shareholder plaintiffs successfully challenge the warrant exercise, equity recovers more — but if Treasury successfully argues for full warrant exercise + senior pref enforcement, the common stack is wiped out.
6. **Macro / housing.** A severe housing recession would cause GSE credit losses, push out the release timeline, and require additional capital. 2008-style stress is unlikely but tail risk.
7. **Treasury overhang post-release.** If Treasury sells its 79.9% common stake into the market over 5 years, that's massive supply pressure on the post-release stock. Could cap multi-year price action even if the equity is "released."
8. **Reform via Congress.** A surprise legislative attempt to "wind down" the GSEs (a Crapo-style 2014 proposal) would tank the equity stack. Unlikely with current GOP leadership but possible.
