# {NUMBER}. IMPP — Imperial Petroleum Inc.
## CYCLICAL TROUGH | Score: 53/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Imperial Petroleum is a Marshall Islands–incorporated tanker and drybulk owner spun off from StealthGas in December 2021. Per the 2024 20-F, the on-the-water fleet at year-end was twelve vessels: seven MR product tankers, two suezmaxes, and three handysize drybulk carriers, generating voyage revenues of $147.5 million in 2024 (down from $183.7 million in 2023 as product-tanker rates normalized). Net income was $50.2 million in 2024 versus $71.1 million in 2023. The headline reason it screens cheap: the company carries $206.7 million of cash and time deposits at 12/31/2024 against essentially zero bank debt, while the equity market cap is roughly $158 million — the cash alone exceeds market cap, and the fleet is essentially being thrown in for free. Subsequent 2025 disclosures expanded the fleet to 19 vessels averaging in Q4 with a 26-vessel target, funded out of internal cash.

Why is it cheap? Three issues stack up. First, governance: Chairman/CEO Harry Vafias controls 49.99% of voting power via Series B preferred and pays his family-owned Stealth Maritime Corporation S.A. for technical, commercial, and crewing management — virtually every dollar of opex flows through related parties. Second, dilution scars: IMPP did multiple equity offerings and warrant issuances in 2022–2024 plus a 1-for-15 reverse split in April 2023, leaving long-term holders torched. Third, fleet age and cyclicality: average tanker age sits above the global average; product-tanker spot rates have rolled off the 2023 highs as Russia-related dislocations partially normalize. Common shareholders also receive zero dividend — only the 8.75% Series A preferred is paid.

The bull case is straightforward asset arbitrage: cash of $206.7M plus a fleet whose stated book value (and likely fair-market value) exceeds another $200M, on a $158M cap. Capital allocation has finally turned shareholder-friendly: a $10M common buyback authorized in early 2024 was nearly exhausted by April 2025 ($8.4M repurchased, plus $1.5M of warrant repurchases). Recent 2025 announcements show fleet growth funded out of cash without re-issuing equity — a meaningful tone change. To close the gap an investor needs (a) Vafias to keep buying back stock instead of issuing more, (b) tanker rates to hold above breakeven through 2026, and (c) no value-destroying related-party newbuild orders. Net asset coverage gives a hard floor most of the way down.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The Series B preferred is the entire governance story. Issued to Vafias-controlled Flawless Management Inc. on October 21, 2022, it carries no economic rights — par $0.01, no dividend, no liquidation premium beyond par — but each share votes with the common. The structure mathematically locks the CEO at exactly 49.99% of voting power regardless of how much common is issued or repurchased. That means even a successful asset-arbitrage thesis cannot produce a hostile bid, a board flip, or a forced wind-down distribution. Holders are entirely dependent on Vafias's discretion to keep returning cash. Combined with the related-party management contract at Stealth Maritime, every accretive action also enriches the family vehicle — a structure that explains why the discount persists despite the cash pile.
