# {NUMBER}. DJCO — Daily Journal Corporation
## CASH FORTRESS | Score: 71/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Daily Journal is a unique two-headed beast: a small legal/business publishing operation (the Traditional Business — Daily Journals in CA/AZ, judicial profiles, court rules services) plus Journal Technologies, a SaaS-and-services court case-management software business that now produces approximately 80% of consolidated revenue (vs 76% in fiscal 2024). Sitting on top is the legendary investment portfolio originally assembled and managed by Charlie Munger until his death on November 28, 2023. As of September 30, 2025 the marketable-securities portfolio had a market value of approximately $493.0 million on an adjusted cost basis of approximately $139.1 million — a cumulative unrealized gain of $353.9 million before estimated taxes of $91.4 million. The portfolio remains concentrated in just six companies, generated $7.4 million in dividends and interest in fiscal 2025, and total assets stood at $548.1 million.

The bear case is that the Munger magic is genuinely irreplaceable, and the company says so explicitly. The 10-K states the board "expects" the portfolio to be managed and harvested primarily to fund Journal Technologies development going forward — which is the language of a company that no longer believes the marketable-securities arm will compound at superior returns. The operating businesses are mediocre on their own: Journal Technologies is a perpetually-money-losing-to-breakeven public-sector SaaS implementation business with long sales cycles, professional-services dependence, lumpy lawsuits and competitive bidding processes. The Traditional Business is a melting ice cube — print legal newspapers, public-notice advertising regulation under attack. Six-stock concentration risk is enormous; a downturn in any single major holding will whip net income and book value violently. Steven Myhill-Jones is CEO and Chairman per the FY25 signature page — leadership transition still being digested.

The bull case rests on the gap between book value (where the portfolio is marked) and intrinsic value of Journal Technologies if it can finally scale. P/B 1.95 understates the situation: the marketable securities are at fair-value mark-to-market, but Journal Technologies — a sticky, switching-cost-heavy public-sector SaaS franchise serving courts, prosecutors, and justice agencies — has zero intangible value on the balance sheet beyond accumulated investment. If Journal Technologies converts even modestly to a steady-state SaaS margin profile, the operating value of that segment alone could approach 9-figure incremental value. Net cash represents approximately 64% of market cap. Munger trained the discipline — there is no leverage, no dilutive issuance, no empire-building. As long as a successor portfolio manager simply doesn't destroy the $493 million pile, downside is well-bounded.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The "P/E 8.0" is essentially meaningless because reported earnings are dominated by unrealized gains on marketable securities — fiscal 2025 saw $134.3 million of pretax unrealized gains versus only $4.2 million from operating income. Strip out the portfolio mark-to-market and Journal Technologies + Traditional are roughly breakeven. Deferred tax liability on unrealized gains is approximately $91.4 million, so true after-tax net asset value attributable to the portfolio is closer to $400 million than $493 million. The portfolio uses margin borrowing collateralized by securities to fund Journal Technologies acquisitions and working capital — a Munger trick — and is therefore subject to margin calls in a sharp drawdown. The company has paid no dividends in fiscal 2025 or 2024 and has no current plans to. Six-stock concentration: per published 13-Fs, the portfolio still tilts heavily toward US bank holdings.
