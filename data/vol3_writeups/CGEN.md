# {NUMBER}. CGEN — Compugen Ltd.
## CASH FORTRESS | Score: 60/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Compugen is an Israeli computational antibody-discovery company that pioneered the PVRIG and TIGIT immune-checkpoint targets. Its near-term value sits in two partnerships. AstraZeneca licenses anti-TIGIT antibodies (including COM902) and developed rilvegostomig — a PD-1/TIGIT bispecific now in Phase 3 across roughly ten oncology trials, with the Compugen-derived TIGIT arm. Gilead licensed the company's anti–IL-18 binding-protein program (GS-0321, formerly COM503), with Compugen running the Phase 1. Internally, Compugen advances COM701 (anti-PVRIG) — a Phase 1 program now positioned as maintenance therapy in platinum-sensitive ovarian cancer, with interim data targeted Q1 2027. FY2025 revenue was $72.8M with $35.3M net income, driven largely by the AstraZeneca milestone. Cash + short-term deposits + marketable securities were approximately $145.6M at year-end 2025, vs. $103.3M at end-2024 — runway management says is "sufficient... over the next 12 months" but the press-release narrative extends visibility into 2029 thanks to a non-dilutive royalty monetization with AstraZeneca ($65M upfront plus $25M added to the next BLA-acceptance milestone in exchange for a slice of the rilvegostomig royalty interest).

The bear case is the entire TIGIT class. Arcus and Gilead disclosed the Phase 3 STAR-221 study failure for domvanalimab; the 20-F itself notes that "recent failures in the TIGIT field" have caused investor skepticism. If rilvegostomig fails to deliver in its Phase 3 readouts, the AstraZeneca royalty stream collapses to optionality on a single bispecific construct that depends on a target with a poor track record. COM701 is a Phase 1 asset in ovarian cancer with modest single-agent data (ORR 12% / DCR 30% in heavily pretreated patients per the 20-F's SITC 2023 table), and the MAIA-ovarian readout is two years out. Compugen has accumulated a $453.4M deficit since inception with limited internal validation events to show.

The bull case: this is one of the few biotech "cash fortresses" where the cash actually came from real partnership economics, not equity dilution. The AstraZeneca royalty monetization is incremental capital without share issuance. If even one of the partnered programs (rilvegostomig in resectable hepatocellular, biliary tract, NSCLC; GS-0321 in solid tumors) crosses regulatory finish, milestone payments and royalties accrue with effectively zero capex. Net cash is ~52% of market cap, and the company has demonstrated discipline — operating cash burn is modest because partner payments cover most R&D.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The royalty monetization deal structure is the most important non-screen disclosure. The 20-F notes the AstraZeneca arrangement was "amended (including most recently as December 16, 2025)" — Compugen sold "a small portion" of its existing royalty interest in rilvegostomig for upfront cash plus an enlarged near-term milestone, while retaining the bulk of the long-tail royalty on a potential blockbuster. That is exactly the right kind of cash-extraction trade for a small biotech with one major asset in someone else's hands. Separately, the IIA (Israel Innovation Authority) royalty obligation of approximately $8.3M — small relative to cash — is a liability disclosed in the financials that screens won't flag. NOL carry-forward of $381.4M for Israeli tax purposes is a meaningful future-earnings shield.
