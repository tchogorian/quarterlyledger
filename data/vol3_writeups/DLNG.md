# {NUMBER}. DLNG — Dynagas LNG Partners LP
## BELOW-BOOK QUALITY | Score: 60/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Dynagas LNG Partners is a Marshall Islands MLP that owns six ice-class LNG carriers, all on multi-year time charters to investment-grade-tier counterparties. Per the FY2025 20-F (filed April 2026), 2025 net income was $61.6 million on adjusted EBITDA of $109.2 million; charters are with SEFE Marketing & Trading (the German entity formerly Gazprom Marketing & Trading), Equinor, Yamal Trade, and Rio Grande LNG. The Arctic Aurora Equinor charter expires September 2026, with a follow-on seven-year charter already contracted. Three vessels serve the Yamal LNG project on long-tenor charters extending into 2028. Cash distributions resumed at modest levels — common $0.05/quarter (paid Feb 2026) and the 42nd sequential Series A 8.75% preferred distribution of $0.5625/unit. With a $145M equity cap against book value of roughly $400M+ and a young, fully contracted fleet, the partnership trades at 0.36x book and roughly 2.9x earnings.

Why is it cheap? Three structural overhangs. First, charterer concentration: SEFE/Yamal exposure means a Russian-related-flag risk premium even though SEFE itself was nationalized by Germany and Yamal cargoes flow legally. Second, MLP structure with a controlling general partner held by the Prokopiou family ("Sponsor") — minority unitholders have no governance levers, the GP receives incentive distribution rights, and the Series A preferreds sit ahead of common in the cash waterfall. Third, refinancing/maturity risk: the senior credit facility was extensively refinanced in 2025 (interest expense recognized on a sponsor facility from June 27 to July 25, 2025), and unitholders bear the credit-spread mark on the post-refi term loan. Fourth-tier worry: U.S.-China port-fee retaliation suspended through November 2026 but unresolved, potentially affecting LNG carrier port economics.

The bull case is "fully contracted asset at half book." All six vessels carry forward charter cover into 2026–2028, generating predictable cash. The newly extended $10M common buyback (through November 2026) signals capital-return intent — 148,933 units repurchased in Q4 2025 alone — and the common distribution though tiny is sequentially restored. LNG shipping rates remain structurally supported by the global LNG export build-out (Plaquemines, Rio Grande, Corpus Christi Stage III all coming online). Ice-class capability is a moat for Arctic loading windows. To close the gap an investor needs (a) the Arctic Aurora to roll cleanly to its contracted Equinor seven-year follow-on, (b) no defaults or sanctions hit on Yamal-trade counterparties, and (c) common distribution to keep stepping back up. Even at current $0.20 annualized common distribution the yield exceeds 5%, with significant room to grow as the term loan amortizes.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 20-F discloses that the partnership relied on a Sponsor (Prokopiou-family) bridge facility between June 27 and July 25, 2025, recognizing $902.3 thousand of interest expense to the related party during a 28-day window — a clear sign that the senior debt refinancing executed in mid-2025 had a tight closing. That kind of related-party bridge is not a long-term financing; it tells you the public capital markets remained shut to DLNG until the refi closed and that the GP family is the lender of last resort. Two read-throughs: (1) the GP has every incentive to keep the partnership solvent and pay the preferreds because it's also a creditor, but (2) the same family controls the GP, the manager, and now apparently the bridge financing — minority unitholders are riding entirely on Prokopiou-family alignment, which has been historically conservative on common distributions but has at least stopped issuing equity at the bottom.
