# {NUMBER}. FLX — BingEx Limited (FlashEx / Flash Express)
## CASH FORTRESS | Score: 56/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY
BingEx, the China on-demand intracity courier branded "FlashEx," is a 2024 IPO that completed its float just as Chinese ADR sentiment cratered. The model is dedicated point-to-point delivery — one rider, one order, average completion in roughly 27 minutes — competing with Meituan Instashopping, Dada/JD Now, and SF Intracity for time-critical parcels (documents, gifts, groceries, restaurant supplies, business B2B runs). FY2024 revenue was RMB4,468.2 million (US$612.1 million) on 277.2 million orders fulfilled, up from 213.4 million in 2022. The screen flag — net cash of roughly US$929M against a US$183M market cap — is the headline; almost all of the IPO proceeds remain on the balance sheet, with consolidated cash plus short-term investments well in excess of current liabilities.

The bear case is straightforward: this is a low-margin gig-economy business in a fiercely contested segment where giants (Meituan, JD, Alibaba's Ele.me) are willing to subsidize delivery indefinitely as a loss-leader for higher-margin commerce. Gross margin sits in the low 10s, FY2024 swung to a small net loss after a brief 2023 profit, and volume growth has decelerated to mid-single-digit. Add the standard China ADR overhang — VIE contracts (the VIE contributed 18.5% of 2024 revenue, but more critically holds the operating licenses), Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act inspection risk, capital controls preventing offshore distribution of mainland cash — and the gap between book cash and shareholder economic value widens materially.

The bull case rests on the cash itself plus a credible management response. The board authorized a US$30M repurchase that was extended in March 2026; roughly US$9.2M had been deployed by mid-March 2026, ~2.8M ADSs retired. FY2025 returned to GAAP profitability (net income RMB109.4M, non-GAAP RMB199.4M — third straight year of non-GAAP profit) on RMB3,992.1M revenue, with gross margin expanding to 10.8% in Q4. If management accelerates buybacks, declares a special dividend out of offshore cash, or executes a strategic transaction, the discount can close. Until then, you own a cash pile with a real (if structurally challenged) operating business attached.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 20-F discloses cash mechanics that screens miss. Of consolidated cash, only the WFOE/VIE pools sit in mainland RMB; significant balances reside at the Cayman parent and Hong Kong sub from IPO proceeds — those are the dollars actually returnable to shareholders without SAFE approval. The filing also notes that "96.9%, 23.2%, and 18.5% of our revenues were contributed by the VIE" in 2022/23/24 respectively — the company restructured to reduce VIE revenue dependence, but the VIE still holds the China courier operating licenses, so the legal contracts remain load-bearing for compliance, not cash flow. The riders are independent contractors, not employees — labor-cost reclassification by PRC authorities is a tail risk that could compress margins overnight, similar to what happened to US gig peers post-AB5.
