# {NUMBER}. CPSS — Consumer Portfolio Services, Inc.
## VALUE TRAP RISK | Score: 42/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Consumer Portfolio Services is a Las Vegas-headquartered indirect subprime auto lender that purchases retail installment contracts from a national dealer network and funds the portfolio almost entirely through asset-backed securitization — 107 term securitizations originated since 1994, with 19 still outstanding. FY2025 results: total revenues of $434.5M (interest income $422.7M plus a $6.5M positive fair-value mark on receivables and $5.3M of other income), against $232.0M of interest expense and a NEGATIVE $2.9M provision for credit losses (a benefit), producing $19.3M of net income or $0.88 basic / $0.80 diluted EPS. The total managed portfolio reached $3.78B against $3.86B in total assets and $309.5M of shareholders' equity (book value ~$14.20). The capital stack carries $2.99B securitization trust debt, $324.9M warehouse, $143.0M residual financing and $29.0M subordinated renewables. At 0.60x book and 10.6x earnings on a $187M market cap, this is a quintessential subprime credit value play.

The bear case is the credit cycle and concentrations. Net charge-offs as a percentage of average managed portfolio rose to 7.8% in 2025 from 7.6% in 2024 and 6.5% in 2023, and Q3 2025 standalone annualized charge-offs hit 8.01% with total delinquencies (including repossession inventory) at 13.96%. Total dollar delinquencies grew to $447.0M (11.8% of $3.78B) from $422.8M, and amount-in-repossession reached $111.2M. The business runs on continuous warehouse and securitization access — a single closed window would force balance-sheet shrinkage and book-value erosion. The Compensation Committee itself notes the inherent conflict that growing the book by underpricing risk is the easiest way to look good short-term. Customer concentration is geographic (Texas, Ohio, California, Illinois are top-four dealer states) and macroeconomically tied to subprime employment.

The bull case is securitization franchise and steady earnings. CPSS reported its 14th consecutive profitable year and just closed its 41st consecutive securitization where the senior tranche received triple-A ratings from at least two agencies — that program access is a real competitive advantage few subprime peers retain. CEO Charles E. Bradley Jr. has been at the helm since 1992 and his executive team carries deep options-aligned exposure (Bradley alone with options for 1.815M shares plus existing holdings). Capital return is meaningful: $8.7M of stock repurchased in 2025 on top of $12.8M in 2024 and $20.3M in 2023 — roughly 4–10% of float per year repurchased through the cycle. Book value grew from $228.4M (2022) to $309.5M (2025).

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The most important number on the income statement is the $2.9M *negative* provision for credit losses — meaning CPSS released reserves in 2025. This is a function of the company's adoption of the fair-value election for receivables originated post-January 1, 2018. For these contracts, CPSS recognizes interest income on a level-yield basis using the originated IRR and "we do not record an expense for provision for credit losses on these receivables because such credit losses are included in our computation of the appropriate level yield." Translation: most of the portfolio runs through a fair-value lens that smooths credit costs prospectively into the yield, while CECL-style provisions only apply to a shrinking legacy bucket. So when the screen reports 10.6x earnings, that earnings stream is more sensitive than it looks to the assumed level yield holding up against the rising 7.8% charge-off line — a small recalibration of fair-value marks could swing several quarters of EPS.
