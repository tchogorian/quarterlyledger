# {NUMBER}. GENC — Gencor Industries, Inc.
## CASH FORTRESS | Score: 71/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY
Gencor Industries is one of the cleanest "cigar-butt-meets-quality" setups in the US small-cap industrial universe. The Orlando-based company has manufactured heavy equipment for asphalt and highway-construction markets for over 50 years — counterflow drum plants, batch plants, soil-remediation systems, fluid heat-transfer units, and combustion controls — selling primarily to North American paving contractors. The screen sees a P/E of 14.2 and a P/B of 1.00, but those numbers obscure a fortress balance sheet: roughly $136 million in cash and marketable securities against zero long-term debt at FY2025 year-end, against an enterprise value that is a small fraction of the equity market cap. Net cash represents an estimated 68% of market cap, which makes the operating business effectively free at current prices and reframes Gencor as a closed-end investment vehicle wrapped around a cyclical equipment manufacturer.

The bear case is real and is mostly about capital allocation discipline and cycle timing. Gencor is family-controlled by the Elliotts, and the company has historically hoarded cash rather than buying back stock aggressively or instituting a regular dividend. Special dividends are sporadic and unpredictable. The marketable-securities portfolio injects mark-to-market noise into reported earnings every quarter — the headline "P/E" moves around with equity markets rather than with the asphalt cycle. More concerning: the FY2025 10-K disclosed a backlog of $23.6 million versus $56.2 million the prior year, a roughly 58% drop. That signals either order timing slippage or a genuine near-term contraction in dealer/contractor demand, which would push FY2026 revenues meaningfully lower despite federal IIJA tailwinds in the macro background.

The bull case rests on three pillars. First, the cash pile is genuinely floor-setting — at current prices an investor is paying near book and inheriting $100m+ of liquid securities. Second, the IIJA highway-funding cycle is still in mid-deployment through 2027, and Gencor is one of only a few US-domiciled producers (competing with Astec Industries) of integrated asphalt plants, with a sticky installed-base service revenue stream. Third, family control creates conditions for either a take-private offer at a premium or a one-time large special dividend if the Elliotts decide the cash hoard has outgrown operating needs. Even modest 6–8% mid-cycle operating margins on $115m of revenue justify the ex-cash equity value several times over.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 10-K's marketable securities footnote is essential: a meaningful portion sits in equity securities, not just Treasuries, so unrealized gains/losses flow through the P&L and distort GAAP earnings vs. operating cash generation. The "real" P/E ex-investments is materially lower than the headline. The backlog disclosure (Dec 1 reporting date convention rather than fiscal year-end) is the single best leading indicator of near-term revenue — at $23.6m it points to a soft FY2026 first half. Geographic concentration is overwhelmingly US highway/paving contractors; there is little international diversification. Insider ownership through John Elliott and family is concentrated enough that minority shareholders are effectively along for whatever capital allocation decision the Elliotts choose to make next.
