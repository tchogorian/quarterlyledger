# {NUMBER}. CAAS — China Automotive Systems, Inc.
## VALUE TRAP RISK | Score: 47/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

China Automotive Systems is a Hubei-headquartered tier-1 supplier of power-steering components and increasingly electronic power steering ("EPS") systems, operating through sixteen joint ventures and wholly owned subsidiaries via its Genesis holding company. Per the FY2024 10-K, net product sales reached $650.9 million, up 12.9% year-over-year, with the Henglong segment alone contributing $325.9 million (+20.0%). Net sales to China were $686.3 million versus $239.1 million to foreign markets. The company supplies most major Chinese OEMs — including Geely, Chery, Changan, SAIC, and FAW — and has shipped to Stellantis since 2009 and Ford since 2016. Cash and short-term investments totaled $84.5 million at year-end (down from $125.7M), with working capital of $146.2 million. At a $129M market cap, that's a P/B near 0.34 against a profitable, growing, EV-exposed steering franchise.

Why is it cheap? The discount is almost entirely about jurisdiction and structure rather than operations. The five largest customers account for 56.9% of sales — heavy concentration in price-pressured Chinese OEMs that aggressively negotiate annual price-downs. Subsidiary cash is trapped: PRC subsidiaries must allocate 10% of after-tax profit to a statutory reserve fund until it reaches 50% of registered capital, and dividend repatriation to U.S. holders requires SAFE approval and withholding tax. The Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act creates ongoing delisting risk if PCAOB inspection access is revoked. In September 2025 the company completed a redomiciliation merger to the Cayman Islands (new CIK 0002037400), partially restructuring the holding architecture but not removing the PRC operating-subsidiary cash-trap problem. Receivables aging in China auto-supply is notoriously stretched.

The bull case rests on a real growth franchise hidden inside the discount. The R&D Testing Center is a Hubei provincial-level technical center developing intelligent and high-torque eRCB steering motors targeted at the EV segment (mass production scheduled late 2025). Margins held up despite RMB price pressure because of mix shift toward EPS. With $84.5M cash + working capital of $146M against zero net debt, balance-sheet collapse is implausible. The redomiciliation removes one technical overhang and arguably positions for a future capital-return policy (the company has paid no regular cash dividend since the 2019 buyback program). To close the gap a holder needs (a) Beijing to refrain from delist-trigger sanctions, (b) at least one meaningful capital-return signal post-Cayman migration, and (c) Chinese OEM end-market demand to remain stable through 2026 EV oversupply.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The minority-interest line is enormous and distorts the headline P/B. CAAS owns sixteen separate joint ventures and subsidiaries, several of them only majority-owned (notably the Henglong-KYB JV and other technical partnerships). A material slice of book equity belongs to local Chinese partners — and their dividend rights take priority on a per-entity basis before any cash can be upstreamed to U.S. holders. Combine that with the PRC statutory reserve requirement and the SAFE approval gate, and the "tangible book per ADS" you see on a screen overstates the cash that could ever realistically be distributed to a New York shareholder. The redomiciliation to the Cayman Islands does not change this: the operating subsidiaries remain PRC entities subject to SAFE controls. This is the single biggest gap between screen value and recoverable value.
