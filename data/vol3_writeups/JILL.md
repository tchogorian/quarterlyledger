# {NUMBER}. JILL — J.Jill, Inc.
## HIDDEN COMPOUNDER | Score: 67/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY
J.Jill is a 256-store omnichannel women's apparel chain targeting an affluent customer aged 45+, with a heritage in relaxed, easy-to-wear fits and a roughly 50/50 split between brick-and-mortar and direct (catalog plus e-commerce). Fiscal 2025 (ended January 31, 2026) revenue was approximately $611M, essentially flat versus FY24, with reported net income near $39.5M and a net margin of about 6.5%. The company has just under 14.9M shares outstanding and trades at a single-digit P/E with a 25% reported ROE — figures that imply the market views the business as a melting catalog asset rather than a durable specialty retailer. Management has raised the quarterly dividend 12.5% to $0.09 and continued to invest in store openings and the New Hampshire distribution and contact center.

The bear case is that J.Jill's customer is loyal but old, the catalog channel is structurally declining, and apparel is a brutally cyclical, fashion-risk business with limited pricing power once promotional pressure resumes. Comps were essentially flat in FY25; capex jumped 33% to $14.3M, suggesting the easy free-cash-flow harvesting period of the post-2020 restructuring is ending. The 25% ROE flatters a business whose return on invested capital is lower once you strip out the depleted equity base and adjust for operating leases. A modest macro shock, a return to mall traffic decline, or a single bad season of inventory could compress margins by 200-300 bps and turn the P/E from 6.7x into a value trap.

The bull case is that JILL is a tightly run, niche specialty retailer with a defined customer, a high direct mix that gives it 1P data, and a balance sheet that has been deleveraged dramatically since the 2020 sale-leaseback restructuring. At ~$200-300M market cap on roughly $40M of net income, the stock is priced for terminal decline; any combination of stable comps, modest store-count growth, and continued buyback/dividend capacity unlocks a re-rating to 9-10x earnings. Tax assets remaining from the restructuring shield cash taxes, boosting reported FCF conversion. If management continues the dividend hike pattern and adds a buyback, total shareholder yield could reach 6-8%.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The 520,000-sqft New Hampshire distribution and contact center is a single point of failure — it processed 28M units in FY25 (13M retail, 15M direct) and any disruption (storm, system outage, labor) would hit both channels simultaneously. The 14.9M share count is unusually small for a public retailer and creates real liquidity friction; a single index reconstitution or one large holder's exit could move the stock 10%. JILL also still carries a meaningful deferred tax asset position from the post-COVID restructuring that is not visible in headline P/E. Watch the gap between reported net income, cash taxes paid, and SBC — operating cash flow is what matters, not GAAP earnings. Lease liabilities (256 stores) are the real "debt."
