# {NUMBER}. REFI — Chicago Atlantic Real Estate Finance, Inc.
## DISTRESSED ARB | Score: 62/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Chicago Atlantic Real Estate Finance is a commercial mortgage REIT that originates and holds senior secured loans collateralized primarily by real estate, equipment, and licenses of state-licensed cannabis operators. The company is externally managed by Chicago Atlantic REIT Manager and listed on NASDAQ. The screen flagged REFI at P/B 0.81, P/E 7.1 on a $250M market cap. The 2025 10-K shows loans held for investment of $411.1M outstanding principal with original-issue discount of $2.1M and current-expected-credit-loss reserve of $5.1M, for a carrying value of $403.9M. Weighted-average maturity is 2.2 years, and the portfolio's yield-to-maturity is 16.3% on a weighted-average basis (range 9.7% to 26.9%) inclusive of coupon, OID, unused fees, exit fees, and other yield-enhancing items. The current expected credit loss reserve increased ~$716K year-over-year, signaling some incremental credit deterioration but not a portfolio break.

The bear case begins with one fact: cannabis remains illegal under U.S. federal law. The 10-K states bluntly that "cannabis remains illegal under federal law," and a federal enforcement turn (or simply a delayed Schedule III rescheduling) could collapse tenant economics across multi-state operators. State-level price compression in mature markets like California, Massachusetts, Michigan, and Colorado has already squeezed wholesale margins, raising borrower default risk. The 10-K notes that certain assets cannot be used as collateral or transferred due to state cannabis-licensing restrictions — a material constraint on workout recovery. Loan concentration risk is real: portfolio of ~30 loans means a single $25-30M position can move the dividend. External management means a permanent fee leak (base + incentive) regardless of REFI shareholder outcomes. Banking-access risk persists pending SAFE Banking, which has stalled in Congress for years.

The bull case is a 16.3% portfolio yield collected by a REIT trading at 0.81x book — even with conservative underwriting, the spread between asset yields and the cost of equity is wide. Short 2.2-year duration means the book repositions quickly if conditions worsen; the manager has done two rounds of new-vintage originations at attractive coupons since rates rose. Catalysts include DEA Schedule III rescheduling (would re-rate the entire cannabis-adjacent capital structure), SAFE Banking passage (would lower borrower funding costs and improve credit), Section 280E tax relief, and continued institutional starvation of the sector that lets REFI write loans others cannot. Capital allocation: high payout ratio (REIT requirement), no acquisition appetite, and a clearly defined "credit-first" underwriting process that has produced minimal realized losses through 2025.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE

Several filing-level points matter. First, REFI co-invests with affiliates of its external manager, including private vehicles ("Other investment vehicles managed by our Manager or affiliates… may co-invest with us or hold positions in a loan that we hold"). Co-investment can be a feature (deal flow access) or a problem (allocation conflict), and the 10-K's allocation policy is what to read carefully. Second, REFI generally will not take a position junior to a Manager-affiliate position — a meaningful protection. Third, "Capitalized PIK Interest" of $6.2M flowed through the loan-balance roll-forward in 2025: a portion of stated income is non-cash, which inflates GAAP NII relative to distributable cash. Fourth, the 16.3% IRR is a portfolio-level WAVG including OID and exit fees that may not be realized if loans repay early or default. Fifth, REFI is not a bank — it cannot access the Fed's discount window if cannabis credit spreads blow out.
