# {NUMBER}. AMPY — Amplify Energy Corp.
## CYCLICAL TROUGH | Score: 56/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Amplify Energy is a small upstream E&P that, after a sweeping 2025 portfolio cleanup, owns just two core asset packages: the Bairoil CO2-flood waterflood in Wyoming and the Beta field in federal waters offshore Southern California (the asset behind the 2021 San Pedro Bay pipeline incident). Per the FY2025 10-K (filed March 2026), the company divested its non-operated Eagle Ford position (July 2025), East Texas/North Louisiana assets (December 2025), and Oklahoma assets (December 2025). Total revenues fell to $263.4M (from $294.7M) reflecting both lower volumes and a $42.5M impairment charge. Net income was reported but cash flow was the headline: divestiture proceeds of roughly $250M were used to retire all credit-facility borrowings, leaving the company with no debt and approximately $61M of cash at year-end 2025. PV-10 of remaining proved reserves was $376.4M; standardized measure $335.1M. At a $227M cap that's a 0.49x P/B and 5.3x earnings on a now-debt-free, simplified portfolio.

Why is it cheap? Beta is the controversial asset. The 2021 oil spill from the San Pedro Bay pipeline (operated by Amplify subsidiary San Pedro Bay Pipeline Company, ~17.5 miles) created multi-year litigation, ongoing regulatory scrutiny from BSEE, and reputational baggage that depresses the multiple. A contemplated January 2025 merger with Juniper Capital portfolio companies (NPOG and COG, which would have added Rockies acreage) was terminated mid-2025 — leaving the company without the diversified scale management originally pursued. Bairoil is mature with declining production. Capital intensity is meaningful — 2026 guidance is $45–$65M of capex, more than 95% concentrated at Beta on 5–8 D-Sand wells, against guided adjusted EBITDA of only $20–$45M. Executive transition: former CEO Willsher transitioned to "Special Advisor" effective July 22, 2025.

The bull case is "debt-free single-asset levered call on Beta drilling success." With zero debt and $61M cash on a $227M cap, downside is genuinely cushioned. The remaining proved reserves' standardized measure of $335M plus cash exceeds market cap before crediting Beta drilling upside. Beta's D-Sand interval is highly economic at strip prices and the 2026 program is the first real capital cycle since the 2021 incident. Bairoil offers optionality on CCUS / Section 45Q monetization — one of the few existing CO2-flood operators well-positioned for tax-credit-backed expansion. To close the gap an investor needs (a) the 2026 Beta D-Sand wells to deliver type-curve results, (b) no new Beta operational incidents, and (c) Section 45Q clarity to monetize Bairoil CO2 infrastructure. The terminated merger is in the rearview; capital allocation has visibly turned conservative and asset-focused.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The Beta pipeline incident liability machinery still runs in the background even though the cash settlements have largely been booked. The 10-K discloses the BetaPipelineIncident as a tagged accounting line — meaning ongoing accruals, indemnity coverage analysis, and regulatory-monitoring costs are still flowing through G&A. More substantively: 65% of total proved reserves are concentrated in (presumably) Beta — a level of single-asset concentration that's unusual for a small-cap E&P and is the direct consequence of the 2025 divestiture program. Translation: a single regulatory event at Beta — a permit revocation, a BSEE production curtailment, a fresh leak — would disproportionately impair the entire reserve report. Counterbalancing this, the divestitures eliminated the corresponding G&A and lease-operating overhead, so the residual cost structure is much more variable than legacy Amplify. The G&A line did spike to $52.1M in 2025 (from $35.9M in 2024), reflecting transaction costs, executive separation, and merger-termination expenses — most of which should not recur in 2026.
