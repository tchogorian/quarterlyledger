### ANALYST SUMMARY
BEO Bancorp is the holding company for Bank of Eastern Oregon, a community bank founded in 1945 by farmers and ranchers and headquartered in Heppner, Oregon. The bank operates approximately 21 branches plus three loan production offices spanning 11 eastern Oregon counties, four eastern Washington counties, and one western Idaho county. Its franchise is distinctive in that it is one of the few remaining independent community banks specifically focused on agricultural lending in the Pacific Northwest interior — a region underserved by money-center banks and even most regional banks. Headline numbers — P/E 8.1, P/B 1.35, ROE 18% — describe an above-average-ROE community bank trading well below the typical 1.5–1.8x book multiple of profitable Pacific Northwest peers. ICBA recognized BEO among its Top Ag Lenders of 2024, validating the franchise's positioning.

The bear case mirrors the structural concerns common to all OTC-traded micro-cap community banks. Float is extraordinarily thin, daily volume is essentially nil for institutional purposes, and price discovery is sporadic. Concentrated agricultural lending — wheat, cattle, hay, irrigated row crops — is exposed to commodity-price cycles, drought risk, and federal farm-bill policy. Eastern Oregon's economy is small and slow-growing; deposit-base growth depends on local economic conditions that have limited demographic tailwinds. Regulatory burden on small banks continues to scale faster than asset bases, pressuring efficiency ratios. Compliance costs on a $700m–$800m asset bank are disproportionate, and the bank's wide geographic footprint (21 branches across three states) implies higher fixed-cost-to-asset ratios than urban peers. Sub-debt or capital raises by similarly sized peers have been at high coupons in the current rate environment.

The bull case is the inverse of the bear: a sticky agricultural deposit franchise with limited deposit competition, an 18% ROE that few community banks anywhere produce, and a price below 1.4x book. The bank's deep specialization in ag credit — confirmed by ICBA recognition — is a genuine competitive moat in markets where money-center underwriters lack expertise. Sticky deposits from multi-generational farm/ranch customers cost less than wholesale funding and rarely flee. The geographic spread, while costly, also diversifies single-county and single-commodity risk. M&A from a larger Pacific Northwest acquirer (Heritage Financial, Washington Federal, Banner) would crystallize the discount-to-fair-value at potentially 1.7–2.0x book in a sale scenario. For a long-duration patient holder, BEOB offers an unusual combination of franchise quality and entry valuation.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
Because BEOB trades on OTC and is not listed on a major exchange, the bank-subsidiary FFIEC Call Report is the most reliable real-time financial source — it discloses the loan portfolio mix (ag operating, ag real estate, commercial, consumer), charge-offs, NPL ratios, deposit composition, and capital ratios. Concentration metrics are critical: any single-borrower or single-commodity concentration above regulatory thresholds is material risk. Dividend policy has historically been modest and stable, supporting a "bond-like" hold. Insider and director ownership tends to be high and locally concentrated, which is positive for alignment but reinforces the float problem. Drought conditions and Pacific Northwest snowpack reports are surprisingly relevant to credit quality given irrigation dependency.
