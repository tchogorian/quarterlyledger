# {NUMBER}. THRY — Thryv Holdings, Inc.
## MELTING ICE CUBE | Score: 43/100

### ANALYST SUMMARY

Thryv Holdings is the post-Chapter-11 successor to Dex Media — yes, the print Yellow Pages — that has spent the last several years deliberately reinventing itself as a small-business SaaS platform. The company now reports two segments: SaaS (its Thryv Platform CRM/marketing/payments suite plus Keap acquired October 2024) and Marketing Services (legacy print and digital, which management explicitly plans to "exit by the end of 2028"). FY2025 revenue was $785.0M, down 4.7% — SaaS grew 34.2% to $461.0M while Marketing Services collapsed 32.6% to $324.0M. Net income was $0.3M (versus a $74.2M loss in 2024), Adjusted EBITDA was $151.8M, and SaaS Adjusted EBITDA reportedly rose to $73.8M from $41.2M. Total stockholders' equity was $218.1M against 43.8M shares outstanding (book ~$4.97), with $688.6M of total assets. At 0.66x book, 329x reported P/E and a $145M market cap, this is a classic melting-ice-cube wager on cash conversion.

The bear case is leverage on a sunsetting cash cow. Total debt sits at $253.5M ($210.9M Term Loan including the related-party tranche plus $25.1M ABL plus current portion), with pension obligations of another $44.2M. The Marketing Services segment is the only thing producing real EBITDA today, and it is contractually scheduled to disappear by end-2028 — every quarter that runs the clock down compresses the cash base used to service the term loan and to fund the SaaS pivot. Goodwill of $253.8M sits against $1.17B of accumulated impairment losses and a $572.1M accumulated deficit, evidence of how value-destructive the prior decade was. Stock-based compensation expense was $25.3M in 2025 (up from $24.1M in 2024) — roughly 17% of market cap annually in dilution, eating any equity value the cash flows might create. The 2024 Keap acquisition was funded by issuing 5.7M shares at $76.8M in net proceeds, further diluting holders.

The bull case is straightforward: pay 0.66x book for a SaaS business that did $461M of revenue at $73.8M of segment EBITDA (16% margin, scaling fast) and get the Marketing Services cash run-off as a debt-paydown engine for free. Management says cash flows from Marketing Services will continue through 2030 even after the 2028 commercial exit, providing four-plus years of de-leveraging visibility. SaaS subscriber base now exceeds 100,000 between Thryv and Keap. 2026 guidance calls for SaaS revenue $461M–$471M and SaaS Adjusted EBITDA $70M–$75M, validating the growth-with-margin trajectory. If the SaaS segment alone is worth even a modest 2x revenue multiple (~$900M+) by 2028, common equity could meaningfully re-rate even after debt paydown.

### WHAT A SCREEN CAN'T SEE
The Adjusted EBITDA bridge tells the real story. To get from $0.3M of GAAP net income to $151.8M of Adjusted EBITDA, management adds back $25.3M of SBC, $28.2M of restructuring and integration expenses, $8.8M of net periodic pension cost, $52.8M of D&A, $34.8M of interest expense, $16.7M of tax, and others — meaning roughly half of "Adjusted EBITDA" relies on add-backs that recur (SBC, pension, restructuring) or on debt service (interest). The 329x P/E is correct as printed because GAAP earnings barely cleared zero, and a $1.16B accumulated impairment line plus a $572M accumulated deficit makes clear that book value here is largely a function of post-bankruptcy fresh-start accounting carried forward — not a fortress NAV. The Marketing Services exit also embeds working-capital release (deferred costs, contract liabilities, unbilled receivables — $152.7M of unbilled AR alone), which will be a one-time tailwind that could fund both deleveraging and SaaS reinvestment over 2026–2028.
